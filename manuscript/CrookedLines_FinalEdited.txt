Deus escreve direito por linhas tortas —
God writes straight with crooked lines


The Meaning Behind the Title
The Portuguese saying “Deus escreve direito por linhas tortas”—translated as “God writes straight with crooked lines”—carries a quiet kind of hope. It suggests that what feels like chaos or failure may, in time, reveal a larger design.
For years, I thought my story was too broken to matter. But every misstep, and detour turned out to be part of the same sentence—God’s handwriting on my life. The crooked lines weren’t mistakes; they were directions.
This is what Crooked Lines means to me: grace inside imperfection, purpose hiding in the mess, and proof that redemption doesn’t need a straight road.

AUTHOR'S NOTE
________________


This book is my fourth step on paper.
For those unfamiliar with Alcoholics Anonymous, the fourth step requires a "searching and fearless moral inventory" of yourself—a comprehensive, honest accounting of your life, your mistakes, your character defects, and the people you've hurt. It's meant to be shared with your higher power, self, and another human being. Today, you are that other human being.
Here's why I’m sharing:
I wrote this book because I needed someone to tell me thirteen years ago that healing and growth were possible. That you could hit bottoms you didn't think you'd survive and still build a life beyond your wildest dreams. That loss and addiction doesn't have to be a death sentence. That trauma can be processed. That broken things can heal.
I needed someone to be vulnerable about the really dark stuff—the childhood violations, the protective orders, the psych wards, the handcuffs, the homicidal ideation, the absolute rock bottoms that no one talks about because they're too ashamed. I needed to know I wasn't uniquely broken, that others had walked this path before me and made it out alive.
So I'm being that person for you.
This book is not a redemption story where I emerged perfect and enlightened. I'm still a work in progress. Still learning. Still making mistakes. Still showing up to meetings and calling my sponsor and mentor and doing the daily work of recovery. The difference is I'm no longer running from myself. I'm no longer hiding behind armor. I'm learning to bring my full, whole self to the table—flaws and all.
I've changed names and identifying details to protect people's privacy, but every story in this book is true. Some of it is difficult to read. Some of it was difficult to write. But recovery taught me that secrets keep us sick, and truth sets us free. So I'm choosing truth.
And before we go any further, let me say this: this is my story. My memories. My perspective. If you were there for parts of it, you might remember things differently, and that's fine. We can both be telling the truth from where we stood. But this book isn't about sorting out everyone's version. It's about telling you mine, as I lived it, as I felt it, as it shaped me. Because perception is reality, and this reality is the one I had to survive.
One note about names: I've changed names and some identifying details to protect people's privacy. Shannon, my wife, has read every word of this book and given her blessing. She is my partner in recovery and in life, and her courage in letting me share our story honestly is one of the many reasons I love her.

If you're struggling with loss, failure, addiction, trauma, mental health issues, or just the overwhelming weight of being human in a complicated world—please know: you are not alone. You are not too far gone. You are not beyond help. One decision can change everything. One phone call. One meeting. One moment of honesty when dishonesty has been your default for years.
It won't be easy. Recovery is the hardest thing I've ever done. But it's also given me everything that matters: my family, my peace, my purpose, my life.
This book is my way of saying: if it's possible for me—someone who lost everything, destroyed everyone, hit bottoms I didn't think existed—it's possible for you too.
You just have to be willing. Willing to be honest. Willing to be open minded to new ideas. Willing to keep showing up even when you can't see the path yet.
The path will reveal itself. I promise.
One day at a time.
— Shaun Critzer

Introduction: Not What You'd Expect
I didn't come from the kind of chaos people imagine when they hear a story like mine. No abusive father, no absent mother. No poverty, no violence in the home, no obvious warning signs that addiction would one day bring me to my knees in a liquor store parking lot, crying over a bottle of Jim Beam I was too terrified to buy.
I grew up in Charlottesville, Virginia, in a middle-class home with parents who loved me. My father started a cabinet business when I was five years old, putting our house up as collateral to get the loan. It was a gamble that paid off. We had family dinners. Summer vacations. A backyard pool where I spent entire afternoons doing cannonballs with neighborhood kids.
From the outside, everything looked normal. Fine. Safe.
And maybe that's the most dangerous lie of all—that addiction only happens to people from broken homes. That it's reserved for the obviously damaged, the visibly traumatized, the statistically disadvantaged.
The truth is simpler and more terrifying: addiction doesn't care about your ZIP code. It doesn't check your family tree or your bank account or whether your parents stayed married. It finds the cracks in your foundation—the wounds you've buried, the pain you've never spoken aloud, the secrets you've carried since childhood. And it promises relief.
For years, I believed I was different. Special. Uniquely broken in a way that set me apart from everyone else. I built layer upon layer of protection out of muscle and discipline, thinking if I could just get strong enough, big enough, impressive enough, I could outrun my own shadow.
I was wrong.
This isn't a story about how I came from nothing. It's about how I became nothing—how I lost everything I loved, everyone who mattered, every version of myself I'd tried so hard to build. How I stood in a parking lot at thirty-four years old, two years sober, and realized I was still looking for God in all the wrong places.
But it's also about what happened next. About the crooked lines that somehow led me home. About grace hiding in ordinary moments. About learning that failure isn't the end of the story—it's just data. Information. Another chance to choose differently.
My journey took me from bodybuilding stages to courtrooms. From winning championships to losing custody of my children. From the hood to rehab. From protective orders to co-parenting at Disney World. From wanting to die to teaching my children how to live.
It wasn't a straight path. Nothing about recovery ever is.
But if you're reading this because you're struggling—with addiction, with trauma, with the weight of secrets you've carried too long—I want you to know something: You're not uniquely broken. You're not beyond hope. And you're not alone.
The liquor store where I almost relapsed? It's still there on Route 29. I drive past it sometimes, and I remember the man I was that day in early 2015—desperate, terrified, convinced one drink would either save me or kill me.
He was right about the second part. But he was wrong about something else.
He thought he was alone. He thought he was the only one who'd ever felt that broken. He thought there was no way out except through the bottom of a bottle.
He didn't know yet that the hardest moment of his sobriety would become the turning point. That choosing the meeting over the bottle that day would change everything. That the crooked lines he'd been walking his entire life were leading him exactly where he needed to go.
He didn't know yet that God writes straight with crooked lines.
But he was about to find out.
This is that story.


PROLOGUE: The Liquor Store Parking Lot


The ABC store sits less than a mile from the Thursday night meeting, its neon sign flickering like a bad conscience in the twilight. I'm parked outside, engine off, hands gripping the steering wheel, keeping me tethered to earth.


Two years sober. Eighteen months of white-knuckling through meetings, forcing myself to say the words, showing up when every cell in my body screamed to run. Eighteen months of people telling me it gets better, that I just need to work the program, that God has a plan.


But I'm not better. I'm worse.


The protective order has been in place for over a year now. I can't go home—the home my parents sold to me and Jennie, the home where my kids sleep, the home I destroyed with my own hands. I'm living in Landon's room at my parents' house like I'm fifteen again, except I'm thirty-four and I've lost everything that ever mattered.


Jennie won't look at me during custody exchanges. My mom has to play mediator, passing messages like we're children, because the court says I can't be within two hundred feet of my own wife. When the kids come to visit, I have to leave my parents' house and drive around the neighborhood, wait at the cul-de-sac like a criminal until Jennie's car disappears down the street.


The meeting starts in twenty minutes. Shannon will be there—the gorgeous woman I've been exchanging pleasantries with for months, the one whose laugh lights up the entire room, the one I think about even though I know I'm in no shape to deserve anyone's attention. She's way out of my league, but she gave me a hug last week after I shared about how hard things were. It was the first time in months anyone had touched me with kindness.


But I can't seem to make my feet move toward the meeting.


Instead, I'm here. At the ABC store. Where I used to come every single day of the week when I was drinking. Where I'd buy my fifths and half-gallons of Jim Beam the way other people buy milk. Where the employees knew my name and probably took bets on how long until I'd kill myself or someone else.


I don't remember deciding to come here. One minute I was driving toward the meeting, the next minute I was sitting in this parking lot, and I couldn't tell you how I got from Point A to Point B. My feet just... brought me here. Without permission. Without conscious thought.


The bodybuilder and alcoholic in me knows what this is. Muscle memory. Autopilot.
The obsession takes you to the places your disease wants you to go. And once you're there, your body knows exactly what to do. No conscious thought required. Just practiced repetition. Just the invisible hand of addiction steering the wheel while muscle memory executes the plan.
Walk through those doors. Find the bourbon aisle. Reach for the Jim Beam. Take it to the register.
I've done this a thousand times before. My body remembers every step.


I turn off the ignition.


My hands are shaking—not from withdrawal this time, but from anticipation. From desire. From the thing that lives in my chest that whispers *just one drink, just one drink, just one drink* like a mantra, like a prayer, like a promise that this time will be different.


I get out of the car.


The parking lot asphalt is cracked, weeds pushing through. My legs feel disconnected from my body as I walk toward the entrance. This is not me making a decision. This is me watching myself make a decision, powerless to stop it, like I'm floating three feet behind my own head.


The automatic doors slide open with a pneumatic hiss.
And I'm inside.
The fluorescent lights are too bright, humming with that particular frequency that feels like it's vibrating inside your skull. The aisles stretch out before me in neat rows—airplane bottles on the left, vodka and other clear liquors straight ahead, and my bourbon aisle on the far right wall.
I can still see the store in my mind 13 years later, still smell it.
My liquor. My poison. My medicine. My god.
I drift toward the bourbon aisle, my stomach churning. Not sure if that's excitement, fear, or death's door calling me home.


There they are. The bottles. Hundreds of them, glass soldiers standing at attention, waiting for me. Jim Beam. Maker's Mark. Wild Turkey. Woodford Reserve. Each one a memory. Each one a blackout. Each one a promise that for just a few hours, I won't have to feel this crushing weight of being alive and awake and aware of everything I've destroyed.


My vision blurs.


I blink, and realize I'm crying.


Standing in the middle of the ABC store on a Thursday evening, tears running down my face, staring at bottles of bourbon like they're old lovers who betrayed me. Or maybe I betrayed them. I can't remember anymore.


An older couple walks past, gives me a wide berth. The woman whispers something to her husband. They speed up their pace toward the wine section.


I don't blame them. If you're ever in a liquor store and you see someone crying while looking at bottles of whiskey, you're witnessing something abnormal. That's not how normal people drink. Normal people don't cry in liquor stores. Normal people don't have relationships with alcohol that end in tears.


Normal people can take it or leave it.


I reach out and touch a bottle of Jim Beam. The glass is cool against my palm, smooth, familiar. I could buy this bottle right now. I have money in my wallet. I'm an adult. I'm allowed. Who's going to stop me?


In my mind, I can already taste it. Feel the burn sliding down my throat, the warmth spreading through my chest, the blessed numbness starting in my fingers and toes and working its way inward until everything—the protective order, the lost kids, the shame, the rage, the fear—all of it just... softens. Blurs. Disappears.


Just for tonight. Just to take the edge off. Just to make it through one more day in this hell I've created.


My hand tightens around the bottle neck.


And then I hear it. Not a voice, exactly. More like a whisper underneath thought. The same whisper that's been trying to get through to me for two years, the one I've been drowning out with anger and resentment and self-pity.


*This won't fix anything.*


I know that. Of course I know that. I've known that since I was thirty-two years old, since the first DUI where I blew a .31 and thought my life was over. I've known it through every psych ward admission, every weekend in jail, every morning waking up and not remembering how I got home. I've known it through watching my best friend Gage fall from a balcony and never walk again. Through Jax dying of a heart attack at twenty-nine. Through holding my newborn son and thinking I was being punished by God for every terrible thing I'd ever done.


I've known it. But knowing hasn't been enough.


My hand releases the bottle.


I step back from the shelf like it's on fire.


And then I'm moving—not toward the register, but toward the exit. Fast. My feet carrying me somewhere different this time, somewhere my disease doesn't want me to go but something else inside me does.


The automatic doors open and I'm gulping night air, stumbling toward my car, hands fumbling for keys. I drive. Not home—I don't have a home. But to the place that's been waiting for me. The place I should have gone first.


---


Seven minutes later, I pull into the parking lot of the Thursday night meeting on Pantops mountain. The same building that houses the IOP program where I first started getting help. I'm ten minutes late, but the door is unlocked.


I can hear them inside, voices raised in unison: *"God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change..."*


I slip into the back row, and I'm still crying. Tears streaming down my face, shoulders shaking, trying to breathe and failing.


The guy next to me—an old-timer with maybe thirty years sober—slides a box of tissues my way without a word. Doesn't ask what's wrong. Doesn't need to. He's probably sat exactly where I'm sitting.


When they ask if anyone wants to share, my hand goes up before my brain can stop it.


"My name is Shaun, and I'm an alcoholic."


"Hi, Shaun," they say back, and it sounds like home.


"I—" My voice cracks. "I just came from the ABC store. I was standing there, holding a bottle, and I... I don't know what happened, but I'm here instead."


The room is silent except for the sound of my breathing.


"I'm two years sober and I feel like I'm dying. I lost my kids, my house, my marriage. I'm living with my parents. I can't go five minutes without thinking about drinking. And I don't understand why people keep saying this gets better because it doesn't feel better. It feels like hell."


More silence. Then the old-timer next to me shared his experience.


"For me, It got better when I stopped fighting it," he says. "When I stopped trying to force it, control it, manage it. When I finally—*finally*—just surrendered and did the work."


After the meeting, Shannon finds me by my car. The woman with the piercing eyes and the laugh that sounds like hope. She doesn't say anything at first. Just wraps me in a hug, and I let myself be held for the first time in what feels like years.


"You okay?" she asks when she pulls back.


"No," I say honestly. "But I'm here. I didn't drink. That's something, right?"


"That's everything," she says.


---


I drive back to my parents' house that night. Back to Landon's room with the wooden bed frame and the smell of my failure hanging in the air. But something is different.


I walked into a liquor store and cried over bourbon bottles like a crazy person. I stood there, an alcoholic in the lion's den, and somehow—*somehow*—I walked out empty-handed.


That was my bottom in sobriety. Not the psych wards. Not the protective order. Not even losing access to my kids. It was standing in that ABC store, crying, finally understanding that no amount of discipline, no amount of white-knuckling, no amount of showing up to meetings would save me if I didn't actually *surrender*.


I didn't know it then, but that night—in early 2015—that was the night I finally put both feet in.


That was the night I chose the meeting instead of the bottle.


That was the night everything started to change.

PART I: FOUNDATIONS & FRACTURES
*The wounds beneath the armor*

Chapter 1: The Oxygen Tent


I remember the rain—JCPenney parking lot, my parents' voices blending with windshield wipers, and words I didn't understand: pneumonia, whooping cough, admission.


I was four years old, and the world was about to shrink to the size of a clear dome.


Martha Jefferson Hospital. The same hospital where I was born. My mom gripped my hand as we walked through automatic doors that hissed open like they were exhaling. Everything smelled like antiseptic and fear. There were other kids crying somewhere down the hall, and the sound made my stomach hurt.


The nurses were nice—too nice, the way adults get when something bad is happening and they're trying to make it seem okay. They led us to a room, and that's when I saw it through the doorway: the oxygen tent.


It looked like something from a science fiction movie. A clear plastic dome that would go over the hospital bed, sealing you inside. I'd seen something like it before—in E.T., when Elliott and E.T. both got sick and the government put them in those plastic tents to study them. In the movie, it meant you were very, very sick. It meant you were separate from everyone else. It meant they didn't know if you'd make it.


"It's going to help you breathe better, honey," Mom said, but her voice was doing that thing where it went up at the end, like she was asking a question instead of making a statement.


I didn't want to get in the tent.


But when you're four, you don't get to choose. The nurses helped me onto the bed, and then the plastic came down around me with a soft whoosh of air. One of the nurses tried to cheer me up, said it would be like having my own little fort. But I knew forts were meant to keep people out.
This felt different. Like something meant to keep me in.
My own homemade prison of plastic and air. I could see out—see my mom's face, see the fluorescent lights, see the door to my room—but everything looked distorted, wavy, like I was underwater.


I couldn't touch anything. Couldn't hug my mom. Couldn't hold my dad's hand. I was the boy in the bubble. Separate. Different. Alone.


The first night was the worst. Mom slept in a chair next to my bed, and I could see her there, just a few feet away, but it might as well have been miles. When I got scared and started to cry, she pressed her hand against the plastic, and I pressed mine on the other side, but we couldn't actually touch. Our hands were separated by millimeters of plastic that felt like an ocean.


That's when my dad showed up with the slippers.


Dad wasn't the emotional one. He never was. His favorite saying was "Go ask your mom," and that's what happened with just about everything—permission for things, questions about girls, conversations about life. He and I had a good relationship, but we didn't talk about the heavier side of life. Not really. He'd come home from work, we'd eat dinner together as a family, but he was a man of few words. Still is.


But that second day in the hospital, Dad brought me two pairs of slippers.


The first pair was Cookie Monster. Big, fuzzy, bright blue, with Cookie Monster's face on the toes. They were soft and silly and exactly the kind of thing a scared four-year-old would love. When you're that age and you're scared, Cookie Monster represents everything good in the world—fun, laughter, innocence, cookies.


The second pair was G.I. Joe. Army green, tough-looking, with little plastic soldiers on the sides. These weren't soft and fuzzy. These were hard, military, serious. When you're four and you put on G.I. Joe slippers, you're not a scared little boy in a hospital. You're a soldier. You're brave. You're strong.


Dad didn't explain why he brought two pairs. He just set them both at the foot of my bed, inside the tent where I could reach them, and said, "Thought you might like these, buddy."


And then he left. Because that's what Dad did—showed his love through actions, not words.


I didn't understand it then, sitting in that oxygen tent with pneumonia and whooping cough threatening to drown my lungs. I didn't understand that my father had just given me a roadmap for survival. A way to navigate being four years old and terrified and separate from everyone else.


But I understand it now.


Those two pairs of slippers were my first lesson in becoming two different people.


Cookie Monster was the fun-loving, sweet, innocent, carefree version of me. The version that laughed and played and didn't take life too seriously. The version that people wanted to be around, that made friends easily, that fit in.


G.I. Joe was the tough, brave face I'd have to put on when things got hard. The armor. The soldier who could endure anything, who didn't cry, who didn't show weakness. The version that could survive.


And from that moment forward, I was always deciding: Which slippers do I wear today? Cookie Monster or G.I. Joe? Which version of myself do I need to be to make it through?


---


I stayed in that tent for three days. Maybe four. Time gets fuzzy when you're that age and that sick. I remember the nurses checking my vitals through arm-holes in the plastic. I remember my mom reading to me, her voice muffled through the barrier. I remember watching TV through the distortion and thinking how strange everything looked.


But mostly I remember the feeling of being separate.


Not just physically separate—though that was real enough, trapped under plastic while the world continued outside my bubble. But something deeper than that. A sense that I was fundamentally different from other people. That there was me, and there was everyone else, and there was this invisible barrier between us that I couldn't quite cross.


My therapist at The Ranch, years later, would have a term for it: dissociation. The beginning of learning to split yourself into different versions, different personalities, different masks you wear to survive. The origin story of the chameleon I'd become.


But at four, I just knew that something had shifted. Before the oxygen tent, I was just a kid. After the oxygen tent, I was a kid who understood isolation. Who understood that sometimes you're on your own, even when people who love you are right there. Who understood that the world isn't always safe, and your body can betray you, and sometimes you have to just endure until it's over.


When they finally lifted the tent and told me I could go home, I remember Mom crying—the good kind of crying, the relief kind. I remember Dad carrying me to the car. I remember clutching those slippers, one pair in each hand, not wanting to leave them behind.


"Which ones do you want to wear?" Mom asked as Dad settled me into the backseat.


I looked down at them. Cookie Monster grinning up at me, all goofy and innocent. G.I. Joe staring forward, ready for battle.


"Can I keep both?" I asked.


"Of course, honey."


And I did. I kept both. Because even at four, some part of me understood: I was going to need them both to make it through this life.


---


The oxygen tent was my first real memory of feeling different. Separate. Like I existed slightly outside of normal reality, watching through plastic while everyone else lived in the real world.


It wouldn't be my last.


But it was the beginning of understanding that I could adapt. That I could be whatever I needed to be in any given moment—fun Shaun, tough Shaun, sweet Shaun, soldier Shaun. All I had to do was figure out which version the situation required, and I could slip into that role as easily as putting on a pair of slippers.


It's a survival skill, really. And when you're four years old and scared and alone under plastic, survival is all that matters.


What I didn't know—what I couldn't have known—is that this skill would save my life in some moments and nearly destroy it in others. That learning to be a chameleon, to shift and adapt and become whoever I needed to be, would help me survive middle school and bodybuilding competitions and the bar scene and addiction and psych wards and a protective order and everything else that was coming.


But it would also mean that I'd spend thirty years not knowing who I actually was underneath all those masks.


Cookie Monster or G.I. Joe?


Turns out, I was neither.


And both.


And learning to integrate them—to find the real Shaun Critzer underneath the slippers—would take a journey through hell and back.


But I'm getting ahead of myself.


For now, I was just a four-year-old boy going home from the hospital, clutching two pairs of slippers, already learning the most important lesson of my childhood:


Figure out who they need you to be. Then be that person.


Even if it kills you.


---


Chapter 2: The Caged Animal In Me
The Cage—that's what they called it. The aquatic center in the basement of U Hall, where Ralph Sampson once played basketball for UVA. Back then it was just a name, but years later, I'd understand what it meant to live like an animal trapped inside one—raging, restless, trying to break free.
I was somewhere between six and eight years old. Old enough to remember, too young to understand.
The swim coach was a family friend. Sort of. The kind of person your parents know casually, wave to at community events, trust because everyone else seems to trust him. He ran the youth swim program at the university pool, and my parents thought it would be good for me—exercise, discipline, learning a skill.
They didn't know what happened in the locker room.
Nobody did.
I need to be careful here. Not because I'm ashamed—though I carried that shame for three decades like a stone in my chest. But because this isn't a story I'm telling for shock value. This isn't about graphic details or explicit descriptions. This is about what happens to a child's mind when trust gets violated in ways that create confusion instead of clarity.
What happened wasn't violent. It wasn't terrifying in the way you might imagine. There were no threats, no pain, no overt force. That's what made it so confusing.
It felt... wrong. But also not wrong. My body responded in ways that made me think maybe this was normal. Maybe this was something that happened to all kids and nobody talked about it. Maybe I was supposed to like it.
The coach never told me to keep secrets. He didn't have to. The silence was built into the act itself—something done privately, in the locker room while other kids were still in the pool, quick and quiet and wrapped in a kind of casual normalcy that made me question whether it was happening at all.
When I finally got to rehab—The Ranch, outside Nashville—my counselors had to convince me it counted as abuse. Had to argue with me about it.
"But he didn't—it wasn't—" I kept trying to explain how it wasn't that bad, how I'd had worse things happen, how other people had real trauma and mine was just... confusion.
"Shaun," my counselor said, looking me dead in the eye. "You were a child. An adult in a position of authority violated your body without your consent for his own gratification. What happened to you was sexual abuse. Real abuse. It doesn't matter that it wasn't violent. It doesn't matter that your body responded. You were six years old. You couldn't consent."
I cried for three hours after that session.
Because hearing it named—hearing someone say definitively that what happened to me was wrong, was abuse, was something I didn't deserve and didn't cause—shattered thirty years of rationalization in an instant.
But I'm getting ahead of myself again. We're still in childhood, still in that basement pool where I learned to swim and learned something else too.
________________


The worst part wasn't the violation itself.
The worst part was that my body reacted.
I was six years old. I didn't understand what was happening. But my nervous system did. And when a child's boundaries are violated in that way, the body doesn't ask permission. It just... responds.
My body reacted. In ways I didn't understand. In ways that created terrible confusion.
My body's response made me wonder if somehow I'd caused it. If I was complicit.
I didn't have words for any of this at six years old. I just knew that something had happened that made me feel powerful and special and terrified and ashamed all at the same time.
And I spent the next thirty years trying to recreate that feeling while simultaneously running from it.
________________


The compulsive seeking started early. Way too early.
I wasn't consciously trying to recreate what happened in the locker room. But my brain had learned something: certain images, certain behaviors created a chemical rush that made me feel powerful instead of powerless. In control instead of helpless.
And once my nervous system learned that pattern, it couldn't unlearn it.
Soon after these incidents, I'd found ways to access material I was way too young for. Material that reminded me of what happened in that locker room. Material that recreated that feeling.
The stealing itself became part of the rush. The fear of getting caught. The thrill of getting away with it. The relief when I made it home.
I'd hide what I'd taken. Study it. Feel that same electrical current I'd felt in the Cage.
I knew it was wrong. I knew I'd be in trouble if caught. But I couldn't stop.
I didn't know the word "compulsion" yet. I didn't know what a dopamine pathway was. I didn't know that trauma rewires young brains, that what happened to me in The Cage had just taught my nervous system its primary survival strategy: seek the chemical relief, no matter the cost.
Fire came next.
I became obsessed with it. Controlling it. My mom's spray deodorant plus a lighter—instant flamethrower. WD-40 cans. Bottlerockets. Firecrackers. Building small fires in the backyard and watching them burn exactly the way I wanted them to.
Once I poured gasoline in the middle of our road, spelling out the word “SHIT” making it easily 20 feet long, and lit each letter on fire. Shit was literally going up in flames. I laughed watching it sear into the pavement. The laughter didn't last long—my dad pulled up right as I was lighting it, then shit really hit the fan.
I was never destructive or violent in the sense of trying to hurt anyone or burn anything important down. But definitely dangerous. Definitely compulsive. I just needed to create something I could control. Light it. Watch it. Master it.
Feel powerful instead of powerless.
And the stealing continued through middle school. Taking things I didn't need, just to feel that rush. Just to prove I could get away with it.
Three different behaviors. One mechanism. One desperate attempt to regulate a nervous system that had been knocked off its axis.
I was six years old. And I was already an addict.
I just didn't know it yet.


________________


I wasn't the only one. There were several other boys that this was happening to. Probably more. I recognized the pattern—the way the coach would keep certain kids back after practice, the way we'd avoid each other's eyes in the locker room, the shared shame we couldn't name.
And all of this was happening while everything else was falling apart too. The oxygen tent. The boiling water burn—something I wouldn’t understand or explain until later. One trauma stacked on another on another, my nervous system getting hammered from every direction that would lead to complete and total destruction in my life, and it would take decades and losing everything dear to me to even begin addressing what was happening.
Years later, at The Ranch, my therapist would tell me she'd never seen a higher score on the PTSD assessment. "Complex trauma," she called it. Multiple traumatic events during critical developmental windows, each one compounding the effects of the others, rewiring my brain in ways that would take decades to untangle.
________________


And like many children who experience this kind of confusion, I tried to make sense of it in ways that weren't appropriate. Ways that adults eventually noticed and gently redirected.
When that happened, I felt a shame so profound I literally couldn't lift my head.
That night, the phone rang. My mom called me into the kitchen. Her voice was gentle. Firm but not angry. She explained that what had been happening wasn't appropriate. That it needed to stop.
I sat with my face tucked between my knees, my whole body folded in on itself, drowning in a shame I had no name for.
I was six years old. And I already knew I was fundamentally broken.
________________


For years I questioned whether my body's response meant something about my identity. About who I was. About what I wanted.
It didn't. But the confusion lasted decades.
It took until my forties to understand: abuse isn't about attraction. It's about power. And a child's physiological response isn't consent—it's biology.
________________


It's the same split that happened in the oxygen tent. The same duality the two pairs of slippers represented—Cookie Monster and G.I. Joe, vulnerable and armored, the real me and the protected me.
Except this time, the split wasn't just metaphorical. It was survival.
When something is happening to your body that your mind can't process, dissociation isn't a choice. It's a gift. Your consciousness steps aside and lets your body handle what it needs to handle, and you watch from a safe distance where the full weight of what's happening can't crush you.
I learned that skill at six or seven years old. Learned to be somewhere else while remaining physically present. Learned to smile and nod while my internal experience was locked away in a box I wouldn't open for decades.
You can't reconcile those two truths when you're six. That someone can harm you and help you at the same time. That the same hands teaching you to float can also violate you.
So you don't. You compartmentalize. You put the bad stuff in one box and the okay stuff in another box and you never, ever let those boxes touch each other because if they did, your whole understanding of reality would collapse.
________________


The coach was kind to me. That was part of the confusion too. He wasn't a monster in the cartoon villain sense. He was friendly, encouraging, patient. He taught me how to swim. Praised my progress. Made me feel seen and valued.
Even now, decades later, part of me still wants to protect him. To say he saw something in me. Made me special. Gave me something other kids didn't have.
I know how that sounds. I know how broken that thinking is.
But that's what abuse does. It tangles your wiring so completely that gratitude and violation become the same thing. You can't separate the attention from the harm. The specialness from the exploitation.
My therapist calls it trauma bonding. I call it the thing I'm still untangling at forty-five years old.
________________


I learned to protect him. Don't know why. He never asked me to. Never threatened me or told me to keep quiet. But I protected him anyway, the same way I'd protect myself years later by lying about karate belts, the BB in my head, and every other tall tale I would tell, by pretending everything was fine when it was all falling apart.
It's a pattern, I think. When something is too big or too confusing to face, you just... don't. You bury it. You minimize it. You tell yourself it didn't happen, or if it did happen, it wasn't that bad, or if it was bad, you probably deserved it somehow.
You make yourself smaller and smaller until you disappear under the kitchen table with your head tucked down and your shame eating you alive.
And then you spend the rest of your life trying to figure out how to be big again. How to take up space. How to be seen without being vulnerable. How to be strong without being broken.
You put on the G.I. Joe slippers and you become the soldier who can't be hurt because you've already been hurt in ways you can't even name.
You build muscles until you're the biggest guy in the room because if you're big enough, strong enough, intimidating enough, maybe nobody will ever touch you like that again.
You drink until you're numb because numbness is better than feeling the confusion of a six-year-old boy trying to understand why his body betrayed him.
You seek out images and experiences and chemical highs that recreate that first dopamine rush because your nervous system is forever chasing what it learned in that locker room.
You become anything—anything—except the small, scared kid who learned in the Cage that the world isn't safe and adults lie and your body isn't always yours to control.
________________


The Cage closed years ago. U Hall itself is gone now too—demolished just a few years back. The night before they tore it down, I took a long walk through Grounds. Stood outside the stadium in the darkness, took one last picture of the building where it all happened.
Maybe it was my way of saying goodbye. Or maybe I just needed to see it destroyed—the bones of the building stripped bare, the structure exposed, crumbling but still standing for one more night before the wrecking ball finished it off.
There was something fitting about that. About seeing the place that held so many of my secrets reduced to rubble. About watching the Cage finally get torn down.
But even with the building gone, I still think about that basement. About the echo of chlorine and children's voices. About the coach who's been dead for years now.
You can demolish the building. But the memory? That stays.
And I think about six-year-old Shaun, walking into that locker room, not knowing what was about to happen, not having the words to explain it when it did.
I wish I could go back and tell him: It's not your fault. You didn't do anything wrong. Your body responding doesn't mean you wanted it. And one day—I promise—you'll tell this story out loud and it won't destroy you.
But I can't go back.
All I can do is keep moving forward, keep doing the work, keep peeling back the layers of protection I built around that little boy in the Cage.
And maybe—just maybe—help someone else who's carrying their own secret, their own shame, their own confusion about what their body did and what it means.
Because here's what I know now that I didn't know then:
A child's physiological response isn't consent. Trauma bonding isn't love. Compulsive seeking isn't desire.
And secrets don't protect you. They poison you.
The only way out is through.


Chapter 3: Fire & Fracture


First grade. Saturday morning. Kitchen table, instant coffee brewing, a picture frame being assembled—and then the scream.


I don't remember who knocked it over. Maybe nobody did. Maybe it was just gravity, just bad luck, just the universe deciding that a six-year-old boy needed another lesson in pain. The thick glass measuring cup full of boiling water, sitting on the edge of the counter, and then suddenly it wasn't.


It poured onto me like liquid fire.


I was in my underwear and a white T-shirt. Nothing else. The cotton absorbed the boiling water instantly, and then the physics of burns took over—the fabric gluing itself to my skin, holding the heat against me, melting into me like we were becoming one thing instead of two.


I remember the sound I made. Not quite a scream. Something animal, something primal, something that came from a part of me I didn't know existed.


The pain was unlike anything I'd ever felt. The oxygen tent had been scary, the Cage had been confusing, but this—this was pure, immediate, undeniable agony. My skin was on fire. My chest, my stomach, everything from just below my belly button up to just below my chest was burning, *burning*, and the T-shirt was melting into me and I couldn't get it off.


Mom was screaming. Dad was there, scooping me up. Someone—I think it was Mom—was running cold water, but moving the shirt hurt worse than leaving it on, and I was just wailing, that animal sound pouring out of me while my parents' faces blurred above me through tears and pain and shock.


---


They carried me to the car wrapped in a towel. Still in my underwear. Still with the melted shirt stuck to my torso. I remember being acutely aware of that—that I was going out in public in just my underwear, that people would see me, that this was somehow embarrassing even though I was in so much pain I could barely breathe.


Even at six, already learning: Hide your vulnerability. Hide your exposure. Don't let them see you like this.


The doctor's office first, not the ER. I don't remember why. Maybe it was closer. Maybe it was Saturday and the ER seemed too drastic. But I remember being carried through the waiting room—people staring, a kid crying in his underwear wrapped in a towel with his parents' faces white with fear—and then into an exam room where the real nightmare began.


The nurse had tweezers.


"We have to get the shirt off, honey," she said gently, but there's no gentle way to peel melted fabric from burned skin.


Every piece they removed took skin with it. Tiny threads of cotton embedded in my flesh, and they had to pick them out one by one while I screamed and Mom held my hand and Dad stood in the corner looking like he might be sick.


The burns were severe. Second-degree in most places, third-degree in others. The doctor's face when he examined me told me everything I needed to know: This was bad. This was going to hurt for a long time. This was going to scar.


They bandaged me up—so much gauze I looked like a mummy. Gave my parents instructions for care, prescriptions for pain medication that would knock me out but not quite kill the fire still burning under my skin. Told them to watch for infection, to change the dressings daily, to bring me back if it got worse.


And then we went home.


---


I missed a week of school. Maybe two. I remember lying in bed, unable to wear a shirt because anything touching the burns felt like torture. I remember Mom changing my bandages every day, her face trying so hard to stay calm while I cried and begged her to stop even though she couldn't stop, the bandages had to be changed or I'd get infected.


I remember the smell—burned skin and antiseptic and gauze.


And I remember my first-grade teacher, Mrs. H, organizing the class to make me get-well cards.


They came in a big stack. Construction paper with crayon drawings of flowers and sunshine and "GET WELL SOON Shaun" in big wobbly letters. Sweet drawings from six-year-olds who didn't really understand what had happened, just knew that their classmate was hurt and might never come back.


One of them—I don't remember who—drew a picture of me in a hospital bed with flames coming off my torso. Accurate, if a bit on the nose.


Mom cried when she brought them to me. The good kind of crying again. The kind that means *look how much people care about you*.


But all I felt was embarrassed.


Embarrassed that everyone at school knew I'd gotten hurt. Embarrassed that they'd seen me weak. Embarrassed that there were now physical scars that I'd have to explain, that people would see, that would mark me as different, as damaged.


I wanted to hide. Wanted to disappear. Wanted to go back to being invisible, unremarkable, just another first-grader instead of *the burn kid*.


Years later—decades later—I'd run into Mrs. H again. In an AA meeting. My first-grade teacher and me, both in recovery, both fighting the same disease.
One day at a noon meeting, she got to introduce me before I shared my story. She told everyone about teaching me cursive writing, about how sweet, shy, and innocent I was. My oh my how time can change things.
"In my defense," she said with a smile, "at that time, I wasn't drinking quite so much."
"Yeah, me neither," I shot back. "Even though we were all in the first grade, I always wondered why you gave us airplane bottles instead of pencils and stickers."
The room erupted in laughter in only the way a dark mind and twisted sense of humor would. Here we were—teacher and student, reunited thirty years later in a church full of lunatics with a pathway out of misery and hell, both trying to stay alive.
That's recovery. You never know who you'll meet there.


---


There's another memory from that year that I can't separate from the burn, even though they're not directly related. It was earlier in the school year, before the accident.


I was sitting in the back right corner of Mrs. H's classroom. I felt sick—genuinely sick, stomach churning, that feeling right before you throw up where your mouth gets watery and you know something bad is about to happen.


I should have raised my hand. Should have said, "Mrs. H, I don't feel good. I need to go to the bathroom." It would have been simple. Easy. The right thing to do.


But I was frozen.


Frozen by some combination of fear and embarrassment and whatever mechanism in my brain was already learning to hide discomfort, hide need, hide vulnerability at all costs.


So I just sat there. Stomach churning. Mouth watering. Panic rising. Unable to move, unable to speak, unable to do the one simple thing that would have prevented what happened next.


I threw up.


Right there in my seat. In front of everyone. In the back right corner of Mrs. H's first-grade classroom.
The room went silent. That awful, crushing silence where everyone stops what they're doing and turns to look. Twenty-five pairs of six-year-old eyes on me, on the mess I'd made, on my face burning with shame.


Mrs. H was kind about it. Called the office, got Oscar the janitor, to help clean up, took me to the bathroom to get cleaned up myself. She didn't make a big deal, didn't scold me, didn't ask why I hadn't raised my hand.


But I knew. I knew I should have asked for help. Knew I should have spoken up. Knew that by trying to hide my need, by trying to tough it out, by trying to be invisible and not bother anyone, I'd made everything so much worse.
The pattern was already there: Don't ask. Don't tell. Don't let them see you're struggling. Endure alone, no matter the cost.


And when that doesn't work? When the pain is too big to hide, when your body betrays you despite your best efforts to keep it all contained?


Feel the shame. Carry it. Add it to the pile of evidence that you're different, that you're damaged, that there's something wrong with you that everyone can see.


The scars from the burn faded over time. Second-degree burns heal if you're young enough and lucky enough. The skin knits itself back together, pink and tight at first, then gradually blending until you can barely see where the damage was.


But I knew. Every time I took off my shirt, I could trace the invisible map of where the boiling water had landed. Could remember the tweezers and the screaming and the get-well cards and the shame of being the burn kid, the one everyone had to make cards for, the one who was marked as hurt, as vulnerable, as different.


The lesson stuck deeper than the scars.


Pain is private. Weakness is something you hide. If you're hurting, you suffer in silence. If you need help, you figure it out yourself. If your body is in danger—whether from boiling water or swim coaches or anything else—you don't make a scene, don't draw attention, don't let them see.


You wrap yourself in a towel. You walk through the waiting room with your head down. You let the nurse peel the shirt off your skin one excruciating piece at a time and you just take it because that's what you do.


You take it.


You always take it.


And years later, when your life is falling apart and your marriage is burning and you're drowning in addiction and you want to scream for help, you don't. Because you learned to believe at six years old that asking for help just makes everyone uncomfortable and puts your pain on display for people to gawk at and make cards about.


So you suffer in silence.


You build muscles to armor the vulnerable places.


You drink to numb the burn that never quite goes away.


You become someone else entirely—someone tough, someone strong, someone who doesn't need anyone—because the alternative is being that kid in his underwear, wrapped in a towel, crying in a doctor's office while everyone stares.


The scars faded.


But the lesson stuck.


Don't let them see you hurt. Don't let them know you need help. Pain is something you carry alone, hidden under your clothes, covered by whatever armor you can build.


And I spent the next thirty years living by that rule—until it almost killed me.


---


Chapter 4: Lies & Bullets


The karate belt lie started small—the way all my best lies did.


Scott was taking karate. Real karate, with a real instructor, at a real dojo where you had to bow and say "Yes, Sensei" and do actual work to earn your belt. He was proud of it, showed me the moves, talked about the classes with this quiet confidence that made me jealous in a way I couldn't quite name.


So I lied.


"I'm taking karate too," I told him one day, casual as anything, like it was just a fact I'd forgotten to mention.


"Oh yeah?" Scott's eyebrows went up. "Where?"


I made up a name. Made up a location. Made up a whole instructor and class schedule, details pouring out of me so easily it scared me a little. I was good at this—too good. The lie built itself, brick by brick, each detail making it more real, more believable.


"What belt are you?" Scott asked.


"Purple stripe," I said without hesitation.


Scott went quiet. Not the good kind of quiet—the skeptical kind. The kind that means *I'm not buying what you're selling*.


"There's no such thing," he said slowly. "Purple stripe belt doesn't exist in karate."


My face got hot. I doubled down, added more details, tried to explain away his doubt with more elaborate fiction. But Scott wasn't stupid, and the more I talked, the less he believed me.


I carried on this ruse for several weeks. All the while knowing I was being dishonest and at the time not even entirely sure why. Eventually, he did what any reasonable person would do: he asked my mom.


I was there when it happened. I just remember trying to interrupt the situation or divert mom’s attention.


“Mrs. Critzer”, Scott said. Can we see Shaun’s Purple stripe Karate belt?


My stomach dropped. My mom said, “What!? Shaun doesn’t take karate”.


The silence between all of us was worse than any punishment I could have been given. Worse than yelling, worse than grounding, worse than anything physical. Because in that silence lived the truth that she saw right through me, that Scott saw right through me, and that I'd been caught looking him dead in the eye and lying.


Not about something that mattered. Not to protect anyone or spare feelings. Just lying to be something I wasn't, to have something I didn't, to be impressive when I felt small.


Eventually that silence was cut with laughter from Scott and our other buddy that was also there. I literally ran up the street with my shame and embarrassment fully in tow.


When I came home that night, Mom didn't make a big deal about it. That was almost worse. She just looked at me with this confusion that said *what was that all about*?


Scott didn't bring it up again. But things were different after that—some invisible line had been crossed, some trust had been broken that couldn't quite be rebuilt. He was still my friend, still came over to play, but there was a distance now. A carefulness. Like he wasn't quite sure which version of me was real.


Looking back, I can see it so clearly: I was practicing. Practicing for a lifetime of becoming whoever I needed to be, of manufacturing identities, of building elaborate fictions because the truth of who I was—the oxygen tent kid, the Cage kid, the burn kid, the small kid—felt too vulnerable, too exposed, too unacceptable to present to the world.


The karate belt lie was just the beginning.


---


The BB gun incident happened the day before freshman year started.


Taylor had this CO2-powered BB gun—a six-shooter pistol that looked like something out of a Western movie. We were at my house, outside in the front yard, and he was showing it off with that teenage boy confidence that believes nothing truly bad can ever really happen.


"It's not loaded," he said, pointing it at my head.


I was wearing my Loyola lacrosse hat—the one I’d gotten from camp that summer. Navy blue with the school logo, pulled low over my eyes the way I always wore hats because I thought it made me look cool.


"Dude," I said, laughing nervously. "Don't point that at me."


"It's fine," Taylor insisted. "There's nothing in it. Look—"


And he pulled the trigger.


The sound was piercing. My ears started ringing and I instantaneously dropped to the ground, going unconscious for a few seconds. The pain was immediate and enormous, radiating out from my left temple like someone had driven a nail into my skull.


I grabbed my head. Felt wetness. Pulled my hand away and saw blood—lots of blood, dark and fast, running down my fingers and dripping onto my shirt.


"Oh shit," Taylor's voice went high and panicked. "Oh shit oh shit we’re screwed!"


His older brother Andrew was going to be a senior that year, he was there to pick up Taylor. He saw the blood, saw the hole in my Loyola hat, saw me starting to sway on my feet as shock kicked in.


"We need to go to the hospital," Andrew said, already moving toward the door.


"No." My voice came out harder than I meant it to. "No hospital."


"Shaun, you're bleeding from your damn head—"


"No. Hospital."


I don't know why I was so adamant. Maybe I didn't want to get Taylor in trouble. Maybe I didn't want to deal with the questions, the paperwork, the explanations. Maybe I was already so practiced at hiding pain that even with a BB lodged in my skull, my first instinct was to cover it up, pretend it wasn't that bad, handle it myself.


Andrew looked at me like I was insane. He wasn't wrong.


We cleaned up the blood. Stopped the bleeding with pressure and ice. The hole in my temple was small—you could barely see it under my hair if I combed it right. And that's what I did. Just... dealt with it. Covered it. In fact, I got a haircut that day. Moved on.


---


I lived with that BB in my skull for over a year.


After a while, it became almost normal. The body does this amazing thing where it tries to protect itself from foreign objects—it builds scar tissue around them, walls them off, tries to incorporate them into the architecture of who you are. That's what happened with the BB. My body built a little capsule around it, turning it into this weird lump under my skin.


I could feel it. Could press on it and feel the hard little sphere rolling around under my scalp. It became a party trick sometimes—"Hey, feel my brain bullet"—and I'd let people touch the side of my head and feel the BB moving beneath the skin.


"What the fuck is that?" they'd ask.


"Got shot at point blank range, freshman year." I'd say. Often intentionally failing to mention the whole, “by a BB” part.


Their eyes would go wide. I'd act like it was no big deal, like carrying a bullet in your brain was just another thing that made me tough, made me interesting, made me someone worth paying attention to.


But it wasn't tough. It was stupid. And dangerous. And a perfect metaphor for the way I'd learned to live: take the hit, bury the pain, walk around with other people's bullets in my head and act like everything's fine.


---


Finally, about 6 months later, I asked to go to the dermatologist to have it checked out. The doctor examined my scalp, and his fingers stopped on that familiar lump.


The doctor that day, misdiagnosed it as a calcified hematoma. I didn’t have the courage to correct him, or say, hey I actually have a piece of metal in my skull would you mind please removing it. 


So I left and went back to school with yet another story and my friends and I who knew it was a BB would awkwardly laugh about my metal calcified hematoma. Another 6 months went by and it definitely was becoming more painful as my body tried to reject the foreign object. So we returned to the dermatologist and I asked to have it removed.


They began the procedure in the office, but when he got in there, he realized it was embedded far too deep to remove in his office and said they would need to immediately send me over to the hospital O.R. for removal.


The surgery was rather quick. Local anesthetic, small incision, and then—*clink*—the BB dropped into a metal tray with a copper bounce that sounded exactly like what it was: foreign object meeting metal, truth meeting light. That piece of metal was lodged in my skull 6.2 millimeters. For context the thickness of the average male skull is 6.5 millimeters.


The doctor held it up with his tweezers, eyebrows raised.


"This is a BB," he said flatly.


"Huh," I said, feigning surprise. "Weird."


He wasn't buying it. Neither was my mom, who was in the room and gave me that look that meant she knew I'd been lying. Again. Still. Always.


Mom kept that BB. Put it in one of her photo albums with pictures from that era of my life—freshman year, the beginning of high school, the start of everything getting complicated. It had rusted and corroded over the course of a year in my skull. I guess that was my body trying to eat away at the metal. Yet another near-death experience that became funny once enough time had passed.


But I think about that BB sometimes. Think about how I walked around for a year with someone else's mistake buried in my skull, how I covered it up and pretended it wasn't there, how I turned pain into a party trick and vulnerability into a performance.


I've been carrying other people's bullets in my head my whole life.


That was just the first one I could actually touch.


---
Chapter 5: Middle School Survival


Elementary school was mostly White kids. Maybe three or four Black kids, one or two Hispanic kids, one or two kids of Asian descent. Everyone else looked like me—suburban White kids from families where both parents worked professional jobs and nobody talked about money or race or anything that might make the adults uncomfortable.


Middle school was different.


Fifty-fifty, maybe even more tilted a bit toward Minority kids than White ones. The demographics shifted dramatically, and with them, the entire social landscape of my world. You either adapted or got swallowed up. I adapted.


First day of sixth grade, in Mrs. Brown’s class, this punk looked at me with his head tilted and said, loud enough for everyone to hear: "Man, you need to put some meat on them bones, faggot."


Everyone laughed. My face got hot. I didn't say anything back—didn't know how, didn't have the armor yet for that kind of direct confrontation. Just sat there, skinny arms crossed over my skinny chest, and took it.


The bus was where I learned my worst habits—and my first business lessons.


Kids teaching each other how to black out—not with drugs or alcohol, with their hands. They'd palm your neck just right, cut off the blood flow to your brain, and you'd go unconscious for a few seconds. Just drop. Come back up dizzy and disoriented while everyone laughed. We thought it was hilarious. Looking back, we were literally teaching each other how to strangle people until they passed out. Twelve-year-olds.


Eraser burns were big too. Hold a pencil eraser against your skin and rub fast and hard until the friction creates a burn that looks like a brand. Kids had whole designs on their arms, wearing their pain tolerance like medals.


But me? I was running a candy cartel. Dad would take me to Sam’s Club, and I’d stock up on Blue Razzberry Blow Pops—the hottest commodity of the day—along with anything else guaranteed to rot your teeth and make you popular.
I sold them on the bus, in the hallways, sometimes right in front of the teachers. Supply and demand was simple: middle schoolers had allowance money and zero impulse control. I was basically the Warren Buffett of sugar.
It went great—until one of my “customers” decided to snitch. I got suspended and banned from the end-of-year pool trip, the one where all the girls would be wearing bikinis. At twelve, that punishment hit harder than prison time.
Meanwhile, the back of the bus was a whole different economy. You could get anything back there—weed, coke, shrooms, steroids, or worse, depending on who you sat behind. I was slinging Blow Pops while other guys were moving controlled substances.
Still, a hustle is a hustle. Looking back, I like to think I just picked the wrong product line.


Back then they had a name for white kids who tried to act Black—an ugly label everyone used too casually. Starter jackets with sports teams, hip-hop blasting from my Discman, learning the slang, the walk, the way to carry myself that said *I belong here, don't test me*. It was pure survival mechanism. Adapt or get swallowed up. I chose to adapt.


The truth is, I was good at it. Good at code-switching, good at reading the room, good at becoming whatever I needed to be to fit in. It was the karate belt lie on steroids—except this time, I was lying about my entire identity, and everyone was buying it.


I was somehow in the popular group growing up. Always, even though I always felt different, even less than in some cases. Got crowned eighth-grade dance king at the formal—later would go on to be voted for several superlatives and things like homecoming court in high school, and everyone cheered, and I felt like I'd accomplished something, like I'd won at the game of being someone people wanted around.


But inside? Inside I was just learning how to be whatever I needed to be to survive. Learning that the real me—whoever that was—wasn't good enough, wasn't tough enough, wasn't cool enough. So I'd bury him deeper, layer more armor over him, become someone else entirely. That was also when I met Ryan.
Ryan was in choir with me—one of the only other dudes who loved music and wasn't considered a total douche for it. We bonded over that, and we were quite the talented duo at such a young age, if I may say so. Still, we were kids who didn't quite fit the tough-guy mold but were trying to figure out how to survive anyway.
One summer, Ryan had pushup, situp and pullup competitions with his dad. When he came back, he'd developed. Like, really developed. This kid who'd been skinny and awkward like me suddenly had muscles—chest, arms, shoulders that filled out his shirts in a way that made the girls absolutely lose their minds.
We went to the pool that summer. I watched those girls—girls who'd never given either of us the time of day—swarm Ryan like he was a celebrity. Touching his arms, giggling, asking him questions, just going bananas over this new version of him that had appeared while we were apart.
And I thought: I want that.
Not the girls, exactly—though that was part of it. But mostly I wanted the attention. The validation. The feeling that my body could be something that made people see me as valuable, as worthy, as someone who mattered.
I didn't know it yet, but that was the whisper. The first quiet voice that would eventually become a roar: If you can't control your life, at least you can control your body.
________________


There was this choir trip to New York my eighth-grade year. We stayed in a hotel, and some of us—me included—decided it would be funny to set off bottle rockets in the stairwell at 2 a.m.
Brilliant plan, obviously.
We also made paper airplanes in Philadelphia out of phone-book pages and launched them off the hotel balcony into traffic.

Because apparently stupidity was our native language.
But the true stroke of genius wasn’t the aerodynamics—it was the material. We weren’t using the boring white pages. No, no, we went straight for the back section—the strip-club ads. We were folding half-naked women and “Two-for-One Lap Dance Tuesdays” into aerodynamic masterpieces, hurling debauchery into the night like some kind of moral clean-up crew on Red Bull.
And when we got caught—and of course we got caught, because paper porn gliders raining from the sky tend to attract attention—our defense was flawless:
“In our defense,” we said with the conviction of future convicts,
“We were just trying to protect the public. If we didn’t rid this room of these escort and hooker ads, some poor soul might’ve fallen victim to temptation. We couldn’t, in good conscience, allow that. We’re not delinquents—we’re heroes.”
The choir director was… not impressed. Apparently she didn’t share our concern for the moral decay of downtown Philly.
The four of us—me, Ryan, Sam, and Brandon—were immediately summoned to the teacher’s office, which felt less like discipline and more like a press conference for idiots. When we finally got released, we insisted on commemorating the moment properly.
We demanded mugshots.
So there we were, all four of us lined up sideways in her office, holding paper airplanes like felony evidence, doing our best “America’s Most Wanted: Choir Edition” poses. The girl who ratted us out stood in the background, arms crossed, looking both mortified and furious—like she’d just realized she was the only sane one in the photo.
To this day, I’m convinced that photo would’ve made a hell of an album cover:
“The Treble Makers – Live from Detention.”
That was middle school and early high school in a nutshell: learning to bend the rules, talk your way out of trouble, and spin stupidity into something almost defensible. Learning that performance and charm could get you further than honesty ever would.

CHAPTER 6: THE CRASH & THE WHISPER OF IRON


Freshman year, basketball tryouts.
I'd been playing my whole life—YMCA, AAU, pickup games, everything. I was decent. Not great, but decent. Quick hands, good instincts, scrappy on defense. The kind of player who could contribute if you gave him minutes.
But when I showed up to tryouts at Albemarle High School, I realized I had a problem.
Everyone was bigger than me. Not just taller—which they were—but thicker, stronger, more physically developed. I was still the skinny kid with no meat on his bones, the one the bully had called out in Mrs. Brown's classroom. And in high school basketball, that matters.
I made it through the first round of cuts. Then the second. By the third day, there were maybe twenty of us left competing for twelve roster spots, and I could feel the math closing in around me.
The coach pulled me aside after practice.
"Critzer," he said, and his tone told me everything before he even opened his mouth. "You've got good playing ability. Nice court vision, decent shot, you work hard. But you're too small. You can't keep up with these big boys physically. I'm sorry."
Cut.
Just like that. One conversation, and suddenly I wasn't a basketball player anymore. Wasn't good enough. Wasn't big enough. Wasn't enough.
________________


I'd grown up watching Michael Jordan. Come Fly With Me, Michael Jordan's Playground—I watched those VHS tapes on repeat. Everyone wanted to be like Mike. And Mike got cut from his high school basketball team too.
But Mike came back bigger, stronger, determined. Made the team the next year. Went on to be the greatest ever.
That's what the coach was offering me: the same path. Hit the gym. Come back. Prove yourself.
But I never came back to basketball. Because I'd found something that actually fit.
________________


I went home and didn't tell anyone at first. Just sat with it, that feeling of failure, that feeling of my body betraying me, of not measuring up.
The shame was enormous and specific: my body—the thing I was supposed to be able to control—had failed me.
Coach was right. I was too small. But the implication underneath felt like a judgment about my entire existence: You're not enough. As you are, you're not enough.
That's when the whisper started. Quiet at first, just a thought, an idea, a possibility:
You could change this. You could make yourself bigger. You could control this one thing even if you can't control anything else.
________________


I started lifting weights in my basement after that cut. A York Barbell set with plastic weights filled with sand. Not seriously at first—just messing around, mimicking training montages from Rocky or feeling the need for speed with Maverick on the Top Gun soundtrack.
But the real obsession—the moment everything clicked—didn't happen until later.
Let me back up to where it actually started.
________________


Age thirteen. Charlie was a buddy from school who'd been to the gym once already, had a personal training session with a guy who'd eventually become my coach over the years. He invited me to come with him, and I said yes without really knowing what I was getting into.
The moment I walked into that gym, I was hooked.
It wasn't just the equipment or the mirrors or even the weights themselves. It was the people. Specifically, the big guys. The bodybuilders. The guys who looked like they'd stepped out of comic books or action movies.
I was mesmerized by them. Gravitated toward them immediately. Would watch them train, study their form, notice what exercises they did, how they moved, what they did between sets. I'd ask them questions until I was blue in the face.
At thirteen, you had to have an adult with you to get into the gym. So sometimes one of my parents would come check me in. But I very quickly got close with the who's who of the gym atmosphere. The big guys started looking out for me, taking me under their wing, letting me train with them even though I was this scrawny kid with no muscle and no idea what I was doing.
I first got a job in the gym’s nursery, childcare where parents could drop off their kids while they worked out. I watched toddlers and changed diapers and wiped noses, all so I could get a free gym membership. Not the ideal career path for a 14 year old boy, but I would have done literally anything to be a part of the gym scene.
Eventually I worked my way up to the front desk at the gym itself, checking people in, answering phones, and spending every free moment asking the bodybuilders questions about training, nutrition, supplements, competitions.
As a kid, I was obsessed with David Copperfield—the magician. His shows, his stagecraft, the way he could twist reality until you doubted your own eyes—it all fascinated me. I loved anything that made the impossible seem real. Illusion. Magic. Transformation. The art of appearing larger than life. Anything that made you different, anything that made people stop and stare. I wanted to be special. Wanted to be impossible. Wanted to be the thing people couldn’t look away from.
And bodybuilding promised exactly that.
________________
By the time I was fifteen, I was living at that gym. Training every day I could. Eating everything I could get my hands on. Reading every magazine I could find. Watching everything I could get my hands on that had anything to do with bodybuilding. That's when Rachel saw something in me.
Rachel was Gage's older sister—though I hadn't met Gage yet. He was still in the Marines, stationed in Okinawa, doing God knows what jarhead shit that would turn him into the gangster I'd eventually call my brother.
But Rachel? Rachel worked at the gym with me. She was graduating high school as I was entering as a freshman. Already part of the bodybuilding world, close with all the big guys, especially this one competitor everyone talked about like he was a superhero: Jax.
Rachel saw something in me. Some spark of obsession she recognized. Maybe she saw her brother in me—that same hunger, that same need to build something out of nothing.


"You should come to a bodybuilding show with us," she said one day at the gym. "In person. Jax's competing in Roanoke."


I'd watched dozens of competitions on VHS. Studied every pose, every routine, every physique. But I'd never actually been to a live show. Never seen it in person. Never felt that energy in real time.


"Yes," I said immediately. "Absolutely."


Because watching it on a TV screen was one thing. But being there? Seeing those physiques under actual stage lights? Feeling the atmosphere?


That was something else entirely.


________________


The show was in Roanoke at the Civic Center. The Jan Tana Classic—one of the largest amateur NPC competitions on the East Coast at the time. This wasn't some small natural show. This was the real deal.
Rachel drove. I sat in the passenger seat, nervous and excited and having no idea what I was about to witness.
The Civic Center was packed. Hundreds of people. The smell I'd later associate with any bodybuilding show I ever attended. It is unmistakable. A product called “Hot Stuff”—the scent reminded me of cinnamon. A vasodilator primarily used to bring out vascularity and help give that obscenely shredded look under the lights. The energy was electric.
I found my seat and waited for the show to start. But before the amateur competition even began, I saw something that changed everything.
The guest posers.
Most big amateur shows hire IFBB pros—actual professional bodybuilders—to come pose between rounds. It's part of the draw. People want to see the freaks, the real deal, the guys who've taken the human body to its absolute limits.
That night at the Jan Tana, there were two guest posers.
First was Michelle Ralabate—a female pro who was popular at the time. She was incredible, but honestly, I barely processed her performance because I was too busy trying to understand what I was seeing in the back of the venue. Turns out, the real show often times wasn’t even on stage. It was often the individuals in the audience themselves that were most jaw dropping. 
Dave Palumbo.
He wasn't competing that night—he'd already won the Junior Nationals, a national-level title. But he was there, and when he walked through the crowd, people stopped and stared. This wasn't someone who just looked good in photos. This was a genetic freak who'd combined brilliant science with ruthless discipline.
Jax saw him too. I watched my mentor—the superhero of our gym—get visibly nervous.
"Fuck," Jax muttered under his breath. "I hope he's not competing."
"Why?" I asked.
"Because look at him. He's a super freak for an amateur. If he's in my class, I'm fucked."
That's when I understood: there were levels to this. Jax was impressive. But Palumbo was something else. He had been to medical school and was well versed in biology and chemistry, knew how to manipulate the human body like a mad scientist. He knew cycles, nutrition, training protocols that most people couldn't even comprehend.
Palumbo wasn't competing that night. But seeing him there—seeing Jax's reaction—showed me that even the best could be outmatched by someone who understood the science better.
And then the main guest poser walked out.
Nasser El Sonbaty.
________________


I'd never seen anything like it.
Nasser was one of the greatest bodybuilders of all time—Born in Germany but of Yugoslovian and Egyptian descent. He spoke seven languages, was nicknamed “The Professor”, and above all was an absolute mass monster. He was one of Dorian Yates's biggest competitions in the mid-to-late 90s, pushed him to the edge at the '97 Mr. Olympia where Nasser took second place to Dorian.
But seeing him in photos didn't prepare me for seeing him in person.
He was easily 300 pounds. Maybe more. In his off-season he'd get up to 320, 325—just a giant. A walking spectacle. When he walked on stage for his guest posing routine, the entire building went silent. Then erupted.
The thing about pro bodybuilders—the thing people don't understand until they see one in person—is that they're somehow bigger in real life than in pictures. It doesn't make sense. Photos actually made them look smaller, more manageable, more human.
But these guys? In person, they were larger than life.
It was like going to the circus all over again. Like seeing something you don't see every day. People want to see the extremes, the outliers, the physical specimens who've pushed their bodies beyond what seems humanly possible. And that's what bodybuilding offered—a legitimate spectacle, a freak show, but one built through discipline and science and sheer force of will.
Nasser was the king of that.
After his posing routine, they let people meet him. Take pictures. I was fifteen years old, standing next to this 300-pound Egyptian giant who looked like he could bench press a car.
Rachel got a picture of us together. I'm grinning like an idiot, absolutely starstruck. Nasser's arm was bigger than my entire torso.
That moment—standing next to Nasser El Sonbaty, shaking his hand, hearing him talk in that deep, unmistakable accented voice about discipline, sacrifice, and what it takes to be great—that was when I knew.
I didn't just want to be big. I wanted to be that. I wanted to walk into a room and have people stop and stare. I wanted to be the spectacle, the attraction, the thing people couldn't look away from.
I wanted to be impossible.
________________


And then the amateur competition started, and Jax walked on stage.
The superhero of our gym. The guy who looked like something out of a comic book. And here he was, under the lights, showing everyone what years of discipline and sacrifice could create.
I sat in those auditorium seats watching men and women in tiny posing suits, their bodies so perfectly developed they didn't look real. The conditioning. The symmetry. The muscle bellies and striations and vascularity—every word was new to me, every concept foreign, but I absorbed it all like a sponge.
These people had taken the raw material of their bodies and sculpted them into art. Into weapons. Into proof that you could control something in this chaotic, painful world.
I was transfixed.
After the show, Rachel introduced me to Jax and some of the other competitors. They were friendly, encouraging, telling me I had potential, that I was young enough to really build something special if I started now and did it right.
"You'd be perfect for teen division in a few years," Jax said. "You serious about this?"
Was I serious?
I was obsessed.
________________


That night changed everything. I'd found my purpose. Found the thing that promised I could control my body, transform myself, become someone worth noticing.
The whisper had become a roar.
And I was ready to do whatever it took.
CHAPTER 7: THE MACHINE


115 pounds, soaking wet. That's what I was when I started. By senior year, I'd be 195 pounds of sculpted discipline—and I'd have done things most people wouldn't believe.
The discipline became extreme, bordering on pathological.
________________


My alarm went off at 6 AM every morning for fasted cardio. I'd walk for miles on the train tracks on my tiptoes—building my calves, keeping my heart rate in the fat-burning zone, grinding out the discipline in the pre-dawn silence when normal teenagers were still asleep.
I set another alarm for midnight. Every night. To wake up and drink a protein shake, maintaining what I'd learned was called "positive nitrogen balance"—keeping amino acids in my bloodstream every two to three hours so my body never went catabolic, never started breaking down muscle for fuel.
Six to eight meals a day. Chicken breast and rice. Oatmeal and egg whites. Sweet potatoes and tuna. My coach would say things like, we don't eat for taste. We eat for fuel. Every meal weighed, every macro counted, every bite in service of the goal.
Senior year, choir trip to Myrtle Beach. Everyone else was packing clothes and snacks and whatever seventeen-year-olds pack for trips. I brought a cooler full of bodybuilding food—cans of tuna I’d crack open on the Greyhound bus, filling the entire vehicle with that unmistakable smell of fish and commitment.
People complained. I didn’t care. Nothing was going to stand in my way.
I was still trying to be a good kid, someone who did things the right way—but within those boundaries, I was relentless.
________________


I subscribed to every bodybuilding magazine I could find: Flex, Muscle & Fitness, Muscular Development, MuscleMag International, Iron Man, and Muscle Media 2000. Over the next decade and a half, I’d collect five to six hundred issues. I studied them like textbooks—training splits, nutrition protocols, posing routines.
I watched every single Mr. Olympia and The Arnold Classic competitions live from 1994 through 2013. Was even streaming through dial up in the early days of piss poor wi-fi connections.
I never missed the Mr. Olympia broadcasts and bought every VHS and eventually DVD release afterward. Watched Dorian Yates grind his way to victory in ’96 and ’97, then Ronnie Coleman explode onto the scene in ’98 with mass and power that redefined the sport. I memorized the way they moved, the confidence in their posing, the unshakable command of presence.
And when I wasn’t watching Olympias, I was glued to ESPN. There were two shows I never missed—Flex Magazine Workout, hosted by Shawn Ray and Boyer Coe, and American Muscle Magazine. They were my classrooms. Every rep, every set, every word from those pros felt sacred.
I didn’t just want to look like them. I wanted to be them—to embody that same electricity, that sense of command when they walked into a room.
At fifteen, I even called a plastic surgery clinic in Florida about calf implants. I hated that my calves wouldn’t grow fast enough. I was willing to do anything—anything—to get bigger.
The bodybuilding community saw something in me—probably that I didn’t have what I call temporary enthusiasms. Most people chase New Year’s resolutions, little bursts of motivation that fade when things get hard. I had something different.
Discipline and consistency. Bordering on insanity. The ability to show up when it wasn’t convenient, when it wasn’t fun, when I was still sore from yesterday’s workout and couldn’t stomach another chicken breast—but did it anyway because that’s what the plan required.
Motivation is fickle; it comes and goes. Discipline doesn’t ask how you feel. Systems don’t care about moods. They just demand obedience. And when I followed them, they worked.
I became a well-oiled machine.
Not in the casual sense—literally. I turned myself into a system of inputs and outputs, cause and effect, stimulus and response. Lift this weight, eat these foods, sleep these hours, and the body will grow. It was mathematical. Predictable. Controllable.
And God, I needed something I could control.


________________


My bodyframe was ectomorphic—what bodybuilders call a "hard gainer." The kind of body that fights you every step of the way, that burns calories faster than you can eat them, that seems designed to stay thin no matter what you throw at it.
I didn't care. I was going to change it anyway.
And it worked. My body responded like it had been waiting for this permission. Like all those years of being too small, too skinny, too weak had just been storing up potential energy, and now I'd finally given it permission to explode.
Freshman year: 115 pounds.
Sophomore year: 145 pounds.
Junior year: 170 pounds.
Senior year: 195 pounds.
Eighty pounds of muscle in four years. On a natural, drug-free frame. Just chicken breast and protein powder and insanely intense training regimens.
________________


I was anti-drugs at that point. Anti-alcohol, anti-steroids, anti-everything that might interfere with my training or compromise the purity of what I was doing.
I wanted to prove that willpower alone could build greatness—that you didn’t need chemicals to earn it.
Looking back, that's beyond hilarious and ironic. Because I'd go on to do nearly every other illegal, black-market drug under the sun once I started drinking and using. But in high school? I was the clean kid. The disciplined one. The one who did everything right.
________________


The bodybuilders at the gym took me under their wing. Taught me everything they knew—training splits, nutrition timing, posing practice, stage presence. Jax especially. I also had a solid crew of others who became some of the best male role models I could have ever asked for, and they saw something in me worth investing in.
They saw the intensity. The willingness to do whatever it took. The obsession that matched their own.
Posing would become my secret weapon. While other guys just flexed through the mandatories, I turned it into performance art—studying the greats like Kai Greene, Shawn Ray, and Melvin Anthony, choreographing routines to music I'd sometimes written myself. The performer in me finally had an outlet.
________________


Here's the complicated truth about bodybuilding:
It saved me. And it nearly destroyed me.
It gave me discipline I wouldn't have learned anywhere else. Taught me that hard work produces results. That you can transform yourself through consistency and dedication. That your body is capable of more than you think.
It built my self-esteem when I had none. Made me feel powerful when I'd spent years feeling small. Gave me a community of people who saw my intensity and respected it instead of mocking it.
I'm grateful for those lessons. Grateful for the countless hours with the boys from the gym and all the others who taught me what it meant to push past limits. Grateful for learning how to set goals and achieve them. Grateful for understanding that discipline matters.
Those skills would save my life later—in recovery, in relationships, in learning how to be a father to a special needs son who needed me to show up every single day no matter how hard it got.
But.
There's always a but.
________________


Years later, I'd take my daughter Cameron to a science museum in Richmond. In the lobby was this massive pendulum representing time—swinging back and forth, back and forth, never stopping. Always in motion.
It reminded me of my personality my entire life and especially in active addiction.
One end of the pendulum: I was nothing. Not enough. Small, weak, worthless.
Other end: I was the man. Elite champion. The guy everyone wanted to be and be with.
Back and forth. Back and forth. Never in the middle.
There’s a book I love that talks about this: Always we tried to struggle to the top of the heap, or to hide underneath it. This self-centered behavior blocked a partnership relation with any one of those about us. Of true brotherhood we had small comprehension.
What I really wanted—what maybe God wanted for me—was to be in the middle. To be steady. To be at peace.
But balance? Moderation? I didn't know how to do any of that. They weren’t in my vocabulary and may as well have been a foreign language. So instead,I swung. Hard. From extreme to extreme.
And I wouldn't learn how to stop swinging until I'd lost everything.
________________


I wasn't just building a body. I was building armor.
Every pound of muscle was another layer of protection between me and the world. Every inch on my chest was proof that I couldn't be hurt, couldn't be touched, couldn't be the small vulnerable kid in the oxygen tent or the Cage or the doctor's office with burns melting his skin.
I thought if I got big enough, strong enough, intimidating enough, I'd finally be safe. Finally be invulnerable. Finally be someone nobody could hurt.
But what they didn't see—what I didn't see—was that I was using bodybuilding the same way I'd later use alcohol and drugs. As a way to avoid feeling. As a way to control something in a life that felt uncontrollable. As a way to become someone impressive enough that maybe I'd finally feel safe.
That's the addiction brain, right? We find something that works—that makes us feel better, look better, seem better—and we use it to avoid doing the real work of healing.
Bodybuilding wasn't bad. The discipline wasn't bad. The achievements weren't bad.
But using them as a substitute for self-acceptance? That was the problem. Because no amount of muscle can protect you from yourself. No amount of discipline can control the chaos inside. And no amount of physical transformation can heal the wounds that live in places barbells and protein shakes can't reach.
I was still the same scared little boy underneath, just carrying more weight on my shoulders.
The machine I'd built—the system of discipline and control and relentless self-improvement—was about to be tested in ways I couldn't imagine. Because machines don't feel. And I was about to discover that I couldn't outrun my feelings forever.
No matter how fast I ran on those train tracks. No matter how heavy I lifted. No matter how much muscle I built or how disciplined I became.
Eventually, you have to stop running.
Eventually, you have to feel.
And when I finally stopped—when the armor finally cracked and all those feelings came flooding in—I reached desperately with the fervor of a drowning man seeking a life preserver, for anything that could numb them faster and more completely than anything I'd ever tried.
But that comes later.
For now, I was still the machine. Still the disciplined one. Still the kid who did everything right. Still building the armor, one rep at a time, still convinced that if I just got big enough, strong enough, impressive enough, I'd finally be enough.
I'd spend the next fifteen years learning how wrong I was.


CHAPTER 8: MEETING JENNIE


Senior year started, fall 1997. I was six months away from my first bodybuilding competition. Training six days a week. Eating six to eight meals a day. My entire world had narrowed to calories, reps, and control.


Until the day control went out the window.


I'd known Jennie since freshman year. We had maybe one class together over four years of high school—just enough to know each other's names, to nod in the hallway, to exist in each other's peripheral vision without ever really connecting.


She was quiet. Sweet. Small-town girl with a softness about her that stood out in the chaos of high school. Sophomore year, she dated one of my good friends, and I remember thinking she seemed like the kind of person who genuinely cared about people—rare in a place where most of us were just trying to survive.


But we'd never talked. Not really. She was just Jennie. Someone I knew, but didn't know.


The auditorium. Senior year. They were handing back senior pictures, and my buddies were looking at Jennie's photos, and started passing them around.
Piercing baby-blue eyes. That smile—genuine, warm, a little shy. That all-American, girl-next-door beauty that wasn't trying too hard, wasn't performing. Just... real.
And I had this thought, clear as day: I'm going to marry her.


Was it love at first sight? I don't know. Seventeen-year-olds don't always know the difference between attraction, chemistry, and destiny. But something about seeing her in that moment—really seeing her for the first time—stopped me cold.


Made me want to know her. Made me think: This one matters.


I handed the photos back to my buddies and started planning how I was going to ask her out.


I caught up with her after class a few days later. Nervous as hell, which was unusual for me—I'd gotten good at performing confidence even when I didn't feel it. But with Jennie, I felt... exposed. Like she could see through the armor I'd been building.


"Hey," I said. "You going to homecoming?"


She smiled. "I don't know. Are you asking?"


"Yeah," I said. "I'm asking."


"Then yes," she said. "I'm going."


And just like that, everything shifted.


We started spending time together. And I discovered something I hadn't expected: I liked her. Really liked her.


Not just because she was beautiful—though she was. Not just because having a girlfriend like Jennie made me feel like I was finally getting life right—though it did.


But because she was funny. Had this dry sense of humor that caught you off guard. Would say something quietly devastating and then look at you with those blue eyes like she was waiting to see if you'd catch it.


She listened when I talked. Actually listened, not just waiting for her turn to speak. When I'd go off about bodybuilding—the training splits, the meal timing, the science of it all—she didn't just tolerate it. She asked questions. Wanted to understand why it mattered so much to me.


And she had this way of making me feel... settled. Like when I was with her, I didn't have to be performing for anyone. Could just exist without all the noise and pressure and constant need to prove myself.


I didn't have words for it then. But what I felt was peace. For maybe the first time in my life, I felt like I could breathe.


Homecoming. October 19, 1997—the night before was the football game, October 18th. That date would matter later: exactly thirteen years to the day, our daughter Cameron would be born. But I didn't know that yet.


All I knew was that it rained. Poured, actually. One of those fall Virginia storms where the sky just opens up and doesn't stop.


We left the football game together. Drove around in my car with the heater blasting and the rain hammering the windshield, talking about everything and nothing. Music. Family. Dreams. The kind of conversation where time disappears and you look up and realize hours have passed.


I drove her to my house. Did something I'd never done before: woke my parents up at whatever ungodly hour it was just to introduce them to this girl. My mom had walked me out onto the field at halftime for homecoming court, but she still hadn't met who would be my date the next day.


Jennie stood in the hallway outside my parents' bedroom with her hair all wet from the rain and her clothes damp, and my parents looked at me like I'd lost my mind. But I didn't care.


"Mom, Dad—this is Jennie."


Mom got up, gave her a hug. "It's so nice to meet you, sweetheart."


Dad just rolled over and went back to snoring.


Jennie smiled that smile, those blue eyes shining even in the middle of the night with wet hair and ruined makeup.


And I was gone. Completely, utterly gone.


She was from a small town, 30 minutes away. It was a quiet kind of place where everyone knows everyone. She was from a good family. Good people. Solid. 


Jennie had this quality about her—meek, maybe, but in a good way. She wasn't trying to control anything or anyone. Wasn't playing games. Wasn't performing some version of herself to impress people.


She was just... Jennie. Steady. Kind. The kind of person who made you feel safe just by being around her.


And she looked up to me in a way I'd never experienced before. Not like parents, who generally love us just because we share a last name. Not like my friends, who respected the discipline but didn't really understand the why.


Jennie saw me—really saw me—and seemed to think I was someone worth admiring. Someone special. Someone who could do great things.


She put me on a pedestal. And I loved it. Loved being seen that way. Loved feeling like I was impressive enough, disciplined enough, good enough to be someone's hero.


I didn't know yet that pedestals are dangerous places to stand. That the higher you go, the farther you have to fall.


But in the fall of 1997, all I knew was that this girl made me feel like maybe I wasn't just the oxygen tent kid or the scared kid from the cage or the kid who was too small and got cut from the team.


Maybe I was someone worth loving.


Over the next six months, Jennie became part of everything. She'd come watch me train sometimes, sitting in the gym doing homework while I went through my splits. She'd pack extra food for me when we'd hang out, making sure I stayed on my meal schedule even when it was inconvenient.


When I had to leave parties early for early morning cardio, she never complained. Never made me feel bad about choosing bodybuilding over normal teenage boyfriend things. She just kissed me goodnight and told me to call her when I got home.


"You're going to do great," she'd say. "I know you are."


And I believed her. Because Jennie didn't say things she didn't mean.


She saw potential in me—some version of myself I was trying to become—and her belief in it was absolute. That kind of faith in someone can be intoxicating. It made me feel capable of anything, like maybe the armor I was building wasn't just protection—maybe it was proof I could be someone worth admiring.


I loved her for that quiet steadiness, for the way she grounded me when everything else felt chaotic.


Senior year rolled on. I kept training. Kept eating my Tupperware meals while everyone else ate pizza. Kept waking at midnight for protein shakes and 6 AM for cardio. Kept building toward this thing I'd been chasing since freshman year when the basketball coach told me I was cut, and increased my belief of the fact that I wasn’t enough. That started the spark which later turned into an inferno of desire to disprove my haters and anyone who ever told me, I couldn’t.


And through it all, Jennie was there. Supporting me even when it didn't make sense to anyone else.


She didn't really understand bodybuilding yet. Didn't need to. She understood the version of me I was willing to show her—the disciplined one, the driven one, the guy who had his life together and knew where he was going.


She didn't see the scared kid underneath. The one who was using bodybuilding as armor, as control, as proof that maybe I was worth something.


But that's the thing about falling in love at seventeen. You don't show the broken parts. You show the best parts. The parts you think will make someone stay.


And Jennie stayed.


My first competition was coming in April. The AAU Natural Virginia State Championships. Everything I'd been working toward since the basketball cut, since the oxygen tent, since every moment I'd felt small and weak and not-enough.


This was my chance to prove I was something more.


And Jennie would be there. Front row. Cheering.


I didn't know yet that external validation—even from someone you love—can never fill the holes inside you. That love built on performance instead of vulnerability was fragile. That pretending to have it all together was just another form of armor.


But I was seventeen. In love. Building toward something that felt like destiny.


And for a while at least, that was enough.


CHAPTER 9: VIRGINIA STATE CHAMPIONSHIP
April 18, 1998. Spring of my senior year. Bethel High School in Hampton, Virginia—the same school Allen Iverson went to. The AAU Natural Virginia State Championships.
My first competition ever. And I was about to win big.
________________


The venue was packed—friends, family, other competitors and their supporters, judges, photographers, the whole bodybuilding community. I could hear my parents somewhere in the crowd with their VHS camcorder running, capturing every moment for posterity.
Jennie was there as well as some of my best friends from childhood. Watching me do the thing I'd been obsessing over since my freshman year. This was what all those meals, all those early mornings, all those nights cutting dates short or not hanging out with my boys, had been building toward.
Backstage, the energy was electric and terrifying. Guys pumping up with resistance bands, slathering on that bronze competition tan that makes you look like a different species under the stage lights, practicing poses in the mirror with this intense focus that blocks out everything else.
I stood there in my posing trunks—basically a Speedo, barely covering anything, leaving your entire physique on display for judgment. Every flaw visible. Every strength and weakness laid bare under fluorescent lights.
This was the moment. Everything I'd sacrificed—every  6AM cardio session, every midnight protein shake, every time I'd brought Tupperware chicken and rice to a party while my friends ate pizza—it had all been building to this.
The women’s classes were called out first. Then the Teen classes followed by the men’s open classes. I walked out onto that stage, hit my poses, and felt time slow down in that weird way it does when you're doing something that matters more than anything else in your life.
Mandatories: front lat spread, front double bicep, side chest, side tricep, rear lat spread, rear double bicep, abdominals and thigh, and finally, the crowd pleaser, the crab most muscular. Each pose held, each muscle flexed, each breath controlled.
Then the comparison rounds—standing next to the other competitors, hitting the same poses simultaneously so the judges could evaluate us side by side.
That was prejudging, held in the morning. And then we waited for the evening finals.
________________


The night show is where I always shined. The finals are where you get to perform your prepared choreographed routine, to the music of your choice, center stage. All my life I’d been a performer. This time, there was no hiding anything. Everything was on display. No armor to shield me.
And then: "First place overall, teen division, Shaun Critzer!"
The applause was enormous. I could hear my parents in the crowd—saw them tearing up, crying with pride as they filmed me walking across the stage to accept my trophy. The youngest teen to ever win the overall at the Virginia State Championship in the sports history. 
I felt so proud. Not for myself—well, yes, for myself, but mostly because I'd just disproved that nagging negative voice in my head. Ever taunting me with, “You’ll never win, you’re just not good enough, why don’t you just give up”
But suddenly. That voice had quieted, and I said to myself, “Look what we just accomplished! We did it.” 
That's all I'd ever wanted: to be enough. To be impressive. To be worthy of pride.
And for a few hours that April afternoon, I was.
________________


But here's what nobody tells you about external validation: it has a shelf life.
The high from winning lasted maybe a few days. A week at most.
And then I was back in my regular body, living my regular life, and the trophy was just a piece of plastic on a shelf and the title was just words on a certificate and I was still the same person underneath—still carrying the same wounds, the same fears, the same fundamental belief that I wasn't enough.
The applause felt good. The trophy looked good. The respect and attention felt amazing compared to feeling invisible and small and not-enough.
But the moment the show was over, the moment the lights went down and I peeled off that bronze tan in the shower and put on regular clothes and drove home—when I look back on it, I can see that the emptiness was still there.
I was still me underneath the muscles. Still the oxygen tent kid. Still the Cage kid. Still carrying all those secrets and wounds and the fundamental belief that who I really was wasn't acceptable, wasn't enough, needed to be covered up and transformed and perfected.
The trophy didn't fix that. The title didn't heal that. The muscles just made it easier to hide.
So I did what any good addict does when they find something that works: I chased more.
________________


Senior year continued. I was still the guy bringing Tupperware to school while everyone else ate pizza. The guy wearing tight shirts to show off the physique even though it made me stand out. The guy who shaved his entire body for competitions—definitely the only guy doing that in my area in the late-90s..
People clowned me for it at first. Made jokes. Called me weird.
Until I started winning.
Then suddenly, every coach wanted me for their team. Wrestling. Football. Track. They all came calling, offering spots, promising playing time.
But I never looked back. Never tried out for another team sport after that basketball cut freshman year.
Because the appeal of bodybuilding wasn't just the physique—it was the nature of the sport itself. Individual competition. If you win, it's because of you. If you lose, it's because of you. Nobody else to blame. Nobody else to rely on. Just you and your discipline and your willingness to do what other people won't.
I never wanted to depend on anyone else again.
Because dependence meant vulnerability. And I'd had enough vulnerability to last a lifetime.
________________


The skinny ninth-grader they'd ignored was now the senior everyone wanted around.
The kid who got cut from basketball was now a state champion.
The one who'd felt small and insignificant was now being congratulated in the hallways.
But the silence inside me just got louder.
Because validation from others can never substitute for accepting yourself. And I didn't accept myself. Didn't even know who my self was underneath all the armor.
I just knew how to build more. Add more layers. Get bigger, stronger, more impressive, more worthy of attention and respect and love.
And if that didn't work? Well, I'd just have to try harder.
That was always the answer: try harder. Be more. Do better.
Never stopping to ask: What if trying harder isn't the solution? What if the problem is that I'm trying to fix something that isn't broken?
But those questions were dangerous. Those questions meant feeling. And feeling meant pain.
So I just kept lifting. Kept eating. Kept grinding.
Kept building the armor.
________________


Graduation came. June 1998. On senior night I was given special recognition and a plaque in front of all of my peers and our families for having become, the 1998 Mr. Teenage Virginia, in our school auditorium. I walked across that stage too—cap and gown instead of posing trunks, but still performing, still proving something.


The week of my high school graduation, my mom's brother died of cancer. He was young. Way too young.
And then, in the middle of grief, my mom and my uncle's wife got into some kind of disagreement. I don't even remember what it was about. Doesn't matter. What mattered was my mom was devastated. Crying. The kind of crying that comes from loss layered on top of conflict layered on top of helplessness.
I couldn't handle it. Couldn't handle seeing her like that. Couldn't handle the weight of the grief pressing down around me. It was too painful.
So I went to the gym.
Thought maybe I could work it off. Lift until the feelings went away. Until I was numb enough to go home and pretend I was okay.
But I couldn't even get to the weights.
I snuck into the back, slipping through the old nursery I used to work in. It was closed. Dark. Empty.
And I sat on the floor in that darkness and cried.
Not the polite kind of crying. The ugly, gasping, whole-body kind. The kind where you're not even sure what you're crying about anymore because everything's tangled together—your uncle, your mom's pain, your own inability to fix any of it, the weight of trying to be strong when you don't feel strong at all.
I don't know how long I sat there. Long enough that I knew I couldn't stay. Long enough to know I couldn't go home either.
So I got in my car and drove.
Thirty minutes to Jennie's house. Her parents' house. She still lived at home, still finishing high school with me.
She knew what was going on. Knew about my uncle. Knew I was struggling.
When I pulled up, she must have seen something in my face. Because she didn't ask me to come inside and sit in her living room to make small talk with her parents while my eyes were red and my chest was tight and I was barely holding it together.
She just saved me.
Met me at my car. Took my hand. Started walking.
We walked for a long time. Didn't talk much at first. She just held my hand and let me exist next to her without having to perform okay-ness.
And somewhere during that walk, I told her.
About The Cage. About the swim coach. About being six years old and not understanding what was happening. About my body responding. About the confusion I'd carried ever since.
I don't know why I chose that moment. Maybe it was the grief. Maybe it was sitting in that dark nursery feeling like I was six years old again—small and scared and alone. Maybe it was Jennie's hand in mine, the way she didn't ask me to be anything other than what I was.
I told her. First person I'd ever told. Seventeen years old, and I finally broke the silence.
She didn't freak out. Didn't pull her hand away. Didn't look at me differently.
She just squeezed my hand a little tighter and kept walking.
Asked if I was okay. Asked if I'd ever talked to anyone about it. Asked if I wanted to.
"I'm okay," I said. "It was a long time ago."
I wasn't okay. But I didn't know that yet.
________________


And then: Beach Week.
My friends and I rented a beach house on the Outer Banks in North Carolina. One of my buddies' moms bravely cosigned for us. I brought a blender for the sole purpose of protein shakes whereas everyone else thought it was for mixed drinks and frozen margaritas.
I didn't touch them. Still the clean kid. Still anti-alcohol. Still convinced that discipline in bodybuilding meant discipline in everything. I would get up at 6 AM to head to the gym to train as my friends were just going to sleep. I'd find them passed out everywhere around the house. Couches. The floor. I was literally stepping over bodies like navigating through a drunken landmine field.
They thought I was weird. I thought they were ding dongs.
I watched them get drunk, get sloppy, make fools of themselves. Watched them have fun in a way I didn't know how to have. Watched them be normal teenagers while I sat there with my Tupperware and my protein shakes and my rigid control over everything I put in my body.
I was so sure I was doing it right. So convinced that my way was the superior way.
I had no idea that within a year or so, I'd be doing the exact same thing they were doing. Except I'd be doing it harder, faster, more destructively than any of them.
Because that's what I did. Extremes. All or nothing. Top of the heap or bottom of the barrel.
Never just a friend among friends. Always trying to be the man. The white knight. The hero.
But I didn't know that yet. Beach Week 1998, I was still the disciplined one. Still the guy who seemed he had it all together.
Still building toward something I thought would finally make me feel complete.
________________


Three months after graduation, my grandfather—my mom's dad—died suddenly. Massive heart attack. Gone.
Another death. More grief. More family devastation.
The hits just kept coming.
And I just kept trying to be strong. Kept lifting. Kept building muscle. Kept constructing armor around the scared kid who'd told his secret on a walk and then immediately tried to bury it again.
But Jennie knew. Even when I pretended I was fine, even when I went back to performing strength, even when I acted like I'd never said it out loud—she knew.
And for years, she was the only one who did.


CHAPTER 10: GOLDEN CHILD
________________


August 1998. Virginia Beach Pavilion. AAU Teenage Mr. America.
The same title Lee Haney won in 1979 before going on to win Mr. Olympia eight times, becoming one of the greatest bodybuilders in history. The same organization that produced Steve Reeves—the 1947 Mr. America who became Hercules on screen, the golden standard of what a physique could be. And of course, my idol, Arnold, had won the equivalent teenage title in Europe at around the same age.
And now, at seventeen years old, I was competing in that lineage.
The book about the competition's history would call it "The Tragic History of a Bodybuilding Icon"—and I'd be listed on page 373, immortalized alongside these legends I'd idolized all the way to the top of the podium. Champions who proved you could transform your body into something extraordinary.
What the book wouldn't say—what I didn't know yet—was that no amount of muscle could protect you from yourself. That the real tragedy wasn't the sport. It was what we used the sport to avoid.
But that was years away.
Right now, I was seventeen years old, 195 pounds of sculpted discipline, and I was competing for Olympic gold.
________________


The level of competition was different here. These weren’t just Virginia kids — these were the top teenagers from every state. To qualify, you had to place top three at your state show. That meant champions from every region, every coast, all in one building. The strongest, the most disciplined, the most obsessed kids in America.
I knew exactly who I'd be going up against. I’d seen the winners from the year before on the internet — that brand-new, slow-as-molasses dial-up world we were all just figuring out. Even printed and put pictures of those winners on my wall in my bedroom at home for motivation. My biggest threat shared the same birthday as me—he was much bigger than I was.
But I wasn't intimidated. If anything, I was more focused. More dialed in. More convinced that this was my destiny, my purpose, the thing I was born to do.
My whole crew from the gym came, ten to fifteen deep. Friends from high school, my girlfriend, aunts, uncles, and cousins. Everyone sitting together with my parents in the middle. My whole support system watching me step into something bigger than anything I'd done before.
Teen tall class. Mandatory poses. Comparisons. But this time, the bar was higher. Every guy on that stage was impressive. Every physique worth attention.
When they called my number for first place in the class, I felt the first surge of victory. But I knew better than to celebrate yet. The overall still waited.
Up against a kid from Baltimore named Mario. Great dude. Incredible muscle, definitely bigger than me, though shorter. But I had a level of definition that bordered on surreal.
And then it happened.
Fred Yale—the legendary MC and promoter—stepped up to the mic. He hadn't said this for any other class or division all night. He scanned the crowd, paused just long enough to let the tension thicken, smirked… and then threw gasoline on the fire.
"Any idea who you think the overall winner might be!?" he shouted.
The entire Pavilion detonated.
I could hear the roar from backstage—the whole auditorium screaming my name. It was surreal. Almost overwhelming. And somewhere in the middle of that tidal wave of sound, I felt a flicker of empathy for the other guy. Not because I didn't want to win. But because the crowd wasn't just loud—it was unanimous.
"Your overall winner, Mr. Teen USA 1998:" a long, long pause—too long—and then, "You’re right! It’s, Shaun Critzer!"
The crowd erupted. My family and friends were going insane somewhere in the seats. Some crying happy tears. And Bill Davey—that year's Mr. America—walked onto the stage and put not one but two IOC gold medals around my neck and whispered to me, "You look incredible man, keep up the great work and you and I will be going head to head in a few years."
I’d done it. Won the same title Lee Haney won before becoming one of the greatest of all time — and the same teenage crown Arnold himself had won on the European stage at my age. Seventeen years old, and I'd reached the pinnacle of natural teen bodybuilding. Cementing my name in the sport's history alongside some of the all-time greats. Something that no one can ever take from me.
It should have been enough.
________________


I even got a vanity license plate. Virginia only allows seven characters, so to fit “MR TEEN USA,” I had to get creative: MRTNUSA.

My friends thought it was hilarious — and they made sure I heard about it.
“Who’s Mr. Ton-Oosah?”
“What’s Martian USAA — insurance for aliens?”
“Bro… nobody knows what that means.”


And they were right. To them, I was just Shaun. The kid they'd grown up with. The one who brought Tupperware to parties and left early for morning cardio. Slapping a vanity plate on my car didn't make me a champion in their eyes—it just made me the guy who needed everyone to know he'd won something.
I could laugh along with them. Even thought it was funny. But underneath? I needed that plate. Needed the email address I'd created: mrteenusa98@hotmail.com. Needed the visible proof that I'd accomplished something, that I was someone worth noticing.
I'd learned this lesson at States. Knew the high wouldn't last. Knew the medals wouldn't fill the void. Knew external validation had a shelf life.
But I chased it anyway. Because for a day or two, it worked. And that was better than nothing.
The vanity plate. The email address. The need for visible proof that I mattered.
I was already an addict. I just didn't know it yet
________________


Six months later, I got invited to the Arnold Classic in Columbus, Ohio—the second-biggest bodybuilding show in the world after the Olympia, hosted by Arnold himself.
I met him. Actually sat down to dinner with Arnold Schwarzenegger. Got my picture taken with the guy whose movies I'd watched obsessively as a kid, whose physique had inspired my entire transformation.
By that point, he'd lost a lot of weight from undergoing a heart procedure. Shockingly to me, I might have actually been bigger than him. Arnold wasn't competing anymore—he'd retired years ago, focused on acting and politics. But it was surreal to sit across from my idol and realize: I'd done what I set out to do. I'd built a body that could stand next to his.
And yet…
Columbus was also where I started to see the underbelly beneath the polished muscle and stage tan. The part no magazine covered. The part I could never have imagined back at fifteen, sitting in the audience at my first Jan Tana Classic watching Jax.
The dark side of bodybuilding.
The politics.
The favoritism.
The whispers about deals and decisions that seemed to be made long before anyone hit their first mandatory pose. The predators who hovered around the sport like vultures—coaches, sponsors, so-called "photographers" who used bodybuilding as a hunting ground. Men far too interested in teenage and twenty-year-old competitors. Men who offered "opportunities" that always came with a cost.
The swingers. The open marriages. The after-parties that were basically orgies with wristbands. None of it hidden. None of it whispered about. None of it in the shadows.
It was public. It was celebrated. It was normal.
The night of the Arnold Classic, we hit the Columbus clubs, and I remember feeling both mesmerized and sick at the same time. Competitors were on stages and tabletops, half-naked, drunk, euphoric, dry-humping to bass lines while someone next to them cut out lines of God-knows-what right on the table beside them. No shame. No hesitation. No secrecy.
Then there were the guys doing "private work." Gay-for-pay. Whatever the camera called for, they did — not because they wanted to, but because bodybuilding was expensive and dignity didn’t pay for gear.
Back then, unless you were top three at the biggest shows or signed to a major supplement company, you couldn't afford the drugs the pros were taking. So men—and women—did whatever they had to do to get what they thought they needed.
And these were the people I had idolized. The heroes whose posters hung in my bedroom. The champions whose footsteps I was trying to follow.
But up close? They weren't gods. They weren't legends. They weren't even stable.
They were human—painfully human—cracked and clawing at the edges of a dream that demanded everything and gave almost nothing in return.
________________


But there was another side of it too. The side that was just as intoxicating… and just as dangerous.
Because around that same time, something else started happening.
Women started noticing me. Not just glancing — noticing.
At the clubs, at the gym, at the mall… even at gas stations and restaurants. A shift I didn't expect. A shift I didn't know how to handle.
One night in Columbus, after leaving the Arnold Classic, we ended up at a gentlemen’s club. Nothing unusual for bodybuilders. But the attention I got that night was different.
Dancers were coming over, asking me to stay after closing, asking me to go home with them. One told me flat-out, "I'll do anything you want." Another asked to take a picture with me and whispered things in my ear I'd only ever heard in adult entertainment.
And it didn't stop there.
The following summer, I'd walk through amusement parks with my shirt off and women—grown women—would come up to me, touch my chest, ask for pictures, ask if I was single, tell me I was ‘beautiful,’ tell me they ‘loved me,’ tell me they wanted me. Complete strangers.
It was surreal. Flattering. Confusing. And electrifying in a way my nervous system wasn't prepared for.
But here's what I didn't expect: it also made me sick.
Not because I didn't want the attention—I did. I'd craved it my whole life. Built my entire body chasing it. But when I finally got it, something felt hollow. Wrong. Because these same women wouldn't have given me the time of day when I was skinny. They didn't see me. They saw the shell I'd constructed. The armor. The performance.
And that realization cut deep.
We're shallow creatures, aren't we? All of us. We want to believe people love us for who we are, but the truth is most of them only notice us when we become something they want. When we become useful to their desires.
So even as the attention washed over me—even as my nervous system lit up with that primal validation—I felt like an imposter. Because I knew the real me was still underneath. The scared kid. The one with secrets. The one who hadn't done the healing work, hadn't processed the trauma, hadn't become someone worth wanting.
I was just wearing a costume made of muscle.
And here's the truth I didn't understand at the time: I wasn't just responding to desire—I was responding to safety. To validation. To proof that I was wanted. That I wasn't powerless anymore. That I wasn't the little boy in the Cage.
Once you've tasted that feeling—once you've felt the world fall at your feet—it becomes its own drug. A legal one. A socially acceptable one. A powerful one.
I can't tell you how many bodybuilders I've met who carry childhood wounds deeper than their striations—men chasing the high of being desired, men who needed the attention as badly as they needed protein, men who used their bodies the same way I did: as armor, as currency, as proof we were worth something.
I fit into that world perfectly. Too perfectly. And that scared me more than all the darkness in Columbus combined.
Because I wasn't just succeeding in the sport. I was disappearing into it.
________________


That same year in the summer of '99, I won my second consecutive Virginia State Championship title. Three competitions, three wins. I was undefeated, and it was starting to feel like invincibility—like I'd found something I couldn't fail at. But that streak ended at the Teen Nationals in Cleveland that fall. The same National Championship I’d won the year before in Virginia Beach. For the Cleveland competition, I had my body fat measured by Dr. Periello—my pediatrician since birth who sat on the IOC advisory board and knew what he was talking about.
2.7% body fat.
For context, essential body fat for males is about 3-4%. Below that, you start risking organ function. I was basically walking around with no fat on my body—just muscle and skin and the absolute bare minimum to keep my heart beating and my brain functioning.
I dropped to third place that year. Still impressive. Still a podium finish at a national competition. But not first. Not the gold.
And that stung more than I wanted to admit.
Because third wasn't enough. Second wouldn't have been enough. Even first probably wouldn't have been enough.
That's the disease, right? Never satisfied. Never arrived. Never able to just be proud of what you've accomplished and let it be enough.
Always chasing.
________________


May 2000. The NPC Pittsburgh Championships. Jim Manion's show. Jim was the president of the IFBB and known as the boss in most circles of bodybuilding.
A lot had changed by then. The people around me were shifting. The community felt different.
The guest posers that night read like a hall of fame. Ronnie Coleman—who'd won his first Mr. Olympia in September '98, just weeks after I won Mr. Teen USA in August. Chris Cormier, one of the biggest partiers in all of bodybuilding, whose addiction would eventually become the subject of a documentary. Melvin Anthony, one of my favorite posers of all time. Paul Dillett, just a freak of nature. Craig Titus and Kelly Ryan were also there—names that would eventually make headlines for darker reasons.
And Nasser El Sonbaty.
The first professional bodybuilder I'd ever seen in person—at Jax's Jan Tana Classic five years earlier, when I was just a fifteen-year-old kid with a dream and the show's program rolled-up in my back pocket. Now here I was, backstage with these guys. Standing among them. Part of their world.
But the moment that stopped me cold was sitting down with Lee Haney.
Sitting across from the man whose teenage title I had followed nineteen years later felt surreal. He wasn’t just a name in a book anymore. He was right there, talking to me about discipline, longevity, and what the sport had cost him and given him. He treated me like an equal. Like I belonged.
He gave me advice I still carry with me. Such an amazing man and ambassador to the sport.
I got third that day. Respectable, but not what I wanted.
One of my sponsors said it to me directly after: "You've got great potential, Shaun. But you know what you have to do to take it to the next level."
He meant steroids. Everyone knew it.
I chose not to.
________________


Two months later, July 8, 2000. The NPC Drug Free Virginia States. My last teen show.
I won. Third state title in a row.
But the victory felt hollow. Something had gone down with Gage that weekend — something bad — and it poisoned the whole experience for me. I was more pissed about what went down with him than proud of my own placing.
Three state titles. One national title. Two Gold medals. Trophies. A legacy. And even an eventual place in the bodybuilding hall of fame in York, Pennsylvania.
And standing on that podium, I felt nothing.
That was the end of my teen bodybuilding career. Not with a bang, but with a shrug.


________________


But back in 1998, standing on that stage in Virginia Beach with two gold medals around my neck and the title of Mr. Teen USA attached to my name—I didn't know any of that yet.
All I knew was that I'd won. I'd reached the top. I'd proven I was enough.
Except I hadn't.
The trophy didn't fix that. The medals didn't heal that. The women didn't validate that—not really. Because underneath all the muscle and discipline and performance, I was still the same scared kid. Still carrying the same wounds. Still believing that who I really was—the authentic me, without the armor—wasn't acceptable. Wasn't enough. Wasn't worth wanting.
I'd gotten exactly what I thought I wanted. The gold. The attention. The validation.
And it still wasn't enough.
Because external validation never fills the internal void. Not when there's healing work you haven't done. Not when there's trauma you haven't processed. Not when the person you're trying to convince isn't the crowd or the women or the judges—but yourself.
Standing there in Virginia Beach at seventeen, golden boy with gold medals…I thought I'd arrived.
I was wrong.
I didn’t know then how high I was — or how far I would fall.


CHAPTER 11: JAX


I first saw Jax compete when I was fifteen—Rachel took me to that Jan Tana Classic in Roanoke. But we didn't really get tight until I started competing myself my senior year. Once I won States in April '98, once I proved I was serious about this, Jax took me under his wing for real. 


For about two years—from spring of senior year through my first year of college—Jax and I were inseparable. Training together. Competing together. Living that bodybuilding life.


And then, that fall, I met Gage—briefly—while he was home on leave from the service. Two brothers. Two mentors. Two completely different paths that would both shape who I'd become.


But Jax was first.


Seeing him on stage was one thing. Training with him was another.


Jax was ten years older than me—my sister's age. He rode BMX semi-professionally in his teen years, had a half-pipe in his backyard that the neighborhood kids would come watch him shred. But bodybuilding was his real love while I was in his life. And he was, by far, one of the best local male competitors we'd ever had.


Our gym had two absolute standouts—people who operated on a different level than everyone else.


Beth was the most elite female bodybuilder in the area. Only a few years older than me. She was from about an hour north in Northern Virginia near the D.C. area. She was studying opera and biomedical engineering at U.V.A. She eventually went on to become an IFBB pro in both bodybuilding and fitness. And on the weekend’s, she’d go back to D.C. and strip, bringing home anywhere from 5-10k in a single weekend. Good money for any college student in the late 90s. She was the kind of person who shattered every stereotype you had about dedication, intelligence, or strippers. Apparently, just being a brilliant woman who could calculate protein synthesis rates and hit high C's wasn't enough.


Brilliant, gorgeous, cool as hell. Just on another level entirely. And clearly, a person of extremes, like myself.


And Jax was the male equivalent. The best local male competitor I ever got to train with.


When I’d go out with these two in public, the looks that they would get, was something like nothing I’d ever experienced. Something straight out of a movie clip where everyone just literally stops, jaws to the floor and eyes wide open. Stunned and confused by what they were seeing. I knew I needed, not wanted, needed, to have that same experience myself. And I was willing to do almost anything to achieve just that.
These were the two I watched. The two I wanted to be like. The two who showed me what was possible if you were willing to go all the way. And two amazing people I was honored enough to ever call, my friends.


Jax became my mentor. My coach. My blueprint for what I wanted to become.
Jax taught me everything. The ins and outs of competition prep. How to tan properly—Pro Tan versus Jan Tana, which worked better for different skin tones. How to oil up before you walk on stage so the lights catch every muscle. Water intake, sodium intake. How to carb load and carb deplete to get that full, dry look the judges wanted.


We traveled to competitions up and down the East Coast together. I always drove—Jax in the passenger seat coaching me the whole way. Telling me about training protocols, nutrition timing, posing strategies. Those highway hours became my real education.


The science of bodybuilding. The art of posing. The psychology of competition. The discipline required to transform your body into something that made people stop and stare.


He showed me Dan Duchaine's "Steroid Bible"—this underground manual that's worth hundreds of dollars now. It was one of the only pieces of published material available for those interested in better understanding how to optimally use physique enhancing drugs in the 80’s and 90’s. Showed me how the guys who were serious about going pro approached the science of enhancement.


And a few times, he even let me watch him inject his steroids. Drew up the syringe. Found the spot. Pushed the plunger. It felt clinical, almost scientific — like we were running some sort of experiment. Not illegal. Not dangerous. Just part of the process.
I didn't think twice about it then. Later I thought about it constantly.
What he was doing wasn’t technically illegal. He had a doctor who prescribed everything he was taking. He never told me the doctor’s name — and I never asked — which I understood and respected. But even with that, it didn’t feel completely above board.


But more than the technical stuff, Jax showed me what it meant to be part of this world. The camaraderie. The brotherhood. The way bodybuilders supported each other even when they were competing against each other.


He was proud of me. Really proud. Like a big brother watching his little brother succeed.


When I'd nail a pose in practice, he'd grin. "That's it, brother. That's the one. You're gonna kill it."

He believed in me. Saw potential in this skinny kid who was willing to do whatever it took to transform himself.


And I looked up to him like he was invincible. Like nothing could touch him. Like all that muscle and discipline and dedication made him immune to the things that hurt normal people.

He'd gone through a divorce that gutted him. Got a DUI that almost cost him everything. Was depressed, probably alcoholic and self-medicating in ways I didn't fully understand at the time.

Jax wanted that. Wanted to prove you could be both—the cop and the champion, the protector and the spectacle.


But the night shifts were killing him. Literally.


He'd work all night, come home exhausted, try to train during the day on no sleep. Taking ephedrine stacks to stay awake and burn fat—homemade ECA stacks: ephedrine, caffeine, aspirin. Speed you up, dry you out, thin your blood. Perfect for getting shredded.


Also perfect for destroying your cardiovascular system.


And Jax was born with an enlarged heart. A genetic condition he probably didn't know he had, or if he did, chose to ignore because stopping wasn't an option. The show must go on. The training must continue. The armor must be maintained.


I used to hear stories about pro bodybuilders being up in their hotel rooms hooked up to IV drips before shows. Having actual doctors come monitor them because what they were putting their bodies through was life-and-death serious.


And even at that point, I was witnessing men in their thirties, forties, fifties just dropping dead of heart attacks.
The sport I loved—the discipline that had saved me—was also slowly killing people. But we didn't talk about that. We talked about trophies and titles and who had the best conditioning and who was using what to get that edge.


We didn't talk about the cost.


Not until it was too late.

He was so proud. Like watching his training pay off. Like seeing his methods work on someone who actually listened and applied everything he taught.


"You did it, brother," he said, that grin splitting his face. "State champion. Seventeen years old. You're just getting started."


He taught me posing routines. How to move between poses fluidly, like a performance, not just flexing. How to command the stage. How to make bodybuilding feel like art instead of just displaying muscle.


Later, after I'd competed a few more times, other competitors would ask me to choreograph their routines. I loved that. Loved being the guy who excelled at the artistry, not just the physique.


That came from Jax. Everything good about my approach to bodybuilding came from Jax.


The bodybuilding community became my family. These massive humans who could bench press Volkswagens but would cry at bagpipes playing at a fallen brother's funeral. Who competed against each other on stage but would spot each other in the gym the next day.


Who showed me that strength isn't just about muscle. It's about showing up. Being consistent. Having each other's backs.


Jax embodied all of that. Was the best of us. The strongest, the most dedicated, the most likely to make it to the pro level if he could just keep it together a little longer.


He was my hero. My blueprint. The person I wanted to be when I grew up.


I thought he was invincible.


I was wrong.

Charlotte, North Carolina. After a bodybuilding show. Club Millennium—or Club 2000, depending on who you asked.

And then it waits. Bides its time. Lets you think you're managing it, handling it, keeping it contained.

The bottle was patient.
It could wait.

I didn't know yet that normal people don't necessarily black out on their first night drinking. Normal people don't drive home with no memory of the trip. Normal people don't immediately fall in love with the feeling of being intoxicated.
Normal people can take it or leave it.
I couldn't. Not from the very beginning.
But I didn't know that yet. All I knew was that Jax had introduced me to something magical. Something that made me feel the way I'd always wanted to feel—confident, comfortable, okay.
And I wanted more. What started as magic would become medicine. And what became medicine would eventually become misery.

And together, we'd take what started in Charlotte and turn it into something that would nearly destroy us both.
But that comes later.
For now, in Charlotte, in Club Millennium, with a Long Island Iced Tea in my hand and Jax by my side—I'd opened the door.

CHAPTER 13: MEETING Gage

CHAPTER 14: Jax’s Death

Heart attack. Not even thirty years old.


They said it happened in his sleep. Peacefully, they said, like that was supposed to be comforting. Like dying at twenty-nine in your sleep because your heart gave out was somehow okay because at least you didn't suffer.


But he did suffer. I know he did. Because I knew Jax. Knew the depression he carried. Knew the divorce that had gutted him. Knew the DUI that had almost cost him the cop job he'd worked so hard to get. Knew the way he'd use the gym and the performance of being invincible to avoid feeling any of it.


And now he was gone. And I was still here. And I couldn't understand why.
I found out later he was born with an enlarged heart. A genetic condition. But the night shifts, the alcohol, the ephedrine, steroids and the stress and the depression—none of that helped.


All that armor. All that muscle. All that discipline and dedication and superhuman effort.


And his heart just... stopped.


His family donated his body to science. I'd never heard of anyone doing that before. Still haven't since. But it made a weird kind of sense—Jax had turned his body into an experiment, a science project, a test of how far you could push human physiology. Maybe his death could teach someone something. Maybe the sacrifice could mean something.


The memorial was held at our local county office building. His police squad car parked right outside. The turnout was enormous—cops in uniform, bodybuilders in suits that barely fit their shoulders, friends and family and everyone whose life he'd touched.


All of us big macho men stood there crying.


I got up to speak. I don't remember what I said—I was in a near-blackout state of grief, words just pouring out of me without conscious thought. But I said something. Had to. Had to honor him, honor what he'd meant to me, honor the mentor who'd shown me how to be impressive, how to command attention, how to build armor strong enough to protect me from the world.


They played the bagpipes. That Scottish song they play at police and firefighter funerals. And that sent me over the edge. Absolutely destroyed me.


His parents gave me his training belt and lifting straps to remember him by. He had been sponsored by Body Masters, the company that outfitted most of the serious gyms equipment in the late '90s and they let me keep his Body Masters track warm up suit too.


I wore that track suit to every competition after that. In his memory. In his honor. Carrying his armor even as I built my own.


Here's what Jax's death taught me, though I didn't understand it then:
You can't outrun being human. You can't build enough muscle or achieve enough success to make yourself immune to pain, to loss, to the fundamental vulnerability of having a body that can fail you.
Jax tried. Transformed himself into something superhuman. Became the best at what he did. And his heart stopped anyway.
Because we're not machines. Using discipline and achievement to avoid dealing with the messy, painful, vulnerable parts of being alive doesn't work. It just delays the reckoning.
But I didn't learn that lesson yet. Instead, I doubled down. Kept building. Kept achieving. Kept using bodybuilding and external validation to avoid feeling the grief, the fear, the crushing awareness that nothing I did could protect me from loss.
And when bodybuilding wasn't enough anymore—when the competitions stopped and the trophies gathered dust—I found other ways to numb. Other armor to build.
Jax taught me how to build muscle. How to pose. How to win.
His death taught me that winning doesn't matter if you're running from yourself the whole time.
I just wish I'd learned that lesson when he died. Instead of spending the next decade proving it over and over again.


CHAPTER 15: WEDDING & 9/11
________________


July 7, 2001. Jennie and I got married at twenty years old.
Both of us barely adults, still figuring out who we were as individuals, trying to build a life together on the foundation of love and hope and the belief that we could make it work if we just tried hard enough.
The wedding was decent-sized—my huge family, Jennie's good-sized family, all from the Central Virginia area. Jennie had six bridesmaids. I had twelve groomsmen standing with me.
I'd always been surrounded by people. Won the popularity superlatives in school. Had friends everywhere I went. But underneath all that social success, I still felt like a nobody. Still felt like I was performing, code-switching, being whoever each person needed me to be rather than just being myself.
All the bodybuilding guys stood with me that day. Scott. Gage. My closest friends. Everyone who'd been part of my journey so far.
And Jax was listed in the program as an honorary groomsman. He'd died fourteen months earlier—February 2000—but he was there in spirit. Had to be. He'd been too important to my story to not be honored on that day.


We got married in the church downtown Charlottesville where Jennie's grandmother attended. Traditional ceremony, beautiful day, everything you're supposed to have when you're young and in love and think you've got it all figured out.
Jennie looked stunning. That smile, those blue eyes, the way she looked at me like I was everything she'd ever wanted. And I felt it—really felt it. Not just the performance of being in love, but actual love. The kind that makes you believe you can handle whatever comes next.
I thought we'd make it. Thought love and discipline and hard work were enough to build a life together. Thought if I just stayed focused, stayed committed, stayed the guy she'd fallen for—everything would be okay.
I didn't know yet how wrong I was.

September 11, 2001. The towers fell. The Pentagon burned. A field in Pennsylvania became a crash site. Everything everyone thought they knew about safety and security and the world order just... shattered.
My response?
Go to the bar. Get drunk. Watch the insanity unfold on TV screens while the world burned.
My instinct wasn't to go home. Wasn't to be with Jennie. Wasn't to hold my wife and thank God we were safe and figure out how to process this enormous tragedy together.
My instinct was to drink.
That should have told me everything. But I wasn't listening.

"Just a few drinks" became blackouts. "Home by 11" became 3 or 4 AM. "Out with the guys" became whatever story would keep Jennie from asking too many questions.
She looked up to me. Idolized me. Put me on this pedestal so high I had nowhere to go but down. In her eyes, I was the disciplined one, the bodybuilding champion, the guy with his life together. Mr. Health and Fitness. The strong one.
And I was dying inside.

________________
But Gage was there through most of it. My brother. My partner in chaos. We were bouncing together at the Biltmore, partying until the sun came up, chasing oblivion in whatever form we could find it.
He saw me spiraling. Saw the lies I was telling Jennie. Saw me becoming someone neither of us recognized. But he never judged. Never lectured. Just kept having my back. That's what real brotherhood looks like—not the fair-weather kind, but the person who stays when you're at your worst.

And I was making choices I can't justify. Doing things I'm ashamed of. Destroying our marriage from the inside while blaming her for pulling away.
Hypocrisy is just another form of armor. Another way to avoid looking at yourself.

Training together when I could still function enough to train. Partying together when training wasn't an option anymore. Living that life like we thought we were invincible.
I'd drive hours to cop in neighborhoods where my presence alone was a death wish. Projects. Trap houses. Places with no names I'd write down, with people whose real names I never knew. Places where white boys didn't make it out alive.
Smoking crack out of Natty Light cans with kingpins and murderers.

Gage was there for all of it. Sometimes as the voice of reason, sometimes as the partner in crime, always as the brother who wouldn't let me go through it alone.
His legal troubles from the steroid shipments were still hanging over him—lawyers working deals, the threat of federal time. But Gage handled it. Always did.

Jax had been dead since February 2000. The hole he left hadn't healed. The grief hadn't processed. The fear of what his death meant—that all this work, all this discipline, all this sacrifice could just end in a heart attack before you're thirty—was too terrifying to face without chemical assistance.
So I drank. And I drugged. And I told myself I was just processing grief, just dealing with the stress of being married, just figuring out adult life.

And he'd nod. Because what else could he do? You can't save someone who doesn't want to be saved. You can't stop someone from self-destructing when they're committed to the path.
All you can do is be there. Show up. Have their back when they hit bottom.
That's what Gage did for me. For three years. From the wedding in 2001 through the chaos of 2002 and into 2003.
He was my brother. My training partner. My partner in chaos. The person who saw me at my worst and didn't abandon me.

June 2003. We were at the Outer Banks. Group trip. Friends, partying, the usual recklessness we'd been living in for years.
And then everything changed.
Gage fell.


CHAPTER 16: THE FALL 
________________


June 2003. Outer Banks, North Carolina.
Gage was bouncing at a bar down there, trying to stay out of trouble while his attorneys worked out deals on the steroid charges. The steroid charges were still pending—his lawyers working to keep it from becoming federal time.
But Gage was handling it. Always did. That's who he was—the guy who carried weight, took risks, protected the people he loved even when it meant putting himself in danger.
His friend Dean was bartending at the same place. They were making money, staying busy, trying to live while the legal system ground slowly forward with motions and negotiations and all the bureaucratic machinery that decides whether someone's life gets destroyed or not.
After their shift ended that night, they were drinking. Having fun. Just two guys unwinding after work, letting off steam, being idiots the way young drunk guys are idiots.
Not a fight. Not an argument. Just horseplay. Wrestling around. The kind of stupid shit that usually ends with someone getting a bruise or a scraped knee and everyone laughing about it the next day.
Gage was leaning against the upper-floor balcony railing.
And it gave out.
________________


Dean fell right beside him. Landed in grass or sand, something soft enough that he stood up fine. Maybe some bruises, maybe sore the next day, but nothing serious. Nothing that wouldn't heal.
Gage landed neck-first on one of those big metal drainage pipes. The circular steel kind you see in construction sites or industrial areas—the ones you can lift up to access whatever's underneath, but they're heavy as hell. The kind that doesn't give, doesn't bend, doesn't forgive.
The impact was catastrophic.
C3 quadriplegic. The same injury Christopher Reeve had. Complete paralysis from the neck down. No movement. No sensation. Dependent on machines to breathe. Everything below the neck just... gone. Not dead, but not alive in any way that makes sense. Trapped in a body that won't respond, won't move, won't do anything except lie there while machines keep the lungs inflating and the heart beating.
They helicoptered him from the Outer Banks to Norfolk General Hospital. Twenty-minute flight. Would have been an hour drive. Every minute counting, every second critical when you're dealing with spinal cord injuries and brain swelling and all the things that happen when a human body hits immovable metal at terminal velocity.
The doctors did everything they could. Surgery. Stabilization. Medications. All the medical interventions that modern medicine can throw at a catastrophic spinal injury.
But they couldn't fix it. Because you can't fix that. The spinal cord doesn't regenerate. The nerves don't reconnect. Once it's severed at that level, that's it. That's your life now—what's left of it, anyway.
They kept him alive for about a month.
________________


Jennie and I went to visit him. Multiple times. Made the drive from Charlottesville to Norfolk, through the tunnels and over the bridges, the same route we'd taken countless times for beach trips and competitions and parties. Except this time we weren't going to have fun. We were going to sit in a hospital room and watch my brother struggle to stay alive.
The last time we saw him, we were meeting my friend Ryan—the musician, one of my closest friends since middle school. The guy who'd gotten ripped one summer and made me realize I wanted that kind of attention, that kind of transformation. We were in Virginia Beach for a James Taylor concert. Jennie loved James Taylor. It was supposed to be this nice thing, this moment of peace and normalcy amidst all the chaos.
And we drove over to Norfolk General to sit with Gage before the show.
________________


The ICU was sterile and cold and smelled like disinfectant trying to cover up the smell of dying. Machines everywhere. Beeping monitors. Ventilators. IVs. All the technology keeping bodies alive when they shouldn't be alive anymore.
Gage was in a bed surrounded by all of it. Tubes down his throat. Trach so he could breathe—or rather, so the machine could breathe for him. Wires attached to his chest monitoring a heart that was still beating but might not for long.
He couldn't talk. The trach prevented that. Couldn't move anything below his neck. Could barely move his head. Could only communicate with his eyes—those same eyes that used to look at me with that Marine intensity, that Long Island edge, that protective big-brother energy that made me feel safe even when we were doing stupid shit that should have gotten us killed.
Now those eyes just looked... trapped. Desperate. Like he was screaming inside a body that couldn't make sound.
It wrecked my world.
________________


This guy who was larger than life. Who'd survived his father's abuse—beatings that should have broken him but only made him harder. Who'd survived the Marines, survived breaking his back in Okinawa, survived coming home to legal troubles and financial struggles and all the weight he carried for his family.
Who'd protected his sisters. Who'd protected me. Who'd stood beside me through my worst shit and never judged, never abandoned, never walked away.
And here he was. Completely paralyzed. Unable to breathe on his own. Unable to move. Unable to do anything except lie there and wait while the doctors tried to figure out if there was any chance, any hope, any possibility that he might recover even a fraction of what he'd lost.
We all knew the answer. But nobody wanted to say it.
________________


I sat beside his bed and tried to find words. Tried to think of something comforting, something hopeful, something that might help.
"Hang in there," I finally said. "You need to stick around for your nieces and nephews."
I meant my kids. The kids Jennie and I would eventually have. Because Gage was going to be Uncle Gage. Was going to be part of their lives. Was going to teach them to be tough and loyal and strong. Was going to show them what real brotherhood looked like.
He blinked. That was all he could do. Just blink. Trying to communicate something—agreement, hope, maybe just acknowledgment that he'd heard me.
But we both knew it was a lie. We both knew he wasn't going to make it. We both knew that surviving a C3 spinal injury is barely surviving, and the quality of life even if he did live was something neither of us wanted to think about.
I held it together while I was in that room. Stayed strong. Stayed positive. Played the role of the supportive friend who believes everything's going to be okay.
And then I left and got drunk. Because that's what I did. That's what I always did. When the feelings got too big, when the reality got too overwhelming, when I couldn't face what was happening—I drank until I couldn't feel it anymore.
________________


A lot of our other friends didn't come visit. At the time, I thought that was selfish of them. Thought they were cowards. Thought they should have been there for Gage the way he'd always been there for all of us.
But I understand now.
Because for the longest time—years after his death—I had nightmares about seeing him like that. That image burned into my brain. This savage, this gangster, this brother who'd always been so strong, so tough, so indestructible—reduced to a body in a bed with tubes and machines keeping him alive.
It's a hard thing to see. A hard thing to carry. A hard thing to process when you're in your early twenties and nobody taught you how to handle grief or trauma or watching someone you love become something unrecognizable.
So I don't blame them anymore for not visiting. I get it. Sometimes love means staying away because you can't handle what you'll see. Sometimes protection means protecting yourself, even when it looks like abandonment.
But I went. Over and over. Because he was my brother. And you don't abandon your brother, even when seeing him destroys you.
________________


About a month in, Gage struck a high fever. 107, maybe 108 degrees. The point where your brain starts cooking. The point where proteins denature and cells die and all the delicate biological machinery that makes you you starts breaking down irreversibly.
The point where the body just can't fight anymore.
Infections are common with spinal cord injuries. The body's paralyzed, can't regulate temperature properly, can't fight off invaders the way it's supposed to. And when infection sets in at that level of injury, there's not much the doctors can do except pump you full of antibiotics and hope.
The hope didn't work.
Gage went into a coma. Fever-induced. Brain swelling. All the things that happen when your body's temperature spikes that high for that long.
They registered him as brain dead. No higher brain function. No consciousness. Just a body kept alive by machines, with nothing left of the person who used to inhabit it.
His mom made the decision to pull the plug.
I can't imagine what that was like for her. Can't imagine having to choose to end your son's life. Can't imagine signing papers that say yes, turn off the machines, let him go, let him die because there's nothing left to save.
But she did it. Because she loved him. Because keeping him alive like that wasn't mercy—it was torture. Because sometimes the kindest thing you can do for someone is let them go.
________________


After he died, the feds contacted his family.
Required them to produce a death certificate. Proof that Gage was actually dead and not faking it to escape the pending charges. Proof that this wasn't some elaborate scheme to disappear and avoid federal prosecution.
I thought it was pretty insensitive at the time. His family was grieving, planning a funeral, trying to process the loss of a son and brother—and the government was demanding documentation like he was just another case file that needed closing.
But I guess when you're dealing with people facing serious charges—especially with connections and resources—you've probably had plenty of heavy hitters fake their deaths and go on the run. Probably seen every con, every scheme, every attempt to escape consequences.
Still felt cold, though. Just one more way the system dehumanizes people, reduces them to paperwork and procedure even in death.
But they got their death certificate. Closed their file. Moved on to the next case.
And Gage's family got to bury their son.
________________


The funeral was enormous. Family, friends, people from the gym, people from his past. All of us gathering to say goodbye to someone who'd died too young, too suddenly, too senselessly.
Of all our friends—all the guys who'd trained with him, partied with him, lived that life alongside him—I was the only non-family pallbearer.
Everyone else was family. Sisters. Cousins. Uncles. Blood relatives who had the official claim to grief, the socially recognized right to carry his casket.
And me. His brother. Not by blood, but by choice. By years of training together, shaving each other's backs, sharing secrets, standing beside each other when shit got hard.
I carried his casket. Helped lower him into the ground. Stood there while they said prayers and threw dirt and did all the rituals humans have invented to make death feel less final, less brutal, less like a permanent ending to someone who should have had decades left to live.
________________


After the funeral, I visited his grave every single day for months.
Rain, shine, hungover, high—it didn't matter. I went. Sat there on the grass beside the headstone. Talked to him. Cried. Raged. Bargained with God or the universe or whatever might be listening.
Why him? Why not me? I'm the one destroying everything. I'm the one making stupid choices. Why did he have to die when I'm still here being a piece of shit?
No answers. There never are. Just silence and grass and the terrible weight of knowing I'd never see my brother again.
And I never, not once, went there sober.
Because the grief was too big. The loss was too devastating. The fear of what his death meant—that life could just end like that, that all our strength and toughness and armor couldn't protect us from a faulty railing and bad luck and random chance—was too much to face without chemical assistance.
So I drank. Every day. Every single day. And soon, I'd add cocaine and pills and weed and anything else that could help me not feel the enormity of what I'd lost.
June 2003 to January 2013. Ten years. Not a single day sober.
That was my decade of darkness.
And it started at Gage's grave, with tears and Jim Beam and the realization that I'd never be the same.


CHAPTER 17: THE GRAVEYARD
________________


The graveyard became my second home.
Not immediately—there were a few days after the funeral where I tried to stay away, tried to pretend I could function normally, tried to convince myself that I didn't need to sit beside a hole in the ground talking to someone who couldn't hear me anymore.
But the pull was too strong. The need to be near him, even if "near him" just meant being near a headstone with his name carved into it.
So I went. Every day. Sometimes twice a day. Rain, shine, hungover, high—it didn't matter.
I'd sit on the grass beside his grave and talk to him like he was still there. Tell him about my day, about what was happening, about how everything felt wrong without him. Ask him questions I knew he couldn't answer. Apologize for things I couldn't change.
Sometimes I'd bring Jim Beam. Pour some on his grave, drink the rest myself. A toast to my brother. A ritual that made me feel like we were still doing this together, still partners, still connected even though he was six feet underground and I was sitting on top of the dirt trying not to fall apart.
I never went sober. Not once. Because sober meant feeling everything, and feeling everything meant drowning in grief so deep I didn't think I'd ever find my way back to the surface.
________________


One day, a few weeks after the funeral, I was driving to the cemetery. It was raining and the sun was shining simultaneously. There’s a saying for it, “The devil’s beating his wife”—anonymous to say the least given what I was about to walk into.
I pulled up and immediately noticed the vehicle. Old school OJ Simpson slow speed getaway style white Bronco with New York plates. Couldn't miss it—distinctive truck, out-of-state tags that screamed Long Island.
And standing by Gage's grave were two people: his mom and some guy I'd never seen before but knew exactly who he was.
The thick build. The New York posture. The way he stood there like he had any right to be at that grave.
Gage's father.
The man Gage had promised to kill if he ever saw him again. The man who'd beaten him, abused his mother, terrorized the whole family. The man who'd turned Gage into someone who believed violence was love and protection meant becoming a weapon.
I sat in my car for a minute, hands gripping the steering wheel, debating whether to leave or stay. Part of me wanted to drive away, to let them have their moment, to avoid a confrontation that couldn't end well.
But this was my brother's grave. And I'd been showing up every day for weeks. I wasn't going to let this piece of shit chase me away.
So I got out. Walked over. Held my bottle of Jim Beam like it was armor.
His mom saw me first. Gave me a small nod—acknowledgment without words. She knew why I was there. Knew I'd been coming every day. Knew that her son and I had been brothers in the way that mattered.
The father looked at me. No warmth in his eyes. No shared grief. No acknowledgment that we'd both lost someone we cared about.
"Still doesn't seem real," I said. Because it didn't. Because even standing there looking at the grave with Gage's name on it, part of me still expected him to show up, to tell me this was all some elaborate joke, to be alive and pissed off and ready to bounce at the Biltmore like nothing had happened.
He looked at me with those cold eyes. Thick New York accent, the kind that sounds like gravel and cigarettes and streets that don't forgive weakness.
"Seems pretty fuckin' real to me," he said.


________________


That was it. That was the extent of our exchange.
No apology for what he'd done to his son. No regret for the beatings, the abuse, the way he'd turned a kid into someone who had to become hard just to survive. No acknowledgment that maybe—just maybe—he bore some responsibility for the kind of life Gage had lived, the kind of choices he'd made, the kind of ending he'd met.
Just: "Seems pretty fuckin' real to me."
And then he walked away.
________________


I stood there shaking. Not from fear—from rage. Pure, white-hot rage that this man had the audacity to show up here, to stand at his son's grave like he'd been a father instead of a monster, to act like he had any right to grieve when he'd been the one who'd made Gage's life a living hell.
I wanted to chase him down. Wanted to beat him the way he'd beaten Gage. Wanted to make him feel a fraction of the pain he'd caused. Wanted to honor the promise Gage had made—that if he ever saw his father again, he'd kill him.
But I didn't. Because Gage was dead. And violence wouldn't bring him back. And I was already destroying myself fast enough without adding assault charges to the list.
So I just sat back down beside the grave. Took another drink. Let the rage burn itself out while the alcohol dulled the edges.
"Your dad's a dick, bro," I said to the headstone. "You were right about him."
The headstone didn't respond. It never did. But saying it out loud made me feel better anyway.
________________


The thing about visiting a grave every day is that you start to notice patterns. The other people who show up. The flowers that appear and disappear. The way the grass grows over the fresh dirt until eventually you can't tell where the grave ends and the rest of the cemetery begins.
I'd see his mom sometimes. His sisters. Other family members whose names I didn't always remember but whose grief I recognized because it looked like mine—raw and desperate and searching for something that couldn't be found in a cemetery.
We'd nod at each other. Sometimes we'd talk. Mostly we'd just exist in parallel universes of grief, each of us trying to make sense of a loss that didn't make sense.
His mom never said anything about me being there so often. Never questioned why this non-family member was spending more time at her son's grave than some of his actual relatives. I think she understood. I think she knew that Gage and I had been brothers in the way that mattered, and that brothers grieve differently than parents or sisters or cousins.
Or maybe she was just too deep in her own grief to care who else was drowning alongside her.
________________


The daily visits became a ritual. A structure to my days when everything else was chaos.
Wake up hungover. Go to the grave. Drink. Talk to Gage. Cry if I needed to. Rage if I needed to. Sit in silence if I couldn't find words.
Then go home. Or to the Biltmore. Or to whatever party or bar or trap house I was using to avoid being alone with my thoughts.
And the next day, do it all again.
The grave became the one constant in a life that was rapidly falling apart. My marriage was disintegrating. My sobriety—what little I'd had before Gage died—was completely gone. My ability to function like a normal human being was eroding day by day.
But I could still go to the grave. Could still sit beside that headstone. Could still pretend that as long as I kept showing up, as long as I kept talking to him, maybe he wasn't really gone.
________________


I'd tell him things I couldn't tell anyone else. Confess things I was too ashamed to say out loud to a living person. Admit how badly I was fucking up my life, how scared I was, how much I didn't know what to do without him.
"I don't know how to do this," I'd say. "I don't know how to be okay without you here. I don't know how to stop drinking. I don't know how to fix my marriage. I don't know how to be the person everyone thinks I am."
The headstone, of course, never offered advice. Never told me what to do. Never gave me the answers I was desperate for.
But somehow, just saying it out loud—admitting to someone, even if that someone was dead—made the burden a little lighter. Made the chaos a little more bearable. Made me feel less alone in the darkness.
________________


Looking back now, I can see what I was doing: I was trying to keep him alive. Trying to maintain the connection we'd had when he was breathing. And in some ways, I succeeded—Gage never really left me. His voice stayed in my head, his way of seeing the world became part of my internal landscape.
But instead of honoring his memory by getting my life together, I spiraled deeper. Used his death as an excuse to drink more, drug more, destroy more. Turned his grave into a place where I could wallow instead of doing the hard work of healing.
________________


Months passed. The daily visits continued. The drinking continued. The slow-motion collapse of everything I'd built continued.
Jennie would ask where I'd been. I'd tell her the grave. She'd stop asking eventually, because what could she say? You can't argue with someone for grieving. You can't tell them to stop visiting their dead brother's grave and get on with life.
Even when the grief becomes an addiction of its own. Even when the visits become another form of escape. Even when sitting beside a headstone with a bottle becomes preferable to facing the living, breathing consequences of your choices.
________________


I don't remember the last time I went to that grave before I finally got sober in 2013. Don't remember consciously deciding to stop. Just know that eventually, the daily visits became weekly, then monthly, then not at all.
Life moved on. Addiction escalated. I hit bottom after bottom, each one lower than the last. Got arrested. Got hospitalized. Got divorced. Lost my kids. Lost my home. Lost everything.
And through it all, Gage was still there in my head. Still my brother. Still the guy I measured myself against, the guy whose loyalty I could never quite live up to, the guy whose death I'd never fully processed because I'd drowned it in Jim Beam instead of feeling it.

Chapter 18: The Decade of Darkness


June 2003 to January 2013. Ten years. Not a single day sober.


Here's what that looked like.


---


The Biltmore became my stomping ground. The restaurant-bar where Gage had bounced, where I'd spent so much time with him, became the place I went every single night. Bouncing, dealing, using. The spiral accelerating, consequences piling up, my life becoming unrecognizable even to myself.


Christmas party 2003. Six months after Gage died. I was in the bathroom with a line of dudes, and someone had cocaine lined up on the back of the toilet.


"You want some?"


I'd been doing cocaine since Christmas 2001—sporadic at first, a party here, a late night there. But with Gage gone, the last guardrail was removed. I leaned into it hard. What difference did it make? Everything was already destroyed. Might as well destroy myself too.


I snorted that line. And I got addicted real quick.


Not slowly. Not over time. Immediately. The way some people do with certain drugs—one hit and your brain just goes *yes, this, more of this, always this*.


---


At my worst, I was in upstate New York. In Harlem ghettos. Transporting things that would have gotten me locked up for life if I'd been caught. Going into neighborhoods where white boys aren't welcome. Where twelve-year-old kids sit on corners as spotters, whistling coded signals to let people in the high-rises know when cops are coming or strangers are approaching.


I was around killers. Actual convicted murderers. Kingpins. Drug dealers operating on a scale I'd never imagined. And I'd find myself in these situations thinking: *What the fuck have I become?*


I was the only white dude in rooms full of people who had every reason not to trust me. No fear whatsoever on the outside—that chameleon skill I'd developed in middle school serving me well, making me blend in, making people think I belonged.


But underneath? Still that scared little boy. Still the oxygen tent kid. Still the Cage kid. Just with more armor, more bravado, more chemicals in my system to numb the truth of what I was doing.


I was living out Tony Soprano. Scarface. Whatever movie I'd watched that made this life look glamorous instead of pathological.


---


Consequences should have mattered. Should have stopped me. But they were nearly irrelevant.


Multiple near-arrests. Situations where I should have been locked up for decades. But I kept slipping through, kept getting away, kept pushing my luck further and further.


I was driving a brand-new fifty-thousand-dollar Denali with 24-inch rims—these big blade things that stuck out from the actual wheels. Spinners, people called them. You could see me coming from a mile away. I was a moving target for authorities, looking exactly like someone who's up to no good.


One night, I blew out my front and rear tires—cost almost five grand just for the wheels and tires—and sparks were flying up into the car as I drove. A state trooper pulled up behind me. Found out a tractor-trailer had dropped its rear axle about a mile up the road.


I blew double over the legal limit while sitting there talking to him.


But I knew a few things about police canines—had a buddy whose dad was a special agent in charge. So I made small talk about how they're trained in German or Russian. Charmed this cop. Told him I had an important business meeting in the morning.


"You got somebody sober who can come pick you up?"


Who did I call? My drug dealer and his girlfriend. The people I'd just been partying with all night.


And the cop let me go. Watched me get in their car. Had no idea that his girlfriend was sitting in the back seat, behind his squad car, doing drugs right out in the open while he talked to my friend.


We went right back to their place and kept partying. Didn't wake me up a bit.


---


Another night, I was blackout drunk, driving around this new development near my house with a buddy. There were these dirt paths, and we were being maniacs, and there was this pond with a dried-up creek bed and a little ramp.


I jumped that Denali. Got airborne. Hit a deer mid-flight. Came down front-first, smashed the entire front end, completely bent the frame of the vehicle.


I don't remember any of it. Total blackout.


The next morning, I woke up and went outside. Saw blood all over the vehicle. Had no memory whatsoever of hitting a deer.


My first thought: *Oh my God. I've finally done it. I've killed somebody. I'm going to jail for the rest of my life.*


This was the early 2000s—no smartphone to immediately check the news. I had to get on a laptop, search around, couldn't find anything. Spent an entire day just staying in my head, convinced I'd killed someone, waiting for police to show up and arrest me.


Finally talked to my buddy who'd been with me that night. He was laughing.


"What do you mean? You don't remember what happened?"


"No! What the hell did we hit?"


"Oh, that deer. Right behind the creek."


---


I could have killed someone. Could have been killed. Could have been locked up for life.


But I kept going. Because that's what addicts do—we go until we can't. Until the body gives out or the cops catch up or we die or something forces us to stop because stopping on our own is impossible.


Ten years. Not a single day sober. Every day using something—alcohol, cocaine, pills, weed, whatever would quiet the noise, numb the pain, help me forget that Gage was gone and I was dying and my life was a disaster I'd created with my own hands.


That was my decade of darkness.


And it would get darker still.


---


Chapter 19: The First DUI


November 19, 2005. Less than a few miles from home. I gunned it past a cop doing sixteen or seventeen over the speed limit.


Thought I could outrun him. Thought I was invincible. Thought the same luck that had gotten me out of so many other situations would save me again.


The cop caught up within a few miles. Easy. I wasn't special. Wasn't fast. Wasn't anything but drunk and stupid.


I blew a .31—almost four times the legal limit of .08.


---


They process DUI cases by presenting them to the judge from lowest to highest blood alcohol content. I was the last one. Wore it like a badge of honor in my sick mind—*see how much I can drink? See how hardcore I am?*


But the arresting officer took that honor away real quick.


He was inventorying my belongings. Standard procedure. I watched him take cash from my wallet and put it in a separate drawer from the rest of my stuff.


"When are you going to put that with the rest of my stuff?" I asked.


He looked at me with this flat expression. "You'll get that when you get out. *If* you get out."


I was gone by morning bail. My attorney laughed when I told him about the money.


"Who do you think the judge is going to believe?" he said. "You, the drunk? Or the cop?"


The lines get very blurry. Good guys, bad guys—turns out everyone's capable of corruption when the opportunity presents itself.


---


In the holding tank, I watched two OG Black dudes doing bumps of cocaine right under the camera. Bold as anything. No attempt to hide it.


The COs had to see it on the monitors. Had to know. But nothing happened. They just let it go.


Corruption everywhere. The system I'd trusted, the authorities I'd respected—turns out they were just as broken as the rest of us. Just had badges that let them get away with it.


The DUI cost me almost five to ten thousand dollars. Interlock breathalyzer in my car for a year—had to blow clean before the engine would start, then blow again randomly while driving. Thirty days in jail, but I was able to do it on weekends.


Mom, Dad, Jennie, and baby Landon in his car seat dropped me off. Watched me walk into jail like I was dropping off laundry. The shame was enormous. The guilt was crushing. But not crushing enough to make me stop drinking.


---


My first weekend in jail, there was this old man. Black dude who looked like he'd seen everything the world had to offer and none of it had been kind.


I was bitching. Complaining about my situation. Poor me, stuck in here, life is so unfair, blah blah blah.


"When you getting out?" he asked.


"Monday, I hope."


"How about you?"


"I'm not. Got twenty-five to life."


I shut up. Felt like an idiot. Here I was, doing weekend jail, would be out in a month total, complaining to a man who would die in prison.


Then he said something I'd never forget:


"But you know what, Shaun? No matter what, there is always, always something you can be grateful for."


That should have landed. Should have sunk in. Should have changed something in me.


But I went right back to bitching and complaining. Went right back to drinking as soon as I got out. Went right back to the same patterns, the same behaviors, the same slow-motion suicide I'd been committing since Gage died.


That old man taught me something I wouldn't understand for years: gratitude isn't about circumstances. It's about perspective. It's about choosing to see what you have instead of only seeing what you've lost.


But I wasn't ready to hear it yet. Wasn't ready to change. Wasn't ready to admit that my way wasn't working, that I needed help, that I couldn't keep living like this.


So I didn't. I just kept going. Kept drinking. Kept destroying. Kept dying one day at a time.


Until the day I couldn't anymore.


---


Chapter 20: Landon


May 27, 2005. "We got a long one here," the nurse said. Twenty-two inches.


My son. My perfect, beautiful, different son.


He's nearly six-foot-five now, that long baby who couldn't stop growing. But on that day in 2005, all I knew was that he was here, he was alive, and everything was about to change in ways I couldn't possibly imagine.


---


Before Landon was born, the ultrasounds had already shown problems. Hydronephrosis—fluid on his kidneys. Stage 2, which meant surgery would be needed, but his body had to grow enough first to handle it. Six months minimum. Six months of hoping that fluid sitting on those kidneys wasn't causing permanent damage.


My dad had cancer that same year. Would survive—he's still alive today, twenty-one years later—but at the time, we didn't know. My grandmother passed away that summer when Landon was just a few months old. Death and birth and fear all mixing together in this chaotic soup of life happening too fast.


And then there was the emergency C-section.


Jennie's oxygen levels were dropping. There was a small knot in the umbilical cord. Not big enough to strangle him, but enough to cause concern, enough to make the doctors move fast. Emergency surgery. Cutting her open. Pulling our son out into the world before he was quite ready.


---


At six months old, he had surgery. Bilateral ureter reimplantation—they had to reconstruct the tubes connecting his kidneys to his bladder. Major surgery for a tiny body.


I remember holding him as they administered the anesthesia. Jennie on one side, me on the other, both of us gripping this small perfect person as the drugs took effect and he went completely limp in our arms.


It must be the feeling of holding someone who just passed away. That complete absence of muscle tone, the weight of a body with no consciousness animating it. We handed him off to the surgical team—handed our son's life over to strangers, trusting them with everything, completely at the mercy of fate and skill and whatever God might be listening.


They took him through those doors. We went to the elevator. And Jennie and I just cried. Held each other and cried and waited and hoped and bargained with the universe: *please let him be okay, please let him survive this, please don't take our son*.


He made it through. A week in the hospital. And then we took him home, kidneys reconstructed, body healing, future uncertain.


---


But something was off.


He wasn't walking. Wasn't talking. Three, almost four years old, and he was still crawling—but not normal crawling. This weird frog-hop thing that made people look twice. A sign, we'd later learn, of special needs.


Around age two, we did genetic testing. Expensive—thousands of dollars. Insurance fought us. But we needed to know. Needed some explanation for why our son wasn't developing like other kids, why he seemed trapped in his own body, unable to communicate what was going on inside his mind.


The call came.


"Landon is missing chromosome 4P, sections 15.1 through 15.31."


I didn't understand. "Speak English, doc. What does that mean?"


"We don't really know. It's extremely rare. You had better odds of winning the lottery three times over than having this happen."


I thought to myself: *I would have preferred that. Give me the lottery. Don't give this to my son.*


---


The diagnosis opened doors for services—physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, everything available until age three. But it also came with more labels: mild cerebral palsy, autism, intellectual disability. A constellation of challenges that would shape his entire life.


And I felt like I was being punished.


Felt like God—if there was a God, which I doubted—was looking at all the terrible things I'd done and deciding that the appropriate punishment was to give my innocent son a disability. That Landon was paying the price for my sins.


That belief nearly destroyed me. The guilt was crushing. The shame was enormous. The feeling that I'd somehow caused this, that my drinking and drugging and cheating and lying had cosmically infected my son, made him pay for my mistakes.


So I drank about it. Drank about Landon's diagnosis. Drank about my life. Drank about everything.


And I wouldn't stop for another eight years.


---


PART IV: ROCK BOTTOM
*The lowest point before the light breaks through*


---


Chapter 21: The Protective Order Night


December 15, 2012. Snowing. I couldn't stop asking questions about her affair.


And that night, everything I'd built came crashing down.


---


It had been over a year since I found out about Jennie's affair—with someone I knew, someone from her world. The betrayal was enormous. The rage was constant. And I couldn't let it go.


Couldn't stop asking questions. Couldn't stop demanding details. Couldn't stop torturing both of us with this obsessive need to know everything—every moment they'd been together, every lie she'd told, every time she'd looked me in the eye and pretended everything was fine.


The hypocrisy was stunning. I'd had multiple affairs that Jennie didn't know about. Multiple women. Multiple betrayals. But in my sick addict mind, my affairs didn't count. Only hers did.


That night, we were arguing. Snowing outside. Me saying crazy things, her trying to manage the chaos, both of us trapped in this dysfunctional dance we'd been doing for years.


"I feel like I want to kill myself," I said.


It was true. And it was heartbreaking. And it was also abusive—using my suicidality as a weapon, making her responsible for my pain, giving her no way to respond that wouldn't make things worse.


She didn't know what to say. Didn't know where to turn. Was trapped in this marriage with someone who was simultaneously her husband and her tormentor, her love and her nightmare.


---


I left that night. "Fuck it," I thought. "Might as well go get drunk."


Drove around. Got flowers. Got that pretzel bread she liked from Panera. The same cycle I'd done a hundred times: rage, take space, come back with gifts, pretend everything's fine. No pause button. No restraint. No filter. Just pure emotional chaos inflicted on everyone around me.


I got home. There was a letter at the top of the stairs.


*"Going to stay with mom and dad for the night. Love you. See you in the morning."*


Simple. Reasonable. A woman removing herself from a volatile situation to get some space and peace.


I still couldn't handle it.


I flipped out. Drove to her parents' house—she wasn't there. Drove around looking for her. Found her car at Piedmont Visitor Center—not the college hidden up the hill, but the public visitor center visible from the road.


"I want to pick up the kids and take them home," I said.


She went dark. Stopped responding. Started concocting a plan that I wouldn't see coming until it was too late.


---


The next morning came. No sleep. No communication. Jennie was completely dark.


I called her parents. No answer. Drove to their house. The kids were inside with their grandparents. I could see them through the windows. But the grandparents wouldn't answer the door.


It felt like kidnapping. Felt like my children were being held hostage. But there was no protective order yet—nothing legal preventing me from being there, from seeing my own kids.


I drove to Jennie's school. They wouldn't tell me if she was there. I sat in the parking lot making calls, trying to figure out what was happening, spiraling into panic and rage.


The principal called the police. Claimed I was "about to initiate a lockdown." Made me sound dangerous. Made me sound like a threat.


Then my cop buddy called. "Shaun," he said carefully. "They issued a protective order. You can't go home. You can't see the kids. You can't call Jennie."


The world stopped.


First night ever spent intentionally apart out of anger and fear. First night realizing I'd lost everything. First night understanding that the consequences I'd been outrunning for years had finally caught up.


---


March 2014 to August 2015. Over a year. Seventeen months with a protective order in place.


I was kicked out of my childhood home—the house my parents had sold to us, the house where my kids lived, the house I'd destroyed with my own hands. I moved back in with my parents like a teenager, sleeping in Landon's room, living in limbo.


Communication with Jennie had to go through my mother. I made her the mediator, the messenger, the person stuck in the middle of our war. It was fucked up. I see that now. But at the time, I couldn't see anything but my own rage.


When the kids came to visit, I had to leave my parents' house. Drive around the neighborhood. Wait at the cul-de-sac two hundred feet away like some kind of criminal until Jennie's car disappeared down the street. Then I could go back and see my children—supervised visits, scheduled exchanges, every moment monitored.


---


This was the hardest time of my life. Harder than Gage's death. Harder than the decade of darkness. Harder than anything I'd experienced because I was sober—barely, white-knuckling it, hating every minute—and I couldn't numb the pain.


I wanted absolutely nothing to do with sobriety. Loathed being an alcoholic. Hated AA meetings. Hated the steps. Hated the whole idea that I was powerless, that I needed help, that I couldn't do this on my own.


But I stayed sober. Because I thought if I stayed sober long enough, Jennie would take me back. The kids would come home. Everything would return to normal.


I didn't understand yet: there was no going back. There was only through. And I was still fighting reality with everything I had.


March 2014 to August 2015. Over a year. The hardest time of my life.


And I still wasn't done hitting bottom.


---


Chapter 22: Courtrooms


The courtroom. March 2014. The hearing to make the protective order permanent.


The judge who didn't care about facts. The principal who lied to my face. The attorney who didn't speak up or argue or fight for me. This is how I lost everything.


---


My representation was shit. The attorney barely said anything, didn't push back on evidence, didn't challenge the narrative being built against me. Just let it happen. Let them paint me as this dangerous, unstable person who needed to be kept away from his own family.


The evidence they used: email harassment from a fake account I'd created. Messages I'd sent trying to make Jennie and her affair partner uncomfortable, trying to punish them, trying to hurt them the way I'd been hurt. Maybe thirty or forty emails over a year.


They said I was "tracking her to Piedmont." The truth? The Visitor Center is visible from the road. It's not some hidden location—it's literally the public parking area where tourists go. But they made it sound like I was stalking her, hunting her, following her to secret locations.


The punishment didn't fit the crime. I believe that still, even today. What I did was wrong—the harassment, the obsessive questioning, the emotional abuse disguised as hurt. But the consequence—losing my home, my kids, my ability to even call my wife—felt disproportionate.


---


The judge asked Jennie: "Has there been physical violence?"


"Well, no," she said. "I don't have bruises. But he's hurt me deeper—emotionally."


And she was right. Emotional abuse is real. The damage I'd done to her psyche, to her sense of safety, to her ability to trust—that was real and valid and deserved consequences.


But the context mattered too. The Yeardley Love case—UVA lacrosse player killed by her boyfriend—had just happened. The city was still reeling. And protective orders were being given out like free candy. The pendulum had swung hard in the direction of protecting women at all costs, even if it meant railroading men who weren't actually dangerous.


The principal testified. Acted like he didn't know me. Like I was some stranger causing problems. But I'd been to that school plenty of times. Picked up the kids. Dropped off lunch. They knew exactly who I was.


The fact that I'd called that morning looking for Jennie was used against me. Proof of harassment. Proof of instability. Never mind that I was a father trying to find his wife who'd disappeared with his kids.


---


After the hearing, the Guardian ad Litem—the attorney appointed to represent the kids' interests—pulled me aside.


"Yeah," he said quietly. "You're the talk of the town. I heard you got railroaded."


That should have felt validating. Instead, it just made me more bitter. Because if everyone knew I got railroaded, why didn't anyone stop it? Why didn't anyone fight harder for the truth?


It made me real bitter toward the system. Made me question everything I'd believed about justice, about fairness, about how things work when you're a white male with "the most privilege" but the pendulum's overcorrected and suddenly you're the villain no matter what actually happened.


I'm not saying the feminist movement is wrong. I'm not saying men don't abuse women. I'm saying some good men get railroaded too. And I was one of them. Or at least, that's what I believed at the time.


---


I was sleeping in Landon's room at my parents' house. Living out of boxes. Seeing my kids on a supervised schedule like I was some kind of danger to them. And drinking about it—no wait, not drinking. That's the worst part. I was *sober* through all of this.


Sober and miserable. Sober and raging. Sober and completely convinced that everyone was the enemy: Jennie, her parents, the judge, her attorney, her therapist, the principal, the system itself.


They were all the devil. All against me. All determined to destroy me.


I had no idea I was my own worst enemy.


---


My Uncle Doug—my dad's middle brother—died in 2015 of cirrhosis. Liver completely destroyed from decades of drinking. Doctor had told him: "If you drink again, you will die."


He picked up anyway. Because that's what we do. We'd rather die than stop.


And I watched it happen—watched the family disease claim another victim—while I white-knuckled my own sobriety, hating every second, convinced that life without drinking wasn't life at all.


But I stayed sober. Not because I wanted to. Not because I'd found some spiritual awakening or peace. But because I thought if I stayed sober long enough, I could get my family back.


I was furious at Jennie. Furious at her parents. Furious at the judge, her attorney, her therapist. They were all the devil, my enemy, the people who'd taken everything from me.


Had no idea that everything I'd lost, I'd destroyed with my own hands long before that protective order was ever issued.


Had no idea that the real work—the actual healing—hadn't even started yet.


—


Chapter 23: Psych Ward Chronicles


I lost count of how many times I ended up in psych wards. But I remember the sensory deprivation room with no clocks, no windows—just me and my mind at war.


Between 2013 and 2015, I cycled through multiple admissions. Sometimes voluntary—me checking myself in, begging for help, desperate for someone to fix me. Sometimes involuntary—cops or doctors deciding I wasn't safe to the Commonwealth of Virginia or to myself, locking me up whether I wanted it or not.


I felt like a freak. Felt like a monster. Felt like I'd become exactly what I'd always feared: broken beyond repair, dangerous, someone who needed to be contained.


---


The pattern was always the same.


I'd go in trying to get help. Sincere. Desperate. Actually wanting to change. And then they'd take away my freedom—my phone, my clothes, my autonomy—and I'd get angry. The anger would build. I'd feel like a caged animal and go fucking bananas.


It triggered every childhood trauma about not being in control of my body. The oxygen tent. The Cage. The swim coach. The burns. Every time someone else had decided what happened to my body without my permission.


And here I was again: locked up, medicated without consent, told when to eat and sleep and piss, my entire existence controlled by people who didn't know me, didn't understand me, just saw another psychotic drunk who needed to be managed until the insurance ran out.


---


There was this room—I don't know what they officially called it. Complete sensory deprivation. No clocks. No windows. No way to mark time passing. Just white walls and silence and me.


Time dragged forever in there. Torturous doesn't even capture it. Your mind starts playing tricks. Minutes feel like hours. Hours feel like days. You start counting your breaths just to have something to measure, some proof you're still existing in linear time.


It was a really tough experience. I didn't want to keep repeating it. But somehow I did. Several more times. Because when you're that sick, when you're that desperate, you'll take any help you can get even if you know it's going to hurt.


---


The jail experience during my DUI sentence gave me perspective I didn't want.


First weekend, I'm in there bitching about my problems. Poor me, lost my family, life is so unfair, everything's terrible. Just a fountain of self-pity pouring out of me to anyone who'd listen.


This old man—Black dude, looked like he'd lived a thousand hard lives—finally asked: "When you getting out?"


"Monday, I hope."


"How about you?"


"I'm not. Got twenty-five to life."


I shut up. Felt like an idiot. Here I was, doing weekend jail, would be out in a month total, complaining to a man who would die in prison.


Then he said: "But you know what, Shaun? No matter what, there is always, always something you can be grateful for."


That should have landed. Should have changed me. Should have made me realize how much I still had, how much worse things could be.


But I went right back to bitching and complaining.


Because that's what addicts do. We find reasons to be miserable even when we're blessed. We focus on what we've lost instead of what remains. We wallow in self-pity like it's a warm bath instead of the drowning pool it actually is.


---


---
The darkest part of those years was the obsession. The fantasies of revenge. The thoughts that consumed me—making everyone pay, making everyone understand how much they'd hurt me.
I shared some of these thoughts in IOP—intensive outpatient program. The counselors were alarmed enough to intervene. They had my firearms removed from my possession.
At the time, I was furious. How dare they take away my property? How dare they treat me like a criminal when I was just being honest about my thoughts?
Now I understand: they probably saved lives. Including mine. Because when you're in that headspace—that obsessive, rage-fueled, pain-driven spiral—you're capable of things you'd never imagine in your right mind. Having the means removed was an act of grace I couldn't appreciate until years later.
---


I was either locked up or planning to lock myself away forever. Either way, I was done.


But I didn't know God wasn't done with me yet.


Didn't know that hitting bottom in sobriety—that liquor store parking lot, those tears, that choice to walk into the meeting instead of buying the bottle—was going to be the turning point.


Didn't know that Shannon was going to give me a hug that night. That we'd eventually build something beautiful together. That I'd get to watch my kids grow up. That Jennie and I would learn to co-parent with love instead of rage.


I didn't know any of that. All I knew was pain.


But sometimes pain is the chisel God uses to crack us open.


And I was about to crack wide.


---


Chapter 24: The Ranch


May 2014. Right before Landon's birthday. Mother's Day weekend.


I was headed to Nunnelly, Tennessee—The Ranch—to finally face what happened when I was six years old.


---


The Ranch sits about forty to forty-five minutes outside Nashville. World-renowned for treating sex and love addiction, substance abuse, childhood abuse. They specialize in the things most people don't want to talk about, the wounds that live in the body and come out sideways in addiction, in violence, in self-destruction.


I was there for dual diagnosis. Still had substance abuse issues—I'd been sober less than eighteen months, white-knuckling the whole time. But primarily, I was there for childhood trauma. The swim coach. The Cage. All the things I'd never processed, never faced, never dealt with.


My parents drove me. Twelve to fourteen hour drive from Virginia, split over two nights. Their son, thirty-something years old, being driven to rehab like a teenager. The shame was crushing. The fear was enormous.


---


Deerfield House. All guys. Brand new facility when I arrived. Beautiful house, actually—you could still smell the fresh construction.


The week before I got there, a female sex addict had snuck out of her house at night, crossed campus to the guys' sex addict house, snuck in, and fucked all the guys. Just chaos. Complete insanity.


And guys were still getting drugs shipped to them by their wives. Actual narcotics, mailed into rehab, because addiction doesn't stop just because you're in treatment. It finds a way.


But for the guys who were serious—who really wanted to do the work—it was transformative.


---


Daily schedule: Wake up, fix breakfast, group check-ins where we'd share physically, emotionally, mentally, spiritually. Then lessons. Then more groups. Breaks for lunch. Sometimes gym. Sometimes yoga. Sometimes sweat lodges—Native American traditions, spiritual experiences in heat and darkness.


We made dream catchers. Arts and crafts. Things that made me feel ridiculous at first but actually opened something up inside me. Using my hands to create instead of destroy. Slowing down. Being gentle with myself.


And then there was Build-A-Bear.


---


They bused us into Nashville. Took us to the mall. A bunch of grown men in a Build-A-Bear Workshop doing inner child work.


Before we went, they had me do this exercise: write a letter to my younger self with my non-dominant hand. My left hand.


The result was absolutely amazing.


Using my non-dominant hand forced me to slow down. The physical act of writing became harder, more deliberate. And something about that slowness—that difficulty—cracked me open.


I was able to write things to my younger self that were vulnerable. Gentle. Loving. Things I would never say with my right hand, with my normal speed, with my normal defenses up.


I told six-year-old Shaun: *It's not your fault. You didn't do anything wrong. You deserved to be protected. I'm sorry no one protected you.*


I cried writing it. Actually sobbed in that group room while other men did the same exercise, all of us breaking open together.


---


At Build-A-Bear, I chose a white teddy bear. Basic. Simple. Pure.


I put a Superman cape on him. And I named him PURE.


P.U.R.E.


For me, it represented the child I was before the loss of innocence. Before the swim coach. Before I learned that adults could hurt you. Before I started building armor to survive.


Pure.


And the Superman cape? That was the protector. The adult I needed to become for that child. The functional, healthy adult who could keep him safe, who could parent him the way he should have been parented, who could finally give him what he'd always needed: safety, love, acceptance.


My therapist said: "We have to get a functional adult onboard. Because when we grow up with dysfunction and trauma, we're still reacting to life as that broken child. We don't know any other way. We have to rewire ourselves."


That bear—PURE—became my tangible reminder. I still have him. Kept him through everything that came after.


---


Then came EMDR therapy. Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing. Trauma therapy that works by having you track movements with your eyes while recalling traumatic memories, somehow allowing your brain to reprocess them differently.


Before EMDR, my childhood trauma was like a slideshow. Individual frames. Click. Next image. Click. Next image. Compartmentalized. Manageable. Controlled.


EMDR turned that slideshow into a movie reel.


Cracked open the compartments. Let the memories flood through. And I had meltdown after meltdown, week after week, as my brain finally processed what had happened to me instead of just storing it away.


---


Later that week, we did psychodrama therapy in the attic. Third floor, unfinished space. Dark. You could see the rafters, the wooden trusses. Felt like a dungeon.


They gave me a nerf bat. Told me to beat the shit out of a pillow. Scream. Let it all out. Rage at my predator, at my powerlessness, at everything stolen from me.


I had a really hard time with it.


Because I had this weird feeling—this need to protect my predator. The swim coach. The person who violated me.


That's what trauma does. It makes you protect the people who hurt you. Makes you minimize what happened. Makes you think somehow you're responsible for keeping their secret, for not making it worse, for being the good victim who doesn't cause problems.


Even in that attic, even with the nerf bat in my hands, even with permission to rage—I couldn't fully let go.


Because protecting my predator had kept me safe for thirty years. And letting go of that protection meant facing the full weight of what had been done to me.


---


I went to The Ranch thinking I'd fix myself so Jennie would take me back.


I left knowing I'd never be the same.


The trauma work cracked me open in ways I couldn't have anticipated. Started a healing process that's still ongoing today. Gave me tools I didn't know I needed.


But I still had no idea how much further I'd have to fall before I could finally rise.


---


Chapter 25: The Serpent Vision


The first night after EMDR, something happened that I still can't explain.


Some call it out-of-body. Some call it divine. I call it the moment I knew I'd never be the same.


---


My brain was fried from the trauma work. That slideshow turning into a movie reel—processing decades of buried pain in a single afternoon—had left me lightheaded, disoriented, barely able to walk straight.


I went to bed early. Dozing off to sleep in that Deerfield House bedroom, exhausted beyond anything I'd ever felt.


And then I felt something wake me up.


---


I felt myself rising.


Not metaphorically. Literally. I could feel my consciousness lifting up from my body, separating from the physical form lying in that bed.


I turned around—rolled my neck, looked over my shoulder—and saw my body still there. Physical. Solid. But I was outside of it. Like a translucent hologram. A spirit. My aura, maybe, if you believe in that sort of thing.


It felt realer than any dream. I've had vivid dreams. Lucid dreams. Dreams that felt so real I woke up confused about what was real and what wasn't.


This was different.


I wasn't dreaming. I wasn't hallucinating—and I know what hallucinations are, I've done enough hallucinogens to know the difference. This was something else entirely.


---


While I hovered there—out of body, looking at my physical form—I had a vision.


A snake.


Glowing black with shimmering golden glitter, like someone had taken darkness and made it beautiful, made it holy.


The snake coiled around the room. Moved with intention. Purposeful. Not threatening. Just... present. Powerful. Ancient.


And then it came toward me.


Up my spine. I could feel it—actually feel it—going up the back of my spine, vertebra by vertebra, until its head merged with mine. Became one with me. Our consciousnesses blending, two becoming one, transformation happening at a cellular level.


I woke up gasping. Back in my body. Heart pounding. No idea what the fuck had just happened.


---


The next morning in group, I shared casually during check-in.


"I had a weird fucking night," I said. "Here's what happened."


I described the out-of-body experience. The snake. The merging. Expecting people to think I was crazy, that the trauma work had broken something in my brain.


Instead, my counselor's face went serious.


"Come with me," he said.


---


He took me to another building. To meet a woman who did energy work—something I was pretty closed-minded about at the time. I didn't believe in Eastern philosophy much. Thought it was all woo-woo nonsense for people who couldn't handle reality.


But my life was ruined. I was over. What did I have to lose?


The woman sat me down and explained what my vision meant.


"Snakes in dreams and visions," she said, "represent renewal. Transformation. Shedding old skin. They appear when you're ready to become someone new."


It aligned perfectly with the 12&12 definition of spiritual experience. Not the burning bush, white light, angels singing version. But the educational variety—what they call "rearrangement of old ideas, new way of thinking, reprogramming."


Carl Jung had said something similar to Roland Hazard—one of the alcoholics who would help create AA. Jung said Hazard needed "vital spiritual experiences" to recover. That psychology and medicine alone wouldn't be enough. He needed something bigger.


Bill Wilson later wrote Jung a letter thanking him for that conversation with Hazard. Because it led to the spiritual foundation of AA. The idea that you need a power greater than yourself to recover.


Jung wrote back saying he'd been careful in those days about what he said. He was a man of science, a psychologist. He couldn't just tell patients to "find God" without some evidence-based framework.


But he believed it. Believed transformation required something transcendent. Something beyond the purely rational.


---


The woman explained: "You don't need white light or burning bushes. Spiritual experiences can be slow, subtle, educational. A gradual awakening instead of a lightning bolt."


But mine felt like a lightning bolt.


That snake—that glowing, golden-black serpent merging with my spine—felt like God saying: *I'm remaking you. Shedding your old skin. Making you new.*


I didn't need to understand it completely. Didn't need to explain it scientifically. Didn't need to convince anyone it was real.


I just needed to know: something bigger than me was at work.


And I was being remade from the inside out.


---


Chapter 26: The Chaplain Question


November 2014. Six months after The Ranch. Bilateral pneumonia. 106.5 fever.


"Mr. Critzer, do you want to speak with a chaplain?"


That's when you know you're dying.


---


I felt like shit. But I didn't know I was dying. Didn't understand that when a doctor asks if you want to speak with a chaplain, it means they've done everything medical science can do and now it's time to prepare your soul for what comes next.


I'd been smoking non-stop. Chain-smoking, actually—stress and anger and the year of the protective order grinding me down to nothing. When you can't drink or drug anymore, you find other ways to slowly kill yourself. Cigarettes felt harmless by comparison. Just tobacco. Legal. Socially acceptable self-destruction.


The flu hit me hard. Knocked me flat. But I didn't go to the doctor right away because addicts don't. We tough things out. We pretend we're fine even when we're not. We avoid medical professionals who might ask uncomfortable questions or judge our life choices or tell us truths we don't want to hear.


By the time I finally went in, the flu had turned into bilateral pneumonia. Both lungs. Fluid filling the spaces where air should be. My body drowning from the inside out.


---


The fever climbed. 103. 104. 105. They gave me the strongest antibiotics they had. Pumped me full of fever reducers. Packed me in ice. Nothing worked.


106.5 degrees.


That's the temperature where your brain starts cooking. Where proteins denature. Where the delicate machinery of consciousness begins to malfunction. Where the line between life and death gets real thin real fast.


The doctor's face was grave when he came in. Professional distance cracking just enough for me to see the concern underneath.


"Mr. Critzer," he said carefully. "We're giving you the strongest things we can, but we can't get your temperature down."


A pause. The kind of pause that means bad news is coming.


"Do you want to speak with a chaplain?"


I stared at him. "What?"


"A chaplain. Hospital clergy. Someone to... talk with. About spiritual matters."


It took me a moment to understand. *Oh. He thinks I'm dying. He's asking if I want last rites. Or the Protestant equivalent. Whatever you call it when they're preparing you to meet your maker.*


---


The memory of Gage hit me like a freight train.


June 2003. Norfolk General Hospital. My brother lying there paralyzed from the neck down, machines breathing for him, fever climbing higher and higher until his brain couldn't take it anymore.


107 degrees. Maybe 108. The point where the body just gives up. Where the fight ends not because you chose to surrender but because biology has limits that even the strongest will can't overcome.


They'd registered Gage as brain dead. His mom made the decision to pull the plug. I was the only non-family pallbearer at his funeral. Visited his grave every single day for months afterward, never once sober, trying to drink away the image of him in that hospital bed—larger than life reduced to a body attached to machines.


And now here I was. Same fever. Same hospital system. Same choice point between life and death.


The universe has a sick sense of humor sometimes.


---


"I'm not ready," I heard myself say.


The doctor nodded. "We're going to keep trying. But I wanted you to know... the situation is serious."


"I understand."


But I didn't. Not really. I felt like hell but I didn't feel like I was dying. Didn't feel like I was standing at the edge of the same cliff that had taken Gage. The fever was making everything fuzzy, distant, like I was experiencing my life from underwater.


They kept working. More medications. More interventions. More attempts to bring my temperature down before my brain melted.


And somewhere in that fever dream, I made a choice.


*I want to live.*


Not because life was particularly good at that moment. The year of the protective order had been absolute hell. I was the angriest recovering alcoholic you'd ever meet—not recovered whatsoever, just dry and miserable. White-knuckling sobriety while hating every second of it.


I'd heard a speaker at a conference say once: "I'm grateful for every drop I consumed. Because it preserved just enough sanity in me that I didn't kill myself before I found the rooms of AA."


That resonated. Because if I'd been sober through all my trauma—through Gage's death, through Jax's death, through Landon's diagnosis, through the protective order—I don't think I would have survived it. The alcohol and drugs, as destructive as they were, gave me just enough numbness to keep breathing. Just enough distance from reality to not walk into traffic or eat a gun.


First it was magic. Then it was medicinal. Then it was misery.


But it kept me alive long enough to find another way.


---


The fever finally broke. 106.5 down to 105. Down to 104. Still dangerous but no longer actively cooking my brain. The antibiotics caught up. The bilateral pneumonia started to respond to treatment.


I wasn't going to die. Not today.


I chose to live. But I still didn't know how.


Didn't know what living sober actually looked like when you weren't white-knuckling it. Didn't know how to be happy without substances. Didn't know how to process emotions without numbing them. Didn't know how to be present in my own life instead of constantly running from it.


The year of the protective order continued. The absolute hell of being separated from my kids, living in my parents' house, communicating with Jennie through my mother like we were children who couldn't be trusted to speak directly.


I was progressively getting worse—not drinking, but still deteriorating. Because sobriety without recovery is just being a sober miserable drunk. And I was the soberest, most miserable drunk you'd ever meet.


---


What I didn't understand yet was that being dry wasn't the same as being recovered.


I had honesty—I knew I was an alcoholic. Could admit it out loud in meetings. Could say the words: "Hi, I'm Shaun and I'm an alcoholic."


But I didn't have willingness. Didn't truly want to change, to do the work, to surrender my will to something greater than myself. I was just enduring sobriety like a prison sentence, waiting for it to be over, waiting for Jennie to take me back so I could go back to being the person I'd always been.


And I didn't have open-mindedness. Was closed off to any suggestion that I might be the problem, that my thinking was the issue, that I needed to fundamentally change who I was at my core.


Without all three—honesty, willingness, open-mindedness—I was just a sober miserable drunk. Going through the motions. Showing up to meetings but not really working the program. Saying the words but not believing them.


The three indispensables, they call them in AA. And I only had one out of three.


---


But I chose to live. In that hospital bed with the chaplain question hanging in the air, I chose to live.


Didn't know why. Didn't know how. Didn't know what I was living for. But something in me—some stubborn, determined, unwilling-to-quit part—said *not yet. Not like this. Not the same way Gage died.*


Maybe it was pride. Maybe it was fear. Maybe it was love for my kids even though I couldn't see them. Maybe it was some small spark of hope that things could get better even though I had no evidence to support that belief.


Or maybe it was grace. God reaching down and saying *I'm not done with you yet. I know you don't believe in me. I know you're angry and hurt and lost. But I'm not done.*


I chose to live.


But I still didn't know how to live.


That lesson was still coming.


---


Chapter 27: December 2009 - The First White Chip


December 5, 2009. I was dying, hallucinating, coughing up blood. My childhood friend Sam drove four hours from Charlotte to save my life. That night, I got my first white chip from the same man who sold me my last bottle.


---


The delirium tremens hit me like a freight train.


DTs—that's what they call it when your body revolts against the sudden absence of alcohol. When your nervous system, so accustomed to being suppressed by booze, suddenly fires on all cylinders with nothing to regulate it. Seizures, hallucinations, tremors so violent you can't hold a glass of water. Death, if you're unlucky.


I was coughing up blood. Shaking so hard my teeth rattled. Hallucinating things that weren't there—shadows moving in corners, voices whispering things I couldn't quite make out, the walls breathing like they were alive.


I called Sam. My childhood friend, the guy I'd grown up with, who'd since become a professional race car driver. Living down in Charlotte. Four hours away.


"Sam, I need help."


He took one look at me when he arrived and his eyes bugged out. I must have looked like something from a horror movie—wild-eyed, skeletal, shaking, bleeding. The physical manifestation of what alcohol withdrawal does when you've been drinking the way I'd been drinking.


"Jesus Christ, Shaun."


I had prescriptions for diazepam and Ativan—benzos to help with the detox. Standard protocol for someone coming off as much alcohol as I'd been consuming. The doctors knew what they were doing. They'd given me enough to taper down safely.


But that first night was hell anyway.


I took five or six benzos. Maybe more. Lost count. And I was still shaking. Still hallucinating. Still feeling like my body was coming apart at the seams.


"My hands," I said to Sam, staring at them. "They're disappearing."


"What?"


"Look. They're turning into leaves. Blowing away."


Sam looked at my hands. They were right there, solid, attached to my wrists like they'd always been. But in my mind, they were disintegrating—flesh becoming foliage, fingers becoming wind.


"You're hallucinating, man. Your hands are fine."


But they didn't feel fine. They felt like they belonged to someone else. Like I was watching myself from outside my body, witnessing my own dissolution.


---


Sam had a solution I wouldn't have thought of on my own.


"Pixie Sticks," he said.


"What?"


"Sugar. Your blood sugar's probably in the basement. Alcohol fucks with that. You need sugar, fast."


He ran to the store, came back with bags of Pixie Sticks—those paper tubes of pure sugar that kids pour directly into their mouths. And I started pounding them. Spoonfuls of sugar straight from the bag when the Pixie Sticks ran out.


It helped. Not completely—the DTs don't just disappear because you ate some sugar. But it took the edge off. Made the hallucinations less intense. Brought me back from the brink just enough to keep breathing.


---


Earlier that day, before Sam arrived, I'd made one last trip to the ABC store.


The same store I'd been going to for years. The same employees who knew my name, knew my order, knew exactly how much Jim Beam I went through in a week. They probably had a betting pool on when I'd finally drink myself to death.


I bought a half-gallon. My usual. The big bottle with the handle, the one that would last me a few days if I was being conservative, a day if I wasn't.


It was my last bottle. I didn't know it at the time. But it was.


That night, after the sugar and the benzos and the shaking started to subside enough for me to function, Sam looked at me seriously.


"You need to go to a meeting."


"What?"


"AA. Alcoholics Anonymous. There's one tonight. We're going."


I wanted to argue. Wanted to say I could do this on my own, that I didn't need some church basement full of broken people telling me about their problems, that I was different, special, capable of white-knuckling this thing by myself.


But I was too tired to fight. Too sick to argue. Too scared of dying to resist.


So we went.


---


The meeting was in a church basement. Fluorescent lights, folding chairs, bad coffee in styrofoam cups, that particular smell that all church basements seem to have—must and old carpet and something vaguely institutional.


People were already there when we walked in. Sitting in a circle, talking quietly, that easy familiarity that comes from shared experience. They looked up when we entered. Smiled. Nodded. Made space.


"Hi, I'm John," one guy said, extending his hand. "Welcome."


I shook it. Felt the firm grip, the genuine warmth. These people didn't know me. Didn't know what I'd done, how far I'd fallen, how much I'd destroyed. And they were welcoming me anyway.


The meeting started. Someone read something called "How It Works"—the preamble, they called it. Basic instructions for staying sober. Twelve steps. Twelve traditions. The foundation of AA.


Then people started sharing. One by one, going around the circle. Talking about their day, their struggles, their victories, their fears. Raw honesty. Vulnerability I wasn't used to seeing in men. Emotions I'd spent my entire life trying to avoid.


When it got to me, I froze.


"Hi," I managed. "I'm Shaun. And I'm... I'm an alcoholic."


The words felt foreign. Like admitting I was something I'd spent years pretending I wasn't. Like pulling back the curtain on a performance I'd been giving my entire adult life.


"Hi, Shaun," everyone said in unison.


That simple greeting—that acknowledgment—hit me harder than I expected. These strangers, saying my name, accepting me exactly as I was in that moment. No judgment. No condemnation. Just recognition.


"This is my first meeting," I continued. My voice was shaking. "I almost died today. I don't... I don't know what to do."


An older man across the circle spoke up. "You're in the right place."


Then something surreal happened.


The guy who'd been sitting quietly to my left stood up. "I'd like to give Shaun his newcomer chip," he said.


He walked to the front, grabbed a white chip—a little plastic coin with "24 hours" stamped on it—and brought it back to me.


And I recognized him.


It was the manager from the ABC store. The same man who'd sold me that half-gallon of Jim Beam earlier that day. The same man who'd been taking my money for years, watching me slowly kill myself one bottle at a time.


"Oh shit," I thought. "Group discounts or something?"


But he wasn't mocking me. Wasn't judging me. He handed me that white chip with genuine compassion in his eyes, like he understood exactly what it meant to be sitting in that chair, desperate and broken and finally willing to admit defeat.


The same man who sold me my last bottle gave me my first chip.


The universe has a sick sense of humor sometimes. But maybe it also has a sense of poetry.


---


I went to meetings for about a month after that. Kept going, kept being there, kept collecting those 24-hour chips and then seven-day chips and eventually a 30-day chip.


People told me it would get better. That if I just kept coming back, worked the steps, got a sponsor, did the program, my life would transform. That I'd find peace, purpose, a way to live that didn't require destroying myself.


I wanted to believe them. Wanted to think that showing up to church basements and drinking bad coffee and admitting I was powerless would somehow fix everything that was broken inside me.


But then one night, I sat next to someone at a meeting who absolutely reeked of Jim Beam.


The smell hit me like a physical force. That distinctive bourbon smell—sweet and sharp and medicinal all at once. The smell of my drink. The smell of my addiction. The smell of everything I was trying to escape.


"What the fuck is this?" I thought. "Where people learn to drink better?"


The whole thing suddenly felt pointless. These people weren't sober—or if they were, they were white-knuckling it just like me. Miserable. Struggling. Barely hanging on. Trading one addiction for another, alcohol for meetings, dependence on substances for dependence on AA.


"Fuck this," I decided. "I'll do it on my own."


And I walked out.


---


December 2009 to June 2012. Two and a half years. I didn't drink at all during that time.


I was proud of that. Thought I'd beaten it. Thought I'd proven I could do it on my own, without meetings, without sponsors, without any of that "higher power" bullshit they kept talking about in AA.


But I still did every other drug under the sun.


Cocaine. Pills. Weed. Anything except alcohol. Convinced myself that because I wasn't drinking, I was sober. That I'd solved the problem. That I was in recovery.


I wasn't. I was still a dry drunk. Still miserable. Still running from myself. Still using substances to numb emotions I didn't know how to process. Just trading one poison for another.


And in June 2012, I picked up my first drink in two and a half years.


Within weeks, I was back to drinking more than I ever had before.


They say in AA: "Your disease is out in the parking lot doing push-ups, getting stronger while you're in here getting weaker."


They're right.


I'd stayed away from alcohol for two and a half years. But I hadn't done any work on myself. Hadn't addressed the underlying issues that made me drink in the first place. Hadn't learned how to live life on life's terms without numbing myself into oblivion.


So when I picked up again, all that discipline, all that white-knuckling, all that time not drinking—it meant nothing.


The disease was stronger than ever. And I was weaker than I'd ever been.


---


The three indispensables—honesty, open-mindedness, and willingness—I finally had all three. Not just one or two like before. All three. And that made all the difference.


And that's a recipe for relapse. Every single time.


---


Chapter 28: January 1, 2013 - The Real Beginning


December 31, 2012. I bought a fifth of Jim Beam and put it in my freezer. My sobriety date is January 1, 2013. But I didn't drink that bottle for weeks.


---


People think sobriety starts with a dramatic moment. Rock bottom. Moment of clarity. The final drink that finally breaks you.


Mine started with a bottle in my freezer that I couldn't bring myself to open.


December 31, 2012. New Year's Eve. I was still in the year of the protective order—still separated from Jennie, still living at my parents' house, still unable to see my kids without elaborate custody arrangements and supervised exchanges.


I went to the ABC store. Same store. Same routine. Bought a fifth of Jim Beam like I'd done a thousand times before.


But I didn't drink it that night.


I put it in the freezer. Told myself I'd drink it tomorrow. Or the next day. Or whenever I really needed it. Having it there felt safer than not having it. Like an insurance policy against the chaos in my head.


Days passed. Then a week. Then two weeks. That bottle sat in my freezer, untouched, like a loaded gun I kept meaning to use but never quite picked up.


I was going to meetings again. Not because I wanted to. Not because I believed in it. But because I had nothing else. No home. No family. No purpose. Just meetings and work and the slow grinding misery of being sober without being recovered.


But something was different this time.


---


January 1, 2013. That's my sobriety date. Thirteen years ago now, as I write this.


But I didn't drink that fifth of Jim Beam until weeks after that date.


Eventually I poured it down the drain. Watched that amber liquid swirl away, taking with it whatever illusion of control I'd been clinging to. The belief that I could manage this on my own. The fantasy that someday I'd be able to drink normally, like other people, without destroying everything I touched.


I'd tried it my way for two and a half years—staying away from alcohol but still doing other drugs, still white-knuckling sobriety, still refusing to actually work a program or get a sponsor or do any real recovery work.


And it had failed. Spectacularly. I'd relapsed harder than ever. Lost even more. Hurt even more people.


So on January 1, 2013, I made a different choice.


I didn't just stop drinking. I started recovering.


Got a sponsor. Started working the steps. Started going to meetings not because I had to but because I needed to. Started being honest—actually honest—about what I'd done, who I'd hurt, how far I'd fallen.


Started believing, slowly, that maybe there was a way to live that didn't require numbing myself into oblivion. That maybe other people had found it. And maybe—just maybe—I could too.


---


This is where the real story begins. Not with my first drink or my first drug or my first competition or my first affair. Not with the oxygen tent or the swim coach or Jax or Gage or Landon's diagnosis.


But here. January 1, 2013. The day I finally put both feet in. The day I stopped trying to do it my way and started being willing to try someone else's way.


The day I chose recovery instead of just sobriety.


The crooked lines were about to start straightening out. But I couldn't see that yet. All I could see was today. And today, I wasn't drinking.


And somehow, impossibly, that was enough.


---


Chapter 29: Shannon - Meeting Her


Late 2013. Thursday night meeting. She walked in wearing a sundress, and I forgot how to breathe.


---


I'd been going to meetings for about eleven months by this point. January 1, 2013 was my sobriety date, and I was actually working a program this time—sponsor, steps, service work, the whole thing. Not just white-knuckling it like before.


But I was miserable.


I was living at my parents' house, sleeping in Landon's room like a teenager who'd fucked up his life beyond repair. The protective order hadn't happened yet, but my marriage was already dead. I was doing custody exchanges, trying to hold it together, failing at everything except not drinking.


Still watching my marriage die in slow motion. Still trying to be a dad while everything around me was falling apart.


I was sober. But I wasn't happy. Wasn't even close to happy.


And then Shannon walked into that Thursday night meeting.


---


She was gorgeous. Way out of my league—and I'm not saying that for effect, I genuinely believed it. Long dark hair, bright smile, the kind of woman who turns heads when she walks into a room. My stomach did this thing it hadn't done since high school. Actual butterflies. I was a grown man with a destroyed life, and I had butterflies like a teenager.


But it wasn't just that she was attractive. It was something else. Something in the way she carried herself. Confident but not arrogant. Present. Alive. Like she'd been through hell and come out the other side actually better for it.


She sat down a few chairs away. Smiled at people. Said hi to folks who clearly knew her already. She'd been coming to this meeting for a while, I realized. I just hadn't noticed because I'd been so wrapped up in my own misery that I barely noticed anything.


The meeting started. People shared. I barely heard a word anyone said because I was trying not to obviously stare at this woman while also trying to figure out how to maybe talk to her without coming across as a creep.


---


After the meeting ended, people milled around. That after-meeting socializing that's half the point of going—the coffee and conversation, the connections that keep you coming back.


I saw Shannon talking with a group of women. Laughing at something someone said. That laugh—God, that laugh. It lit up her whole face.


I wanted to talk to her. Wanted to introduce myself, say something clever, make her laugh like that. But I had no idea what to say. I was thirty-four years old, separated from my wife, living with my parents, barely holding my life together. What did I have to offer someone like her?


So I just stood there like an idiot, pretending to be interested in my coffee.


Then she walked over.


"Hi," she said. "I'm Shannon."


"Shaun," I managed. My brain had temporarily forgotten how language worked.


"I've seen you here before. You share really honestly. I appreciate that."


"Oh. Thanks. Yeah. I try to... you know. Be honest."


Smooth, Shaun. Real smooth.


But she didn't seem to mind my awkwardness. Just kept smiling, kept the conversation going, asked me questions about how long I'd been sober, what brought me to the rooms, the usual meeting small talk.


I learned she'd gotten sober recently—November 2013. That she had a son—Brody, just turned six. That she'd been through her own hell with addiction and come out the other side. That she was working a program, had a sponsor, was actually doing the work instead of just showing up.


We talked for maybe ten minutes. Felt like ten seconds. And when she said she had to go pick up her son from her parents' house, I felt this weird disappointment—like something good was ending before it had really begun.


"See you next week?" she said.


"Yeah. Definitely."


She smiled one more time and walked out.


I stood there in that church basement, holding my shitty coffee, feeling something I hadn't felt in years.


Hope.


---


The next Thursday, I showed up early. Told myself it wasn't because of Shannon. Told myself I was just being a good AA member, getting there in time to help set up chairs, make coffee, be of service.


But when she walked in and her face lit up seeing me there, I knew I'd been lying to myself.


For the next year, I was too wrapped up in my own chaos to do much about it. The protective order came in March 2014. I went to The Ranch. Came back. Started putting my life back together piece by piece. Shannon was there at meetings the whole time—a familiar face, a warm smile, someone I looked forward to seeing even when I was too messed up to do anything about it.
By the summer of 2014, I started actually talking to her. Not just nodding hello—real conversations. Exchanging pleasantries that turned into longer exchanges. Learning her story in pieces.

We started talking more. Not just after meetings but before them too. Sitting together during shares. Walking out to the parking lot together afterward, extending those conversations as long as we could before real life pulled us back.


I learned more about her story. The addiction that had nearly killed her. The relationship that had ended. The struggle of being a single mom trying to stay sober while raising a kid. The courage it took to keep showing up, keep working the program, keep believing life could be different.


And I told her mine. Not everything—not yet. But enough. The drinking, the protective order, the kids I couldn't see, the marriage that was ending even though we were still technically married. The feeling that I'd destroyed everything good in my life and was now just trying to figure out how to survive the wreckage.


She didn't judge. Didn't run. Just listened. Nodded. Said things like "I get it" and "That sounds really hard" and "You're exactly where you're supposed to be."


---


It took me months to work up the courage to ask her out.


Not because I wasn't interested. I was interested from day one. But because I was still married—separated, sure, but married. Still had a protective order against me. Still living with my parents. Still barely holding my shit together.


What business did I have asking this woman—this beautiful, sober, emotionally healthy woman with her life together—on a date?


But my sponsor had different advice.


"The divorce is finalized," he said. "You're working a program. You're being honest about where you are. If she's interested, let her make that choice. Don't decide for her."


So one Thursday night, after we'd been talking for months, after I'd built up enough courage to actually ask without my voice shaking, I said:


"Would you want to get coffee sometime? Like, not meeting coffee. Actual coffee. Just us."


She smiled. That smile that made me forget how to breathe.


"I'd like that."


---


Our first date was August 1, 2015. Starbucks. Nothing fancy. Just coffee and conversation in a neutral public place where we could talk without the context of a meeting surrounding us.


I was nervous as hell. Showed up early. Ordered my coffee. Sat there watching the door like a teenager waiting for prom.


When she walked in, I stood up automatically. Some reflex from growing up Southern, from being taught manners, from wanting to show respect even though my palms were sweating and my heart was racing.


We talked for two hours.


About everything. Her childhood, my childhood. Her son, my kids. Her addiction, my addiction. The things we'd lost, the things we'd found. The dreams we had for the future, the fears we had about whether we'd ever be truly okay.

Chapter 30: The Remarriage


November 2015. I married Shannon on a perfect fall day in Virginia. Two and a half years sober. Finally divorced from Jennie. Finally ready to do it right.

The divorce from Jennie was finalized in August 2015. Over a year and a half after the protective order was put in place. Three and a half years after the night everything exploded.


The legal end of a marriage that had been dead long before the paperwork made it official.


I felt relief more than sadness. Not because I didn't care about Jennie—I did, in that complicated way you care about someone you've hurt and been hurt by, someone you've shared a life with even when that life became unbearable. But because it meant we could both finally move forward instead of being trapped in this legal limbo where we were married but separated, tied together but unable to touch.


The protective order was dropped as part of the divorce settlement. I could finally be in the same room as Jennie without violating a court order. Could finally do custody exchanges directly instead of through my mother. Could finally start the long, slow process of learning how to co-parent instead of just surviving the wreckage of our failed marriage.


Shannon had been patient through all of it. Never pressured me. Never demanded I hurry up the divorce process. Just supported me while I navigated the legal system, the emotions, the grief of watching my first marriage officially end even though it had ended in my heart years before.


"Take your time," she'd say. "I'm not going anywhere."


And she meant it.


---


I proposed to Shannon in October 2015. One month after the divorce was finalized.


Some people thought it was too fast. My parents had concerns—"Are you sure you're ready? Are you sure you're not just trying to replace what you lost?"


But I knew. I knew Shannon was different. Knew what we had was different. Knew I was different.


With Jennie, I'd been performing. With Shannon, I was present.


With Jennie, I'd hidden who I really was. With Shannon, I'd shown her everything—the addiction, the affairs, the protective order, the absolute worst of who I'd been—and she'd chosen me anyway.


With Jennie, I'd put her on a pedestal and then resented her for being there. With Shannon, we were equals. Two people in recovery, two people working programs, two people committed to being honest even when honesty was hard.

"I don't have much to offer you except my sobriety and my honesty. But if you'll have me, I promise to show up every day and be the man you deserve."


She said yes before I finished talking.

We got married on November 14, 2015. Two and a half years after my sobriety date. Two months after my divorce was finalized. On a perfect fall day in Virginia when the leaves were changing and the air was crisp and everything felt new.


It was a small ceremony. Family and close friends. People from the program. The community that had supported both of us through our recovery.

Brody was there too. Shannon's son, now eight years old, who'd become part of my life in ways I never expected. He stood with us during the ceremony. Part of the family we were creating.

Jennie wasn't there, obviously. But her absence felt less like a wound and more like... acceptance. This chapter was ending. A new one was beginning. And we were both learning how to let go of what was and embrace what could be.

Shannon walked down the aisle in a simple white dress. Not a wedding gown—we were both on second marriages, both had kids, both knew this wasn't about the fairy tale. This was about commitment. About choosing each other every day. About building a life in recovery together.


When she reached me, she was crying. Happy tears. The kind that come from joy instead of pain.


I was crying too.


My sponsor stood as one of my groomsmen. The man who'd helped me work the steps, who'd answered my calls at 2 AM when I wanted to drink, who'd told me the truth even when I didn't want to hear it. He smiled at me with this look that said: *See? This is what happens when you do the work.*

And when the officiant said, "You may kiss your bride," I kissed her like it was the first day of the rest of my life.


Because it was.

Full circle. But different. Not returning to where I'd been, but building something new on top of the foundation of everything I'd learned.

Marriage to Shannon was different from day one.

It was hard sometimes. Being that honest. Being that vulnerable. Letting someone see you when you're not at your best.

I didn't have to pretend anymore. Didn't have to perform. Didn't have to be the perfect husband or the guy who had it all together.

Chapter 31: Co-Parenting with Jennie


The coffee shop. 2016. Sitting across from my ex-wife, discussing our son's challenges. No lawyers. No mediators. No protective orders. Just two parents trying to do right by their kids.


---


Co-parenting with Jennie after the divorce was... complicated.


We had years of wounds between us. Years of lies and betrayals and fights and court battles. The protective order. The custody disputes. The divorce proceedings where we'd each tried to prove the other was the worse parent.


And now we were supposed to just... work together? Be civil? Make joint decisions about our kids' lives?


It felt impossible at first.


---


The early custody exchanges were brutal. Even after the protective order was dropped, even after we were legally allowed to be in the same space, we could barely look at each other.


I'd pull up to her parents' house to pick up the kids. She'd have them ready at the door, would barely say two words to me. "They're ready. Landon's bag has his medication. Cameron has homework due Monday."


Transactional. Clinical. The absolute minimum communication required to transfer our children from one parent to the other.


I'd leave feeling angry. Feeling like she was still punishing me. Feeling like we'd never be able to move past what had happened between us.


My sponsor had different perspective.


"Of course she can barely look at you," he said. "You destroyed her trust. Cheated on her. Put her through hell. And then when she finally had an affair too, you tortured her about it. Made her life miserable. Got a protective order put on you. She has every right to be angry."


"I know. But I'm different now. I'm sober. I'm in recovery. I'm trying."


"You're trying. That's good. But her healing isn't about you. She gets to take whatever time she needs to feel safe around you again. Your job is to show up consistently, be reliable, prove through actions—not words—that you've changed. That takes time."


He was right. I knew he was right. But it was still hard.


---


Slowly—over months, then years—things started to shift.


I kept showing up. Kept being on time for pickups and drop-offs. Kept following through on what I said I'd do. Kept being sober, stable, present.


When Landon had issues at school, I'd text Jennie: "I heard about what happened. What can I do to help?"


When Cameron had a soccer game, I'd show up. Even if Jennie was there with her new partner. Even if it was awkward. Because my daughter was playing, and she deserved to have both her parents present.


When Jennie needed me to take the kids on a weekend that wasn't mine because something came up, I'd say yes. Not begrudgingly. Not as a transaction. Just yes, because co-parenting means being flexible.


And Jennie noticed.


Started responding to my texts more. Started having actual conversations during exchanges instead of just transactional handoffs. Started trusting that I'd follow through, that I'd be reliable, that I wouldn't let the kids down.


It wasn't overnight. It wasn't linear. There were still hard moments, still days where old wounds resurfaced and we'd slip back into old patterns.


But overall, the trend was toward healing.


---


One day in 2016, Jennie texted me: "Can we meet for coffee? Need to talk about Landon."


My stomach dropped. "Need to talk" usually meant bad news. Usually meant conflict. Usually meant something I'd done wrong or something we were going to fight about.


But I texted back: "Sure. When?"


We met at a local coffee shop. Neutral territory. Public place. Safe for both of us.


Jennie looked nervous when I walked in. I probably did too.


We sat down. Ordered coffee. Made awkward small talk while we waited for our drinks.


Then she said: "Landon's really struggling at school."


And for the next hour, we talked. Actually talked. About our son. About his challenges. About what the teachers were saying, what the therapists recommended, what we each were seeing at home.


No accusations. No blame. No "this is your fault" or "you're not doing enough." Just two parents who loved their son and were trying to figure out how to help him.


At one point, Jennie said: "I know you're doing your best with him. I can see that. And I appreciate it."


I almost cried. Because I'd been waiting years to hear her acknowledge that I was trying. That I wasn't the same person who'd hurt her. That I'd changed.


"You're doing a great job with both of them," I said. "I know how hard it is. And I'm sorry I made it harder."


She nodded. Didn't say "it's okay"—because it wasn't okay. But she acknowledged the apology. Let it land instead of deflecting it.


We created a plan that day. For Landon. For how we'd support him. For how we'd communicate with each other and with his school. For how we'd be a team—not a married team, not a romantic team, but a parenting team.


And it worked.


---


That coffee shop meeting became a template.


When issues came up with the kids, we'd meet. Talk it through. Make decisions together. Put the kids first instead of our own hurt and anger and unresolved shit.


When Landon needed more therapy, we split the cost and coordinated schedules.


When Cameron wanted to try a new activity, we figured out logistics together.


When birthdays came up, we worked out how to make them special without making it a competition.


It wasn't always easy. We still had moments of tension. Still had old patterns that would surface. Still had to work through the complexity of co-parenting when you've hurt each other as much as we'd hurt each other.


But we kept showing up. Kept trying. Kept putting the kids first.


---


Shannon was instrumental in this shift.


She never tried to replace Jennie as the kids' mom. Never competed for their affection. Never spoke badly about Jennie or tried to turn the kids against her.


Instead, she'd say to me: "Jennie's their mom. She loves them. Your job is to support that relationship, not undermine it."


When I'd get frustrated about something Jennie did or said, Shannon would listen. Let me vent. Then gently redirect: "Okay. You feel frustrated. That's valid. Now what's the next right thing to do?"


She modeled healthy co-parenting for me. Showed me what it looked like to prioritize kids over ego. To be the bigger person when it would be easier to be petty. To extend grace when you'd rather hold a grudge.


And slowly, I learned.


Learned that co-parenting wasn't about winning. Wasn't about proving I was the better parent or making Jennie pay for what she'd done. It was about creating stability and love and safety for Landon and Cameron—even if that meant swallowing my pride, apologizing when I was wrong, giving Jennie credit when she did something right.


---


By 2018, Jennie and I could have full conversations. Could laugh about something one of the kids did. Could coordinate complex logistics without it turning into a fight.


We'd even started doing some things together—not as a couple, but as co-parents. School conferences. Doctor's appointments. Major events where both of us needed to be present.


It was weird at first. Sitting next to each other in a conference room, listening to teachers talk about our son, making decisions together. The ghosts of our marriage sitting there with us—all the things we'd done to each other, all the ways we'd failed.


But we pushed through the weirdness. Because Landon needed us both. Cameron needed us both. And they deserved parents who could be in the same room without it being a war zone.


---


One day, sitting in that same coffee shop where we'd first started actually co-parenting instead of just coordinating custody, Jennie said something that hit me hard.


"I've been talking to my therapist about us. About everything that happened."


"Okay," I said carefully.


"And she asked me: 'Can you see that he's changed? Can you see that he's doing the work?'"


I waited. Heart pounding.


"And I realized... yes. I can. You're not the same person who hurt me. You're sober. You're showing up for the kids. You're reliable. You're... you're being a good dad."


"Thank you," I managed. "That means a lot."


"I'm not saying I've forgiven everything. I don't know if I ever will completely. But I can see you're trying. And I respect that."


We sat there in silence for a moment. Years of pain between us. Years of growth too.


"I'm sorry," I said. "For everything. For all of it. I know I've said it before, but I need you to know I mean it. You deserved better than what I gave you."


"You did too," she said quietly. "I wasn't perfect either. I had my own shit. My own ways of hurting you. And I'm sorry too."


It wasn't a Hollywood moment. Wasn't some dramatic reconciliation where all the wounds healed and everything was forgiven. 


But it was real. It was honest. It was two people who'd destroyed each other learning how to be kind. Learning how to co-exist. Learning how to love their kids more than they hated each other.


And that was enough.


---


Today, in 2025, Jennie and I are friends. Not best friends. Not people who hang out socially. But friends in the way co-parents can be friends when they've both done the work.


We text about the kids. Share funny stories. Coordinate schedules. Support each other when parenting gets hard.


Last week, we met at that same coffee shop to discuss Landon's challenges. He's twenty now, struggling with the awareness of his disability, heartbroken over relationships he can't have, frustrated by limitations he can't control.


Jennie and I sat there creating a plan. How we'd support him. How we'd help him navigate these feelings. How we'd be a team.


"How did we get here?" Jennie asked at one point. "From the protective order to... this?"


"Recovery," I said. "And time. And you giving me chances I didn't deserve. And both of us choosing to prioritize the kids over our own hurt."


"It's kind of miraculous," she said.


"Yeah. It really is."


We finished our coffee. Hugged goodbye—a real hug, not an awkward side-hug. And went our separate ways to our separate lives.


Co-parenting. Not perfectly. But honestly. With love for our kids and grace for each other and gratitude for how far we'd come.


From courtrooms to coffee shops. From protective orders to partnerships. From enemies to... whatever this is.


It's not the ending I expected. But it's better than what I deserved.


And I'm grateful for it every single day.

Chapter 32: Cameron - The Miracle

Chapter 33: Landon's Journey


He's twenty now. He'll never drive a car, probably never have a girlfriend, might never work a traditional job. But he's taught me more about life than anyone else ever could.


---


Landon was born on May 27, 2005. My addiction was at its worst—Jax had died five years earlier, Gage two years before that. I was deep into cocaine and alcohol, bouncing at the Biltmore and living a life I barely recognized.


And into that chaos came my son.


I remember holding him in the hospital. This tiny perfect baby. All his fingers and toes. Healthy weight. Strong cry. The doctors said everything looked good. No apparent issues. Just a normal, healthy newborn.


We took him home and started the exhausting beautiful terrible wonderful experience of being first-time parents.


But from the beginning, something felt... different.


---


Landon didn't hit his milestones like other babies.


Most kids start walking around twelve months. Landon wasn't walking by eighteen months. By two years. By two and a half years.


Most kids start talking in simple words by their first birthday. Landon was three, almost four, and barely had any words at all.


He crawled in this weird frog-hop way—propelling himself forward using his arms more than his legs, not the typical hands-and-knees crawl that most babies do. The pediatrician said it might be a sign of something neurological but to wait and see.


We waited. And saw. And the differences kept piling up.


His body tone was off—sometimes too tense (hypertonic), sometimes too loose (hypotonic). He'd get excited about something and his whole body would clench up, muscles rigid, making this humming sound: "Mmmmm." Like his joy couldn't be contained, had to physically manifest in ways he couldn't control.


Jennie and I took him to specialist after specialist. Neurologists. Developmental pediatricians. Geneticists. Everyone running tests, doing evaluations, trying to figure out what was going on.


And I was getting drunk about it. Every appointment that revealed another delay. Every evaluation that showed more challenges. Every doctor's appointment where they'd use words like "developmental disability" and "lifelong challenges"—I'd leave and head straight to the bar.


---


The diagnosis finally came when Landon was three: Chromosome 4P deletion syndrome.


Extremely rare. Like, "better chance of winning the lottery three times over" rare. A genetic condition where part of the short arm of chromosome 4 is missing. Causes a constellation of issues: intellectual disability, mild cerebral palsy, autism, speech delays, motor delays, everything we'd been seeing.


The geneticist showed us the karyotype—the picture of Landon's chromosomes. And there it was. Chromosome 4. A piece missing. Something that should have been there just... wasn't.


"We don't really know much about this condition," the doctor said. "It's so rare we don't have a lot of data. Every child is different. All we can tell you is what we're seeing now and make educated guesses about the future."


"What's the future look like?" I asked.


"He'll likely always need support. May never live independently. May never drive. May struggle with speech and fine motor skills. But with early intervention—therapies, special education, the right support—he can make progress. He can have a good quality of life."


Quality of life. Such a clinical term for a parent's worst fear: that their child will struggle, will suffer, will face a world that isn't built for them.


---


I felt like God was punishing me.


That was my immediate thought. Not "how can I help my son?" Not "what resources do we need?" But: *This is my fault. This is karma. This is what I deserve for all the terrible things I've done.*


I'd cheated on Jennie. I'd lied constantly. I'd hurt people. I'd been selfish and destructive and cruel. And now my innocent son—who'd done nothing wrong, who was just born into this world like any other baby—was going to pay the price for my sins.


That belief almost destroyed me.


The guilt was crushing. The shame was enormous. Every time I looked at Landon and saw him struggle—struggle to walk, struggle to communicate, struggle to do things other kids did effortlessly—I thought: *I did this. Somehow, my drinking and drugging and terrible choices caused this.*


Which was bullshit, of course. Chromosome deletions happen randomly. Genetic lottery. Nothing I did or didn't do caused it. But addiction doesn't care about logic. Addiction takes every bad thing that happens and makes it about you, makes it your fault, gives you an excuse to use.


So I used. Drank about Landon's diagnosis. Drank about my guilt. Drank about everything.


And I wouldn't stop for another eight years.


---


As Landon grew, his personality emerged despite his challenges.


He had this incredible memory—almost savant-like. Could watch a bodybuilding video once and memorize the entire routine. Knew which competitor would come out next, could anticipate their poses, would hum along with the music like he'd watched it a thousand times.


He loved elevators. Obsessed with them. I think because they were something he could control—push a button, door closes, you go up or down, door opens. Simple cause and effect in a world that often felt chaotic and unpredictable for him. A transport system he could operate independently.


He loved video games. Would play for hours, his hand-eye coordination surprisingly good despite his other motor challenges. I think games made him feel capable. Like he was running and jumping and doing parkour, things his body couldn't do in real life but his mind could control on screen.


He loved WWE wrestling. The drama, the spectacle, the clear good guys and bad guys. Would watch matches and explain to me who was heel and who was face, who was going to win based on the storyline, analysis that showed his comprehension was way beyond what his speech abilities suggested.


---


Speech has always been Landon's weakest area.


His receptive language—what he understands—is actually pretty close to normal. He gets jokes. Understands sarcasm. Picks up on context and nuance in ways that typical autism wouldn't allow. He's not literal like many autistic kids. He understands the unspoken stuff, the between-the-lines communication.


But his expressive language—what he can say—is severely limited. The muscles in his mouth and throat don't work right. The connection between brain and speech is compromised. So he can think complex thoughts but can't always get them out in ways others understand.


That's heartbreaking. Knowing my son has so much going on inside his head and can't always communicate it. Can't always make himself understood. Has to live with the frustration of being trapped in a body that won't cooperate.


---


Landon is twenty now. In a post-high program for special needs kids that goes until age twenty-two. Learning "functional skills"—how to stock shelves, how to work in a T-shirt factory, how to follow directions in a work environment.


Will he ever have a traditional job? Probably not. Will he ever live independently? Probably not. Will he ever drive a car or have a girlfriend or experience the things most twenty-year-olds take for granted? Probably not.


And the absolutely crushing thing? He knows it.


Landon understands his disability. Understands that he's different from other kids. Understands what "normal" twenty-year-olds are doing and knows he can't do those things.


There was this girl from his school. Sweet young lady he'd known since first grade. His "girlfriend" in the innocent elementary school way—they'd hold hands, sit together, clearly cared about each other.


She graduated. Got into a relationship with someone else. Got pregnant. Posted prom pictures on social media.


Landon saw them. And broke down. Sobbing. Heartbroken. Because he understood: she's moving on with her life. She's experiencing things he never will. She's having relationships and going to prom and having a baby and he's... he's in a functional skills program learning how to fold T-shirts.


Sometimes I wish he didn't have that awareness. Wish he was blissfully unaware of what he's missing. Because the awareness—the understanding of his reality combined with the inability to fully participate in it—that's a deeper cut. A deeper pain.


---


Landon loves me. I know he does. When I pick him up for our weekends together, his whole face lights up. He gives me these amazing hugs, his whole body expressing what his words can't quite say.


We have our routines. We go to his favorite restaurants. Watch his favorite shows. Play his video games together. He shows me his latest WWE wrestling obsessions, explains the storylines with more enthusiasm than clarity.


And I love him with an intensity that sometimes scares me. Love him not despite his challenges but including them. Love him for exactly who he is, not who I wish he could be.


Landon taught me patience. Taught me that milestones aren't the measure of worth. Taught me that different doesn't mean less. Taught me to celebrate progress—any progress, no matter how small—instead of mourning what isn't there.


Taught me humility. Because I used to think I had my shit together. Used to think I was special because I could build muscle and win trophies and perform success. And then I had a son who couldn't do any of those things but was more pure-hearted and loving than I'd ever been.


Taught me gratitude. Because when your kid finally learns to tie his shoes at age ten, or says a full clear sentence at age fifteen, or does something independently that took years of therapy to achieve—you realize how much you take for granted. How much you don't appreciate until it's hard-won.


---


There's hope now that didn't exist when Landon was born. Technology. AI. Neural interfaces.


Elon Musk's Neuralink. Brain-computer interfaces that might someday let people like Landon communicate directly from their minds to devices. Let them express thoughts without needing their mouths to cooperate.


AI language models that could interpret Landon's limited speech, context clues, body language, and translate it into clear communication.


Assistive technology that's advancing faster than anyone predicted. Ways to give people with disabilities access to experiences and communication and independence that weren't possible even five years ago.


I think about that a lot. About how if we can just keep Landon healthy and safe and supported for the next ten, twenty years, there might be technologies that change his life completely. That let him communicate fully. That give him independence. That let him participate in the world in ways we can't imagine yet.


That hope keeps me going on the hard days.


---


Landon will never be "normal." I've accepted that. He'll always need support, always face challenges, always live in a world that wasn't designed for people like him.


But he's perfect. Exactly as he is. My son. My teacher. My reminder that worth isn't measured by ability or achievement or hitting milestones on some arbitrary timeline.


He's taught me that the crooked lines—the broken places, the different paths, the things that look like tragedy from the outside—those are where God does His best work.


Landon is one of my greatest treasures. Not despite his disability. Not in some condescending "he's so inspiring" way that treats disabled people like motivation porn for able-bodied folks.


But because he's Landon. Because he's mine. Because he's taught me more about unconditional love and patience and gratitude and humility than any amount of success or achievement ever could.


He's twenty years old. Working in a post-high program. Living with his mom and stepdad. Spending weekends with Shannon and me. Playing video games and watching wrestling and loving elevators and being exactly who he is.


And I'm so damn proud of him.


Not for overcoming. Not for being "brave" or "inspirational." Just for being. For existing. For being my son.


That's enough. He's enough. He's always been enough.


And learning that? Learning to love him exactly as he is instead of mourning who he isn't?


That's been one of the greatest gifts of my recovery.

Chapter 34: Brody & The Blended Family

With Brody, I tried to lead with curiosity instead of fear. "What were you thinking when you did that? What were you hoping would happen? What do you think you learned from this?"


Not interrogation. Just genuine curiosity about his internal experience. Helping him develop his own internal compass instead of just following external rules.

Chapter 36: The Protocol—Lessons from the Crooked Road


Every morning, I wake up and do the same thing. Gratitude first. Always gratitude first.


---


People ask me all the time: "How do you stay sober? How do you maintain recovery? What's the secret?"


There is no secret. There's just a protocol. Daily practices. Consistent actions. Showing up even when I don't feel like it.


Recovery isn't about one big moment of transformation—though those help. It's about a thousand small decisions, compounded over time, that create a life worth living.


Here's what that looks like for me.


---


**Morning Protocol:**


I wake up and the first thing I do—before checking my phone, before getting out of bed, before doing anything else—is express gratitude.


Out loud. "Thank you."


Sometimes I know who I'm thanking. God, the universe, my higher power, whatever you want to call it. Sometimes I don't. But I say it anyway.


Then a quick prayer: "Thank you. Please help me stay sober today. Please help me see the ideal path forward. Please help me be of service."


My sponsor's version is slightly different: "How can I be of service to your children today?" Meaning: how can I show up for other people? How can I be useful? How can I get out of my own head and focus on contribution?


Then I hydrate. Water first. Coffee second. Get the body functioning before the mind starts racing.


Supplements. Whatever my body needs that day. I'm forty-four now. Recovery isn't just about not drinking—it's about taking care of this physical vessel that got beat to shit for fifteen years.


Sunshine if possible. Even just standing outside for five minutes. Light therapy. Grounding. Reminding myself I'm alive, I'm present, I'm here.


Light exercise. Stretching. Meditation. Nothing extreme. Just movement. Getting blood flowing. Connecting mind and body.


That's the morning. Every morning. Non-negotiable.


---


**At Work - Written Practice:**


When I get to work, I write down three daily gratitudes.


Has to be written. Can't just think them. Writing makes them concrete. Makes them real. Prevents my brain from skipping over them.


Today it might be: "Wife, kids, parents. Health. Business. Vacation this weekend."


Simple. Specific. Things I can actually feel grateful for instead of abstract concepts.


Then affirmations. Acting my way into right thinking.


"I am patient. I am kind. I am enough."


"I love and accept myself today for being human the way I know God does."


"I will be kind and gentle with myself and others."


Not because I feel these things. But because saying them creates the possibility of feeling them. Words shape thoughts. Thoughts shape actions. Actions shape reality.


I used to think affirmations were bullshit. New age nonsense. Positive thinking garbage that didn't actually change anything.


But then I realized: I'd been doing negative affirmations my whole life. "I'm a piece of shit. I'm worthless. I'm going to fuck this up." And those negative affirmations shaped my reality just fine.


So why not try positive ones? Worst case, nothing changes. Best case, I start believing them.


---


**Lessons Learned:**


Recovery has taught me things I wish I'd known thirty years ago. But I wasn't ready to learn them thirty years ago. So maybe the timing is perfect.


**It's okay to make mistakes.** That's where the treasure is. Every failure is data. Every mistake is information. The only real failure is not learning from it.


**It's okay to ask for help.** In fact, it's essential. I spent so many years trying to do everything on my own, thinking asking for help was weakness. But asking for help is strength. It's vulnerability. It's humility. It's the only way to actually grow.


**Failure is data, not defeat.** When something doesn't work, that's not a judgment on your worth. It's just information. Adjust. Adapt. Try again differently.


**Time alone doesn't heal.** You have to take action during that time. You can wait five years for things to get better, but if you're not doing anything different, five years from now will look exactly like today.


**Shame is a gauge, not a sentence.** When you feel shame, that's your internal compass saying you're off course. It's not saying you're a bad person. It's saying: correct back to the middle. Get back in alignment with your values.


**The pendulum swings.** From "I'm the worst piece of shit" to "I'm the best, I'm special, I'm different." Both extremes are ego. The goal is the middle: friend amongst friends, worker amongst workers. Not better than, not worse than. Equal.


**Progress, not perfection.** I'm not trying to be perfect. Perfect is impossible and exhausting. I'm trying to be consistent. To show up. To do the next right thing. That's enough.


---


**The Three Indispensables:**


The Big Book of AA talks about three things required for recovery: honesty, open-mindedness, and willingness.


I had honesty in December 2009. Knew I was an alcoholic. Couldn't deny it after nearly dying from DTs.


But I didn't have open-mindedness. Didn't believe AA could help. Didn't think I needed a higher power or a sponsor or any of that spiritual stuff.


And I definitely didn't have willingness. Wasn't willing to go to any length. Wasn't willing to surrender control. Wasn't willing to do whatever it takes.


Without all three, I was just a sober miserable drunk. And I stayed that way until January 2013, when I finally got all three ingredients.


**Honesty:** Admitting not just that I'm an alcoholic, but admitting all the ways I'd hurt people. All the lies I'd told. All the damage I'd caused. Complete transparency, no matter how painful.


**Open-mindedness:** Being willing to believe that maybe—just maybe—other people knew something I didn't. That their way might work even if I didn't understand it. That I could trust the process even when it felt weird or uncomfortable.


**Willingness:** Going to any length. Meetings every day. Calling sponsor when I wanted to drink. Working steps even when they were painful. Being of service even when I didn't feel like it. Doing whatever it takes to stay sober.


All three. Not one or two. All three. That's the requirement.


---


**Daily Maintenance:**


Recovery isn't something I did. It's something I do. Present tense. Every single day.


I still go to meetings. Not as many as I did early on—I'm not doing ninety meetings in ninety days anymore. But weekly at minimum. More when I need it.


I still call my sponsor. Check in. Process things. Get perspective when I'm too deep in my own head to see clearly.


I still work with sponsees. Guys I'm helping through the steps. Because service is how I stay sober. Helping others keeps me accountable.


I still read recovery literature. Not every day, but regularly. Big Book. Twelve and Twelve. Daily reflections. Whatever keeps me connected to the principles.


I still practice gratitude. Write it down. Say it out loud. Notice the good things instead of just focusing on problems.


I still take care of my body. Work out. Eat reasonably well. Sleep. Because physical health supports mental health supports spiritual health. It's all connected.


I still show up for my family. For Shannon. For the kids. For my parents. Because relationships are what make life worth living, and relationships require presence and effort and consistency.


None of this is glamorous. None of it is exciting. It's just... maintenance. The daily practices that keep me sober, stable, sane.


Like brushing your teeth. You don't do it once and you're done forever. You do it every day because that's what maintains dental health. Same with recovery. You do the practices every day because that's what maintains sobriety.


---


**The Treasures:**


My greatest asset, I've learned, is my experience with doing dumb shit that I can now share.


I used to think there was only shame in these things. Only regret. Only "why did I waste so many years?"


But now I see it differently. All of that—the addiction, the affairs, the protective order, the rock bottoms, the near-death experiences—it's a goldmine of data. Information. Lived experience that I can share to help someone else.


In meetings, when I share my story, people come up afterward: "Thank you. I needed to hear that." Or "That's exactly what I'm going through." Or "You just described my life."


And that's when I know: nothing was wasted. Every mistake has purpose. Every failure has value. If my experience can help one person avoid the same pitfall, or give them hope that recovery is possible, or show them they're not alone—then it was worth it.


You never know when a seed you sow might plant. Someone hears your story. Doesn't do anything with it immediately. But then three years later, they're facing a similar situation and they remember what you said. And it changes their decision. Saves their marriage. Saves their sobriety. Saves their life.


Sometimes people come back years later: "You said something in that meeting five years ago. I wasn't ready to hear it then. But it stuck with me. And when I was ready, I remembered. And it saved my life."


That's the goal. Show up. Do the work. Help someone else.


My experience—all of it, even the worst parts—is now my greatest asset. Not because I'm proud of what I did. But because I'm willing to share it honestly so others don't have to suffer the same consequences.


---


**The Practice:**


This is the protocol. This is the practice.


Not perfect, but consistent. Not arrival, but progress. One day at a time, one decision at a time, one crooked line that somehow leads straight home.


Wake up. Gratitude. Prayer. Hydrate. Move. Connect.


Write gratitudes. Write affirmations. Do the work—whatever work that day requires.


Call sponsor when struggling. Go to meeting when needed. Help others when possible.


Come home. Be present with family. Eat dinner together. Put kids to bed with prayers and conversations instead of passing out drunk.


End the day with gratitude. Review what went well. Acknowledge what didn't. Plan for tomorrow.


Sleep. Wake up. Do it again.


That's it. That's the whole thing. No secrets. No shortcuts. Just consistent daily practices that compound over time into a life worth living.


Some days it feels easy. Some days it feels impossible. Most days it's somewhere in between.


But I show up anyway. Because showing up is the practice. And the practice is what keeps me alive.


---


Thirteen years sober now. January 1, 2013 to October 2025. Over 4,700 days. Each one a choice. Each one a small victory. Each one proof that recovery is possible.


I'm not special. I'm not stronger than other addicts. I'm not more disciplined or more intelligent or more deserving.


I just kept showing up. Kept doing the work. Kept being honest. Kept asking for help. Kept being willing to change.


And somehow—by grace, by community, by commitment to the process—it worked.


This is the protocol. These are the lessons. This is what the crooked road taught me.


And if it can work for me—someone who lost everything, destroyed everyone, hit bottoms I didn't think existed—it can work for anyone.


You just have to be willing. Willing to be honest. Willing to ask for help. Willing to keep showing up even when you don't see results yet.


The results will come. Not overnight. Not in dramatic lightning-bolt moments. But slowly. Incrementally. One day at a time.


Until one day you wake up and realize: I have a life. I have a family. I have purpose. I have peace.


And it was worth every uncomfortable, painful, humbling step it took to get here.


That's the protocol. That's the practice. That's the path.


And it's available to anyone willing to walk it.

Chapter 37: God Writes Straight


The oxygen tent kid. The swim coach's secret. The bodybuilder. The addict. The father. The husband. The recovered alcoholic. All of it—every crooked line—led exactly where it needed to go.


---


**Full Circle Moments:**


Looking back now, I can see the pattern. The design. The way every broken piece somehow fit together into something whole.


The oxygen tent at age six—feeling separate, isolated, different from other kids. Learning to be two people: the sick vulnerable kid and the tough protected kid. That duality would define my life for the next thirty years. But it also prepared me for recovery, where I'd finally learn to integrate those two selves instead of keeping them at war.


Now I help others breathe. Literally and metaphorically. Helping people in recovery learn to take full breaths again. Learn to exist without constant anxiety and fear. Learn to be present instead of constantly fleeing.


The swim coach's violation—the secret I kept for thirty years, the shame that lived in my bones, the confusion about physiological response versus consent. It nearly destroyed me. But cracking that open at The Ranch, processing it through EMDR, finding words for it—that broke something fundamental. Shattered the protective shell I'd built around my trauma. Taught me that secrets keep you sick, and truth sets you free.


Now I help others break their silence. Encourage people to share what they've been hiding. Show them that vulnerability isn't weakness—it's courage. That the things we're most ashamed of are often the keys to our healing.


Bodybuilding obsession—the way I used my body to feel powerful when I felt powerless inside. The discipline, the control, the performance of strength. It was addiction in a socially acceptable form. Training my brain to seek external validation, to measure worth by appearance and achievement, to never be satisfied.


But understanding that helped me understand addiction mechanics. The way our brains get hijacked by compulsion. The way we chase feelings instead of addressing underlying needs. The way discipline without emotional health is just another prison.


Jax's death in 2000—the brother who introduced me to the party life, who showed me what it meant to live large and die young. His death should have been my wake-up call. Instead, I never had another day sober until I finally got sober thirteen years later.


But Jax taught me something important: you can't save people. You can love them. You can be there for them. But ultimately, everyone has to choose their own path. And some people don't make it. That knowledge makes me gentler with myself, more patient with others, more grateful for every day I get.


Gage's death in 2003—my brother, my training partner, the guy I went through things with that weren't average everyday dude stuff. Visiting his grave every single day for months, never once sober. The image of him in that hospital bed, paralyzed, fever burning his brain. That haunted me for years.


But Gage showed me consequences. Showed me that invincibility is an illusion. That one moment can change everything. That the people you love can be here today and gone tomorrow. That lesson—as painful as it was—probably saved my life. Made me just scared enough to eventually reach for help.


Landon's challenges—the disability diagnosis that I initially experienced as punishment. The genetic lottery that gave my son struggles he'll face his entire life. The guilt I carried thinking somehow I'd caused it. The grief of watching him struggle while other kids excelled effortlessly.


But Landon taught me gratitude. Taught me humility. Taught me that worth isn't measured by ability or achievement. Taught me patience I never would have learned otherwise. Taught me what unconditional love actually means—loving someone not despite their challenges, but including them. Seeing the whole person, not just what they can or can't do.


Cameron's strength—my daughter who somehow emerged from the chaos perfectly intact. Who developed emotional intelligence I didn't have at twice her age. Who set boundaries and made healthy choices and became someone I look at in confusion thinking: *How did she turn out so perfectly?*


Cameron gives me hope for the future. Proof that kids are resilient. That generational patterns can be broken. That doing the work matters, even when you think you've already done too much damage. That it's never too late to become the parent your kids deserve.


The protective order—losing my home, my kids, my identity. The year of absolute hell. Living at my parents' house like a teenager who'd fucked up beyond repair. Communication through my mother. Having to leave when my kids visited. The court system that felt rigged against me.


But the protective order taught me to surrender control. Taught me I couldn't manipulate or performance my way out of consequences. Taught me that sometimes the universe has to take everything away before you're willing to actually change. Taught me humility—real humility, not the performance of humility.


The Ranch—going for Jennie, leaving for me. The trauma work that cracked me open. The EMDR that turned my slideshow of compartmentalized memories into a movie reel I had to actually process. The group therapy. The psychodrama. The serpent vision. The spiritual awakening I didn't ask for but desperately needed.


The Ranch taught me that healing is possible. That you can face your worst memories and survive. That community and vulnerability and honesty are more powerful than any substance. That transformation isn't just a concept—it's a lived experience available to anyone willing to do the work.


The serpent vision—that out-of-body experience after EMDR. The glowing black and golden snake coiling around the room, going up my spine, merging with my head. The symbolism of shedding old skin. Renewal. Transformation. The message from something bigger than me saying: *I'm remaking you.*


I didn't need to understand it completely. But it gave me faith. Faith that something larger was at work. That my life had purpose. That the crooked lines were somehow part of a design I couldn't see yet but could trust anyway.


Shannon—meeting her in that Thursday night meeting. Love built on truth instead of performance. A relationship where I couldn't hide, couldn't pretend, couldn't perform being someone I wasn't. Where honesty was the foundation instead of the thing that destroys it all.


Shannon taught me that I don't have to be perfect to be loved. That showing up is enough. That vulnerability creates connection instead of destroying it. That partnership is possible when both people are committed to honesty and growth and showing up for each other even when it's hard.


---


**The Paradoxes:**


My life has been full of paradoxes. Things that seemed contradictory but were actually two sides of the same coin.


Strongest body, weakest mind. I could lift hundreds of pounds but couldn't lift myself out of depression. Could discipline my diet and training but couldn't stop drinking. Had external strength that masked internal chaos.


Most disciplined externally, most chaotic internally. Perfect meal prep, perfect training splits, perfect performance. Meanwhile, inside: addiction, rage, suicidal ideation, complete emotional dysregulation. The armor was perfect. The person inside was dying.


Performing for others, dying inside. Everyone thought I had it together. Thought I was successful, strong, disciplined. And I was dying. Literally killing myself with substances. Destroying relationships. Losing everything that mattered while maintaining the appearance of having it all.


Married the love of my life, destroyed it. Jennie was everything I thought I wanted. And I hurt her in ways that still make me sick to think about. Cheated. Lied. Raged. Made her life hell. Lost her through my own actions. Had to watch that marriage end knowing I caused it.


Perfect son, felt punished. I did everything my parents asked. Got good grades. Competed. Achieved. Never openly rebelled. And inside, I felt like I was being punished for being good. Like perfection was a cage. Like I couldn't breathe under the weight of being what everyone needed me to be.


Lost everything, found myself. Rock bottom. Protective order. Living with my parents. No home. No family. No identity. Nothing left of the life I'd built. And that's when I finally found the real me. Not the performance. Not the mask. The actual person underneath. Broken but honest. Struggling but real.


Went to Ranch for Jennie, left for me. Went to save my marriage. To prove I'd changed. To check the box so she'd take me back. Left knowing the marriage was over but I was just beginning. Left with tools and awareness and commitment to recovery that had nothing to do with her and everything to do with finally choosing myself.


Hated being alcoholic, became grateful for being one. Loathed the label. Hated admitting it. Fought it for years. And now? Grateful. Because being alcoholic led me to AA. Led me to recovery. Led me to a community and a way of life and a spiritual practice that saved me. Without addiction, I never would have found this path.


---


**What Recovery Gave:**


Recovery didn't just give me sobriety. It gave me a life.


I used to put kids to sleep—meaning I'd pass out drunk while they were still awake. Not there for bedtime. Not present for prayers or conversations or tucking them in. Just... gone. Unconscious.


Now I put my kids to sleep—actual present parenting. Prayers together. Conversations about their day. Listening to their fears and hopes and dreams. Being there. Being present. Being a dad instead of just a guy who shares DNA with them.


I used to cheat on every woman. Multiple affairs. Constant dishonesty. Using women for validation, for escape, for whatever need I was trying to fill. Destroying trust. Destroying relationships. Destroying myself and everyone who loved me.


Now I'm a loyal, faithful husband. And honestly? I deserve a parade for being a decent human. Because it's not natural for me. Not easy. Requires conscious choice every day. Requires honesty and communication and working through shit instead of running from it. But I do it. Because I promised Shannon. Because I promised myself. Because being honest is less painful than living a lie.


I used to protect the predator. Kept the swim coach's secret for thirty years. Protected him by staying silent. Made sure he wouldn't face consequences for what he did to me. Carried his shame like it was mine.


Now I protect the vulnerable. I speak up. I tell my story. I encourage others to break their silence. I believe survivors. I create space for people to share what happened to them without judgment or minimization. I use my voice to make sure no one else has to carry secrets that keep them sick.


I used to hide pain in my skull—that BB bullet lodged in there, symbol of all the trauma I'd buried. Secret pain. Compartmentalized memories. Everything locked away where it couldn't hurt me but also couldn't heal.


Now I share pain to help others heal. In meetings. In conversations. In this memoir. I expose the wounds. Make them visible. Show others that you can survive trauma, process it, integrate it, and come out the other side intact. That vulnerability isn't weakness—it's the path to healing.


---


**What's Possible:**


At thirteen: skinny kid cut from basketball. Embarrassed. Ashamed. Convinced I wasn't good enough. That I'd never be good enough. That my body was something to be ashamed of instead of something to develop.


At seventeen: state champion bodybuilder. Proof that transformation is possible. That discipline and consistency and showing up every day can fundamentally change who you are. That you're not stuck being the person circumstances made you.


At twenty: married, thought I had life figured out. Thought I'd arrived. Thought I knew what success looked like. Thought the external markers—wife, house, career—meant I was doing it right. Had no idea I was building on a foundation of lies.


At thirty-two: lost everything. Protective order. Psych wards. The absolute bottom. Everything I'd built destroyed. Every relationship damaged. Every bit of progress erased. Thought my life was over. Thought I'd never recover from this.


At forty-four: sober, remarried, blended family, thriving business. Life beyond my wildest dreams. Not perfect—still challenges, still struggles, still days where I have to fight for my peace. But alive. Present. Grateful. Contributing. Being of service. Actually showing up for my life instead of running from it.


Every chapter felt like the end. The oxygen tent. The violation. Jax's death. Gage's death. Landon's diagnosis. The protective order. The bilateral pneumonia. The chaplain question.


Every ending was just a bend in the road. Just another crooked line that was somehow leading somewhere I couldn't see yet but needed to go.


---


**The Message:**


No one is too far gone. I've been as far gone as you can get without dying. Multiple rock bottoms. Multiple near-death experiences. Years of active addiction. Hurting everyone who loved me. Destroying everything I touched.


And I recovered. Not because I'm special or strong or deserving. But because I was willing. Willing to be honest. Willing to ask for help. Willing to keep showing up even when I didn't see progress yet.


Nothing is wasted. Every mistake. Every failure. Every painful experience. All of it has purpose. All of it has value. Not because the pain was good—pain is never good. But because we can learn from it. Can use it to help others. Can transform suffering into service.


Every crooked line has purpose. Even the ones that look like pure destruction. Even the ones that feel like they're leading nowhere. Even the ones you wish you could erase from your history. They're all part of the design. All part of what makes you who you are. All part of what qualifies you to help others.


One decision away from different life. That's always true. One decision to pick up the phone instead of the bottle. One decision to go to a meeting instead of a bar. One decision to tell the truth instead of another lie. One decision to ask for help instead of white-knuckling it alone.


Your life can change with one decision. Mine did. Multiple times.


Don't need to be filthy rich to be rich. I'm not wealthy by conventional standards. Don't have millions in the bank. Don't have a mansion. Don't have the external markers society says equal success.


But I'm rich in the ways that actually matter. Rich in relationships. Rich in purpose. Rich in peace. Rich in the ability to be present for my life instead of constantly trying to escape it.


Full whole self is the wealth. Bringing all of me to the table. Not performing. Not pretending. Not splitting myself into compartments. Just showing up as I am—flawed, imperfect, still learning, still growing. That integration is the real wealth.


Closest to that I've ever been. I'm not there yet. Probably never will be completely. But I'm closer than I've ever been to being fully myself. To living in alignment with my values. To showing up honestly. To being the same person in every room instead of changing masks depending on the audience.


Of use and value to society, and hopefully to you. That's the goal. Not to be perfect. Not to have all the answers. But to be useful. To share what I've learned. To help someone else find their path. To be of service.


If my story helps one person—one person—decide to get sober, or stay sober, or ask for help, or break their silence, or keep going when they want to give up—then everything I went through was worth it.


Every crooked line. Every rock bottom. Every painful lesson. All worth it if it helps you.


---


God writes straight with crooked lines. I'm living proof.


And if He can do it with my mess, He can do it with yours.

Your story isn't over. Your past doesn't define your future. Your mistakes don't disqualify you from redemption.

You just have to be willing. Willing to be honest. Willing to ask for help. Willing to keep being present even when you can't see the path yet.

One day at a time. One decision at a time. One crooked line at a time.

Until one day you look back and realize: all of it led exactly where it needed to go.

Straight home.

The sun is warm on my face. October in Virginia—perfect fall weather. The kind of day where the air is crisp but the sun is still strong enough to make you feel alive.


I'm parked outside the high school waiting for the kids. Shannon texted me twenty minutes ago: "On my way with Brody. See you there."


We're doing family photos today. All five of us. At the foothills of Monticello—Thomas Jefferson's estate, one of the most beautiful spots in Charlottesville. The leaves are changing. Everything is golden and red and orange. Perfect timing.


I'm picking up Landon and Cameron early from school. They know about it, approved it, are actually excited. That alone is a miracle—teenagers being excited to take family photos instead of treating it like torture.

Normal kid stuff. Normal family stuff.

But we're here. Together. Present. Showing up.


---


This weekend, Shannon and I are going out of the country. Just the two of us. A vacation. Something we couldn't have done a few years ago when money was tight and life was chaotic and we were still figuring out how to blend a family.


But now? Now we can. Can take time for ourselves. Can invest in our relationship. Can step away knowing the kids are safe and cared for and supported by their other parents.


Jennie has Landon and Cameron. Brody's dad has him. Everyone coordinated. Everyone on the same page. Co-parenting working the way it's supposed to work.


I can't even believe how good my life is. Can't believe this is the same life that thirteen years ago was characterized by protective orders and psych wards and absolute desperation.


---


I still work. Still run the family business during the day. Still build my business in the evenings and weekends. Still hustle. Still create. Still show up for what I'm building.


But I can also relax. Can take vacations. Can work out without it being obsessive. Can enjoy my life instead of constantly running from it.


Still going to meetings. Not as frequently as early recovery, but regularly. Still sponsoring guys. Still being of service. Still working my program because the program is what keeps me alive.


Still writing gratitudes and affirmations every morning. Still doing my protocol. Still showing up for the daily practices that create the life I'm living.


Still imperfect. Still learning. Still making mistakes. Still having days where I struggle.


But also: still sober. Still growing. Still choosing recovery every single day.


---


Earlier this week, Jennie texted me: "Can we talk about Landon? I have some ideas."
We met at a different coffee shop this time—a new place near the school. Not our usual spot. It felt right, somehow. New location for a new chapter.
She'd been researching programs. Vocational training. Social skills groups. Things that might help Landon build the independent life he wants so badly but can't quite reach on his own.
We sat there for an hour, laptops open, comparing options, making lists, dividing up phone calls to make. Two parents. One team. Working together for their son.
At one point she looked up and said, "You know what's wild? We're better co-parents than we ever were married."
I laughed. "That's because we're both actually healthy now."
"True." She paused. "I'm grateful, Shaun. For where we are. For who we've become."
"Me too, Jennie. Me too."
We packed up our laptops. Hugged goodbye—a real hug, the kind that says *we made it through the impossible and we're okay.* And went our separate ways to our separate lives.
---


I'm so full of love today. That's what I keep thinking as I drive home from dinner with my family. As Shannon sits beside me talking about the vacation. As I think about Landon and Cameron and Brody and the life we've built together.


So full of love. So full of gratitude. So full of this feeling that I can't quite name but that feels like peace.


Life beyond my wildest dreams. That's the AA promise. That if you work the program, if you stay sober, if you do the work—your life will exceed your wildest dreams.


I used to think that meant money. Success. External markers of achievement.


But it doesn't. It means this. This moment. This life. This family. This peace. This ability to be present instead of constantly running.


Not filthy rich monetarily. We're comfortable, stable, able to take vacations and handle emergencies without panic. But not wealthy by conventional standards.


But in many ways I am rich. Rich in relationships. Rich in purpose. Rich in peace. Rich in the ability to show up for my life. Rich in the knowledge that I'm doing something meaningful. Rich in community and connection and contribution.


Never thought I would feel that way. Never thought I'd get here. Never thought recovery was possible for someone like me—someone who'd done the things I'd done, hurt the people I'd hurt, fallen as far as I'd fallen.


But here I am. Thirteen years sober. Remarried. Blended family. Thriving business. Helping others. Being of service. Actually living instead of just surviving.


Closest to bringing my full whole self to the table I've ever been. Not there yet. Probably never will be completely. But closer. Every day, a little closer.


Of use and value to society. That's what I wanted. Not to be special or famous or impressive. Just to be useful. To provide value. To help someone else.


And I am. Through meetings. Through sponsoring. Through my courses and coaching. Through this memoir. Through showing up for my family. Through being honest about my story so others don't have to suffer the same way.


---


I don't know what tomorrow brings. Can't predict the future. Can't guarantee I'll stay sober forever or that my marriage will last or that my business will succeed or that my kids will be okay.


But I know what today brings: gratitude, purpose, love, and one more chance to get it right.


And today? That's enough.


Tomorrow I'll wake up. Do my protocol. Gratitude first. Prayer. Hydrate. Move. Show up.


Work. Meetings. Family. Service. The daily practices that create a life worth living.


And the day after that, same thing. And the day after that.


Not because I'm weak or fragile or can't handle thinking long-term. But because presence is the practice. Being here now. Not in the past regretting what was. Not in the future worrying about what might be. Just here. Now. In this moment.


---


The sun is setting as we drive home. Shannon reaches over and takes my hand. Doesn't say anything. Just holds it.


I think about the oxygen tent kid. The six-year-old boy lying in a hospital bed learning to be two different people. The Cookie Monster slippers and the G.I. Joe slippers. The choice between sweet and tough. Between vulnerable and protected.


I think about everything that came after. The violation. The bodybuilding. The addiction. The deaths. The marriages. The kids. The rock bottoms. The recovery. All of it.


Every crooked line leading here. To this moment. To this car. To this hand holding mine. To this family. To this life.


God writes straight with crooked lines.


I'm living proof.


And if it's possible for me—the oxygen tent kid, the violated child, the addict who lost everything, the father who destroyed his family, the man who nearly died multiple times—it's possible for anyone.


Your story isn't over. Your past doesn't define your future. Your worst moments don't disqualify you from your best ones.


You just have to be willing. Willing to be honest. Willing to ask for help. Willing to keep showing up even when you don't want to.




One decision at a time.


One crooked line at a time.


Until somehow—impossibly, miraculously, by grace—those crooked lines straighten out.


And you find yourself home.


---


We pull into the driveway. Brody jumps out first, already on his phone making plans with friends. Cameron follows, hugs me goodnight, heads inside. Landon lingers, gives me one of his amazing hugs, says "Love you, Dad" in that way he says things—not quite clear, but clear enough.


"Love you too, buddy."


Shannon and I sit in the car for a moment after the kids go inside.


"Good day," she says.


"Great day."


"I'm proud of you. Of us. Of what we've built."


"Me too."


We go inside. End the day the way we start it: with gratitude.


Thank you for today. Thank you for my sobriety. Thank you for my family. Thank you for one more chance to get it right.


Tomorrow we'll do it again.


And the day after that.


For as long as we're given.




---

Shaun Critzer is a recovered alcoholic, trauma survivor, entrepreneur, and father living in Charlottesville, Virginia.
